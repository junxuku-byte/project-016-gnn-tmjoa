Abstract
Background  Temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJOA) is a prevalent inflammatory disease with unclear 
pathogenesis. Serum and glucocorticoid-inducible kinase 1 (SGK1) has been implicated in osteoarthritis-related 
cartilage degeneration. This study investigated the role of SGK1 in a mouse model of TMJOA and explored its 
mechanism, focusing on SGK1’s regulation of Forkhead box O1 (FoxO1) and its subsequent effects on autophagy and 
extracellular matrix degradation in mouse condylar chondrocytes (MCCs).
Methods  A TMJOA model was established using unilateral anterior crossbite (UAC), and an SGK1 knockdown model 
was generated via intra-articular injection of AAV virus.
Results  SGK1 expression was significantly upregulated in condylar cartilage of UAC-induced TMJOA mice. SGK1 
knockdown alleviated cartilage matrix degradation and increased the expression of anabolic marker. In MCCs, SGK1 
directly bound to FoxO1, promoting its phosphorylation and nuclear export, thereby inhibiting autophagy. Inhibiting 
SGK1 enhanced autophagy, as evidenced by increased Beclin-1, elevated LC3-II/LC3-I ratio, and decreased P62, which 
consequently attenuated IL-1β-induced extracellular matrix catabolism. These effects were reversed by autophagy 
inhibitor chloroquine and FoxO1 inhibitor AS1842856.
Conclusions  This study is the first to propose the regulatory role of SGK1 in TMJOA, providing new insights for future 
therapeutic research.

Background
Temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJOA) is 
characterized by the gradual deterioration of cartilage, 
remodeling of the underlying bone, inflammation in the 
synovial membrane, and persistent pain [1]. The preva­
lence of this condition varies from 8% to 60%, with a 
higher occurrence among women [2, 3]. Currently, symp­
tomatic treatment can control pain and inflammation, 
but effective strategies for alleviating TMJOA progression 
are lacking. Unlike the hyaline cartilage found in knees 
and hips, the TMJ is composed of fibrocartilage and is 
subjected to a distinct biomechanical environment influ­
enced by complex occlusal forces and mandibular move­
ments [2, 4]. This fundamental anatomical and functional 
difference suggests that the pathological mechanisms 
underlying TMJOA may not simply mirror those in other 
joints, necessitating dedicated investigation.
Autophagy, a crucial cellular catabolic process, has 
emerged as a key regulatory mechanism in cartilage 
homeostasis. Its dysregulation is implicated in the patho­
genesis of various arthritic degenerative diseases [5, 
6]. Under inflammatory conditions, autophagy is often 
suppressed in chondrocytes, leading to an imbalance 
in extracellular matrix (ECM) metabolism—promot­
ing the expression of catabolic enzymes (e.g., MMP-
13, ADAMTS-5) while inhibiting anabolic factors (e.g., 
Collagen Type II Alpha 1 Chain, COL2A1; Aggrecan, 
ACAN) [7–9]. Consequently, enhancing autophagy has 
been proposed as a potential therapeutic strategy for OA.
Serum and glucocorticoid-regulated kinase 1 (SGK1), a 
member of the AGC kinase family, is a well-established 
negative regulator of autophagy [10–12]. Its expression 
is markedly elevated in osteoarthritic cartilage tissues, 
wherein SGK1 suppression effectively activates autoph­
agy and significantly attenuates ECM degradation [13]. 
In chondrocytes, inhibition of SGK1 with the Chinese 
herbal compound columbianetin activates autophagy, 
thereby reducing cell death and alleviating osteoarthritis 
progression [14]. Moreover, SGK1 modulates autoph­
agy through its action on the downstream transcription 
factor Forkhead box O1 (FoxO1) [15–17]. SGK1 phos­
phorylates FoxO1, prompting its translocation from the 
nucleus to the cytoplasm, which leads to FoxO1 down­
regulation and consequent suppression of autophagy [18, 
19].
Given the potential involvement of SGK1-FoxO1 
signaling and autophagic dysfunction in TMJOA, we 
hypothesized that SGK1 exacerbates TMJOA by inhibit­
ing autophagy via FoxO1 phosphorylation. In this study, 
we aimed to understand the impact of SGK1 on TMJOA 
in a mouse model. To elucidate potential underlying 
mechanisms, we investigated how SGK1 affects autoph­
agy and cell matrix degradation in mouse condylar chon­
drocytes (MCCs) by regulating FoxO1. Our findings 
contribute toward developing more effective TMJOA 
treatments.
Methods
Animal models
Twelve C57BL/6J mice (male; 8 weeks) were divided into 
Control (n = 6) and unilateral anterior crossbite (UAC) 
groups (n = 6) using a random allocation method. In the 
UAC group, the upper and lower incisors on the right 
side were bonded to construct an anterior anticlinal 
occlusal relationship, and the intervention lasted for 21 
days. In the Control group, the same operation was per­
formed but without bonding the cannulas. All mice were 
reared under the same conditions. All experimental pro­
cedures involving animals were approved by the Ethics 
Committee at Shandong University (no: 2022080606, 29 
July 2022).
Histological analysis
Arthritis was assessed using the Osteoarthritis Research 
Society International (OARSI) scoring system: grade 0: 
normal cartilage with smooth surface; grade 1: cartilage 
surface affected, with slight fibrosis and abrasion-like 
changes; grade 2: localized fibrosis in superficial layers of 
cartilage; grade 3: presence of vertical fissures (3.5 if there 
are branches); grade 4: cartilage erosion (4.5 if there is 
exfoliation); grade 5: exposed or semi-exposed phenom­
enon in the depths of cartilage; repair tissue on bone sur­
face may be 5.5; grade 6: joint deformation with marginal 
or central osteophytes.
To analyze optical density, positive cellular responses 
(brownish-yellow or reddish area) in the image were 
assessed in Image-Pro Plus 6.0 (Media Cybernetics, Inc. 
Rockville, MD, USA). The total positive optical density 
was determined by multiplying the area of the image pix­
els, and the average optical density (AOD) was obtained 
through their division. Positive response increases as the 
AOD rises, which is calculated by dividing optical density 
by pixel area.
Hematoxylin-eosin (H&E) staining
Tissue sections were placed in xylene for dewaxing; 
hematoxylin staining was processed for 5 min. After acid 
washing, the sections were immersed in a 0.5% eosin 
solution for 2 min before being desiccated and securely 
sealed. Sections were analyzed using the OARSI scoring 
system.
Safranin O staining
The tissue sections underwent deparaffinization, fol­
lowed by 3–5 min of staining with solid green and sub­
sequent washing to eliminate excess stain. The sections 
were stained with safranin O for 1 min and washed again, 
dehydrated in anhydrous ethanol, and blocked with

neutral gum. The proportion of positive areas was ana­
lyzed based on AOD.
Immunohistochemical (IHC) staining
Sections were dewaxed and dehydrated and then 
immersed in a 3% hydrogen peroxide solution for approx­
imately 10 min, followed by rinsing with PBS. An antigen 
retrieval solution was added and incubated at ambient 
temperature for 20  min; 10% goat serum was used for 
sealing. The primary antibody was diluted and incubated 
at 37 ℃ for 2  h. The secondary antibody labeled with 
horseradish peroxidase (Beyotime, China) was added 
and incubated at 37 ℃ for 1.5  h, and chromatography 
was performed. The proportion of positively stained cells 
was analyzed based on AOD, and the primary antibodies 
were against SGK1 (1:1000, Sigma, USA) and COL2A1 
(1:1000, Sigma).
SGK1-knockout mice
Twenty-four male, 6–8-week-old C57BL/6 mice were 
selected for TMJ intra-articular injection of AAV9-SGK1 
with AAV9-GFP (Vizen Biotech, China). Each mouse was 
injected unilaterally into the TMJ with 15 µl, 1 × 1012 vg/
ml. After 2 weeks, a 3-week UAC inflammation model 
(UAC group) and negative control groups were con­
structed. The mice were categorized into AAV9-GFP (6 
mice), AAV9-SGK1 (6 mice), UAC + AAV9-GFP (6 mice), 
and UAC + AAV9-SGK1 (6 mice) groups. The final TMJs 
were collected for histological analysis after euthanizing 
the mice.
MCCs culturing and characterization
MCCs were isolated from condylar tissues of eight male, 
3-week-old C57BL/6J mice. The condylar tissues were 
fragmented using delicate surgical scissors; the condy­
lar tissues underwent enzymatic digestion with 1 ml of 
pancreatic enzyme solution (0.25%) at 37 ℃ for 20 min, 
followed by digestion with 1  ml of type II collagenase 
solution (2 mg/ml) at 37 ℃ for 120 min. Afterward, the 
cells were placed in a culture medium containing 15% 
fetal bovine serum and incubated at 37℃ with 5% CO2. 
To visualize their characteristics, the MCCs were stained 
with Alcian blue (1%) for approximately 30  min, after 
which they were rinsed with distilled water. Samples 
were observed following dehydration and transparency 
procedures.
MCCs were subsequently subjected to procedures and 
assays, including cell transfection, drug treatment, cell 
immunofluorescence, 
monodansylcadaverine 
(MDC) 
staining, real-time quantitative PCR, western blot­
ting, and immunoprecipitation, which are detailed in 
Additional File (Table S1, Table S2  and Supplementary 
Methods).
Cell transfection
After achieving 85% density in 6-well plates, the MCCs 
underwent transfection using a transfection reagent 
(lipofectamine 
RNAiMAX 
reagent) 
(GenePharma, 
China). The cells were collected 48  h after transfection 
for examination. si-RNA (GenePharma) sequences are 
shown in Table S1.
Autophagy and FoxO1 Inhibition
To investigate functional rescue, we administered spe­
cific inhibitors to MCCs following siRNA transfec­
tion and IL-1β challenge. The cells were incubated with 
either chloroquine (CQ, 25 µmol/L; Sigma) to suppress 
autophagy or AS1842856 (1 µmol/L; Sigma) to inhibit 
FoxO1 signaling for a duration of 24 h. Additional experi­
mental procedures are described in the Supplementary 
Materials.
Statistical analysis
The t-test was used to analyze the differences between 
two groups. Data across multiple groups were compared 
using a one-way ANOVA followed by the Student–New­
man–Keuls post-hoc test. The normality was confirmed 
using Shapiro–Wilk test, and homogeneity of variance 
was verified via F-tests (for t-tests) or Bartlett’s test (for 
ANOVA). *P < 0.05, **P < 0.01, and ***P < 0.001 indicated 
significance. All statistical tests were performed using 
GraphPad Prism 9.0 (La Jolla, CA, USA).
Results
Establishment of TMJOA model and upregulation of SGK1
H&E staining and OARSI scoring indicated that the UAC 
group had a significantly higher score than the Control 
group (Fig.  1A and B). Safranin O staining analysis of 
AOD demonstrated reduced proteoglycan content in the 
UAC group (Fig. 1C and D). IHC staining and optical den­
sity values showed that the expression rate of COL2A1 in 
the UAC group was significantly lower than that in the 
Control group (Fig. 1E and F). Thus, the UAC inflamma­
tion model was successfully constructed, and the mouse 
condyle cartilage matrix degraded under inflammatory 
conditions. Conversely, IHC indicated markedly higher 
SGK1 expression in the UAC group (Fig.  1G and H). 
MCCs were successfully isolated and identified via Alcian 
blue staining (Fig. 1I). Furthermore, in IL-1β-stimulated 
MCCs, IF, qPCR, and WB analyses consistently showed 
upregulation of SGK1 at both mRNA and protein levels 
(Fig. 1J–N). These results confirm aberrant SGK1 upreg­
ulation in TMJOA inflammation.

degradation

non-inflammatory AAV9-GFP and AAV9-SGK1 groups, 
indicating that SGK1 mainly influences ECM metabolism

Given that SGK1 knockdown could alleviate ECM deg­
radation in TMJOA, we knocked down SGK1 in MCCs 
with the IL-1β-induced inflammatory microenviron­
ment (Fig.  2H–J). qPCR and WB showed that, in this 
inflammatory context, MCCs downregulated COL2A1 
and ACAN and upregulated MMP13 and ADAMTS-5, 
i.e., exhibiting ECM degradation. Knocking down SGK1 
gradually restored ECM metabolic homeostasis (Fig. 2K–
M). Thus, at the cellular level, IL-1β-induced ECM degra­
dation occurred in the inflammatory microenvironment 
of MCCs, which was reversed by SGK1 knockdown.
SGK1 knockdown enhances autophagy to alleviate ECM 
degradation
As SGK1 acts as an autophagy inhibitor, we investi­
gated whether cellular autophagy contributes to SGK1-
mediated ECM regulation. SGK1 knockdown promoted 
autophagy in MCCs, which was confirmed by the relative 
upregulation of Beclin-1 and LC3-II/LC3-I conversion 
levels and the relative downregulation of P62 expression 
(Fig. 3A–C). MDC staining further confirmed increased 
autophagosome formation (Fig.  3D and E), indicating 
that SGK1 negatively regulates autophagy in MCCs. Sub­
sequently, an autophagy inhibition rescue experiment 
was conducted using CQ, with ECM metabolic indicators 
detected using WB. CQ reversed the relief of ECM meta­
bolic disorders originally caused by SGK1 knockdown 
(Fig. 3F and G). This functional reversal experiment fur­
ther demonstrated that the ECM metabolic imbalance 
exhibited in inflamed MCCs is mediated by autophagy, 
and that SGK1 also affects ECM metabolism through 
autophagy.
SGK1 phosphorylate FoxO1 and regulate its subcellular 
localization
Co-immunoprecipitation confirmed a direct interac­
tion between SGK1 and FoxO1 in MCCs (Fig. 4A). WB 
confirmed that SGK1 knockdown inhibited FoxO1 phos­
phorylation in the inflammatory microenvironment of 
MCCs, i.e., the ratio of p-FoxO1/FoxO1 was markedly 
downregulated (Fig.  4B and C). Phosphorylation regu­
lation is often associated with subcellular localization. 
Our WB with IF analyses showed that SGK1 knock­
down increased FoxO1 in the nucleus (Fig. 4D–G). In the 
inflammatory microenvironment of MCCs, SGK1 inter­
acts with FoxO1 and induces phosphorylation, thereby 
regulating the subcellular localization of FoxO1. Spe­
cifically, this leads to an increase in FoxO1 outside the 
nucleus.
To determine whether FoxO1 mediates SGK1-
regulated autophagy and ECM metabolism, rescue 
experiments were performed using a FoxO1 inhibitor 
(AS1842856). WB showed that SGK1 knockdown alle­
viated ECM degradation and promoted autophagy in 
MCCs, which was reversed by AS (Fig. 5A and B). Thus, 
in the inflammatory conditions, SGK1 knockdown 
increased cellular autophagy, and alleviated ECM degra­
dation in a FoxO1-dependent manner.
Discussion
Current research investigating the influence of SGK1 
on OA progression is focused on major joints like knees 
and ankles and less so on TMJOA. As the only joint in 
the maxillofacial region, the soft and hard tissues of the 
TMJ are constantly altered by the interactions with the 
surrounding structures and their environment, including 
occlusal forces, resulting in a landscape more complex 
than that found in other joints. Additionally, the sensi­
tivity of SGK1-related inhibitors or drugs is regulated to 
some extent by SGK2 and SGK3 [1, 20]. In drug trials, the 
treatment of SGK1 by relevant inhibitors or drugs had a 
limited impact on the normal homeostasis of the over­
all organism [21]. Therefore, exploring the link between 
SGK1 and TMJOA has potential clinical therapeutic 
value.
In this study, SGK1 was found to regulate autophagy 
during cartilage matrix metabolism by altering FoxO1 
subcellular localization. We verified that SGK1 knock­
down can alleviate ECM degradation in TMJOA by acti­
vating cellular autophagy through both in vivo and ex 
vivo experiments. Rescue experiments using a FoxO1-
specific inhibitor (AS1842856) confirmed that FoxO1 is 
the core downstream target of SGK1 in regulating carti­
lage matrix metabolism (Fig. 5A and B). Furthermore, its 
mediated decrease in FoxO1 phosphorylation, resulting 
in increased retention in the nucleus, may be the key to 
the regulation of ECM metabolism by autophagy-associ­
ated proteins in MCCs (Fig. 5C).
SGK1 is an immediate-early gene widely expressed in 
the tissues examined but is subject to strict transcrip­
tional and post-transcriptional regulation. The half-life of 
both the mRNA and protein does not exceed 30 min [22]. 
This early and stringent transcriptional management 
means that SGK1 expression is characterized by protein 
stability, and therefore, any expression aberrations may 
influence various pathophysiological processes. SGK1

expression is generally minimal in most normal cells; 
however, it undergoes a significant increase under spe­
cific pathophysiological conditions such as excessive 
administration of glucocorticoids or saline corticoste­
roids, inflammation accompanied by the release of TGF-
β, elevated blood sugar levels, cellular shrinkage, and 
ischemia [23]. SGK1 overexpression and aberrant activ­
ity are closely associated with various disease mecha­
nisms, including immunoinflammation, hypertension, 
and fibrosis [21]. Aberrant upregulation of SGK1 has 
been observed in various chronic inflammatory diseases, 
including chronic inflammatory nephropathy [24] and 
pulmonary fibrosis [25]. Therefore, in our preliminary 
study, SGK1 upregulation in TMJOA was confirmed both 
in vitro and in vivo.
In inflammatory diseases, pathologic alterations in the 
ECM microenvironment are common. In normal TMJ, 
the cartilage ECM is structurally stable [26]. The develop­
ment of TMJOA is often accompanied by dynamic imbal­
ance, degradation, and disruption of the cartilage ECM 
structure [27]. Therefore, we linked SGK1 to cartilage 
ECM metabolism and confirmed that SGK1 knockdown 
attenuated ECM metabolic imbalance. This implies that 
SGK1 is involved in TMJOA development.
SGK1 is a regulator of cellular autophagy [28, 29], and 
suppressing SGK1 using SI113 enhances the conversion 
of LC3-II to LC3-I and elevates Beclin-1 levels in human 
glioblastoma cells, thereby facilitating cellular autophagy 
[30, 31]. However, its role in the regulation of autophagy 
proteins in MCCs is unclear. We confirmed that the cel­
lular autophagy mechanism mediated by SGK1 is also 
present in MCCs and that the ECM degradation exhib­
ited during MCC inflammation could be regulated by 
autophagy mechanisms. Therefore, the regulatory mech­
anism of SGK1 in MCC inflammation requires further 
study.
FoxO1 and SGK1 interact to regulate autophagy in 
OS cell lines [32, 33], but this has not been confirmed 
in MCCs. In this study, we determined that p-FoxO1 
undergoes direct binding with SGK1 in MCCs and sub­
sequently influences its subcellular localization. FoxO1 
is rendered inactive through phosphorylation, caus­
ing its translocation from the nucleus to the cytoplasm 
and subsequent degradation. Thus, it loses its ability to 
regulate autophagy-related genes like Beclin-1 and LC3-
b. This could potentially explain how SGK1 influences 
autophagy in MCCs. The potential alteration in the intra­
cellular distribution of FoxO1 may be associated with the 
presentation of its phosphorylated modification to chap­
erone 14-3−3 at its binding site, leading to their simulta­
neous movement into the nucleus. The exposure of the 
14-3−3 chaperone protein modifies the nuclear localiza­
tion sequence of FoxO1 and impedes its re-entry into the 
nucleus [34].
This study presents findings on the correlations among 
SGK1, autophagy, and cartilage ECM metabolism in 
TMJOA pathology. SGK1 is upregulated during TMJOA, 
and SGK1 knockdown mediates autophagy via the sub­
cellular localization of FoxO1, which alleviates ECM deg­
radation. The properties of SGK1 inhibitors observed in 
their respective sensitization assays suggest the potential 
for “SGK1 inhibition” to be investigated as a viable clini­
cal approach for treating TMJOA. However, the lack of 
functional response validation in animal studies and the 
lack of sufficient human TMJ samples have limited fur­
ther exploration of the association between SGK1 and 
TMJOA in the present study.
Conclusions
This study reveals for the first time the mechanism by 
which SGK1 acts as a regulatory factor involved in the 
TMJOA process in MCCs. SGK1 can directly bind to 
FoxO1 and enhance the p-FoxO1/FoxO1 translocation as 
well as nuclear degradation, thereby inhibiting autophagy 
in MCCs and disrupting the homeostasis of the cartilage 
matrix. Therefore, it may be possible to halt TMJOA pro­
gression via SGK1 inhibition.
