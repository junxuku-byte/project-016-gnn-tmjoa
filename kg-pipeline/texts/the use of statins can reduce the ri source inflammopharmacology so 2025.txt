Abstract
Background  Observational studies suggest that statins may be protective against osteoporosis and osteoarthritis. However, 
the association between statins and the risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis has not been fully clarified.
Objective  The association between statins and the risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis was explored by Mendelian rand-
omization analyses using pooled data from a large population-based genome-wide association study (GWAS).
Methods  Data on statin-associated single-nucleotide polymorphisms were obtained from version 10 of the FinnGen database, 
while information regarding osteoporosis and osteoarthritis was sourced from IEU Open GWAS and UK Biobank, database. 
The primary analytical method employed was inverse variance weighting to assess causal effects. Additional methods includ-
ing weighted median, simple median, weighted mode, and MR-Egger regression were utilized for further clarification. Odds 
ratios along with 95% confidence intervals were calculated to evaluate the causal relationship between statins and the risks 
associated with osteoarthritis and osteoporosis. Subsequently, sensitivity analyses were conducted to verify result reliability 
by employing Cochran’s Q test for heterogeneity assessment, MR-Egger intercept for detecting horizontal pleiotropy pres-
ence, as well as leave-one-out analysis to identify potentially influential single nucleotide polymorphisms.
Results  (1) The results of the inverse variance weighting method showed a negative causal relationship between geneti-
cally predicted statins and osteoporosis (OR = 0.998, 95% CI 0.996–0.999, P = 0.01) risk; and a negative causal relationship 
between statins and osteoarthritis (OR = 0.974, 95% CI 0.951–0.998, P = 0.037) risk; (2) MR-Egger-intercept analysis did 
not detect potential horizontal pleiotropy (osteoporosis: P = 0.848; osteoarthritis: P = 0.483); and (3) the findings provide 
favorable evidence that statins reduce the risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis as described in the observational study, but 
further research is needed on the specific mechanisms of statins for osteoporosis and osteoarthritis.
Conclusion  Statins reduce the risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis, which is consistent with the results of observational 
studies. Further studies are essential to validate these results and explore the underlying mechanisms in detail.
Keywords  Statin · Osteoporosis · Osteoarthritis · Mendelian randomization · Causality
Introduction
Osteoporosis (OP) is a common systemic skeletal disease in 
the population, associated with aging, and is characterized 
by a decrease in bone mineral density (BMD), leading to 
increased bone fragility, characterized by increased fracture 
risk in older adults (Sözen et al. 2017). It causes significant 
emotional, physical, and financial stress to patients and often 
leads to disability and reduced quality of life (Lane 2006)
Osteoarthritis (OA) is the most common joint disease, 
where degenerative changes in articular cartilage lead to 
aseptic inflammation and involvement of the entire joint tis-
sue (Glyn-Jones et al. 2015), and is a major cause of reduced 
quality of life in older adults. As the population ages, it is

and pressure, and swelling or deformity of the joints, which 
reduces the patient’s quality of life (Mandl 2019). Risk fac-
tors that have been identified as contributing to OA include 
age, gender, mechanical factors, genetic predisposition and 
obesity (Lane 2006). However, the exact etiology and patho-
logical changes of OA remain unclear, leading to a lack of 
curable treatments.
Hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A reductase inhibi-
tors (statins) have become the mainstay of medications for 
the prevention and treatment of cardiovascular disease 
(CVD), and statins appear to be potentially promising agents 
for the treatment of osteoporosis (). This is because key 
enzymes and molecules involved in cholesterol homeosta-
sis are closely linked to bone formation. Excess cholesterol 
may lead to osteoporosis, and cholesterol and its metabolites 
affect bone homeostasis by regulating the proliferation and 
stimulation of osteoblasts and osteoclasts (Bao et al., 2023).
Many researchers have investigated the effective mecha-
nisms of statin action on bone (Chamani et al. 2021). The 
current literature suggests that the effects of statins on bone 
may involve multiple mechanisms, including proliferation, 
differentiation, protection of osteoblasts, and reduction of 
osteoclastogenesis (Oryan et al. 2015). Therefore, statins 
may play an important role in the clinical management of 
osteoporosis.
Several studies have shown that disorders of lipid metabo-
lism may be one of the pathogenic mechanisms of osteoar-
thritis (Haj-Mirzaian et al. 2019; Kadam et al. 2013). For 
example, high lipid content in synovial fluid is associated 
with arthritic changes. In addition, elevated leptin levels 
in obese individuals are associated with articular carti-
lage destruction (Abramoff and Caldera 2020). Therefore, 
lipid-lowering drugs may play a role in altering the pro-
gression and symptoms of osteoarthritis. Existing studies 
suggest that statins are effective in reducing inflammation 
in patients with OA (Dancevic and McCulloch 2014; Siddiq 
et al. 2023). However, there are no randomized controlled 
trials (RCTs) validating the clinical efficacy of osteoporo-
sis and osteoarthritis with statin therapy. Implementing a 
traditional RCT is challenging due to practical, cost, and 
ethical constraints. Although observational studies are more 
feasible, their confidence in inferring causality from clinical 
phenotypes is reduced by confounding factors. In addition, 
cross-sectional data do not address the issue of temporal 
order between exposure and outcome, which can lead to 
reverse causality (Gala and Tomlinson 2020).
Mendelian randomization (MR) is a technique that uses 
genetic variation as the object of study, with instrumental 
variables (IVs) being genetic variants that are strongly cor-
related with an exposure factor (e.g., environmental factor, 
lifestyle, or medication, etc.) but are not associated with 
any confounders of the exposure–outcome association, and 
there is no pathway through which the instrumental vari-
able can influence other outcomes typically refers to single-
nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs), which serve to assess 
whether the observed relationship between exposure factors 
and outcomes is consistent with a causal effect (Evans and 
Davey Smith 2015). MR investigations differ from standard 
observational clinical research. MR employs single nucleo-
tide polymorphisms SNPs related with exposure events as 
instrumental variables. This technique adheres to Mendel’s 
law of random assignment, which simulates a "natural" RCT 
by effectively eliminating external environmental and cul-
tural impacts on behavior or other factors, as well as sta-
bilizing exposure over time. That is, random assignment 
performed before birth aligns with the causality timeline 
(Gupta et al. 2017). MR methods effectively address poten-
tial confounders and reverse causality (Li et al. 2024). MR 
analyses can accurately predict the outcome of RCTs in drug 
studies, facilitating early and systematic drug target screen-
ing (Khasawneh et al. 2022). Currently, two-thirds of FDA-
approved drugs are supported by genetic evidence (Ochoa 
et al. 2022).
Methods and materials
Ethics/consent statement
No ethical approval or informed consent was needed as this 
study was based on previously published articles and public 
databases.
Research design
This study examined the causal effect of statins on suscepti-
bility to osteoporosis and osteoarthritis by two-sample MR 
analysis. Statins were the exposure factor, whereas osteo-
porosis and osteoarthritis were the outcome variables. To 
conduct an MR study, three key assumptions must be met: 
first, the selected SNPs must be significantly correlated with 
statin exposure; second, the SNPs should be independent of 
potential confounders; and third, the SNPs must be associ-
ated with the risk of developing osteoporosis or osteoarthri-
tis only through the mechanism of statin use (Burgess and 
Thompson 2017).
Data sources
The FinnGen study, version 10, provides summary statistics 
from the Genome-Wide Association Study (GWAS) focus-
ing on statins. The study had a large cohort of 342,499 par-
ticipants, including 233,181 cases of European origin and

were obtained from the MRC-IEU database, with a sam-

Screening of ivs
Single-nucleotide polymorphisms (SNPs) were used as IVs 
and thresholded at a P value of < 5.0 × 10–8. To avoid link-
age disequilibrium (LD) bias, the LD of significant SNPs 
associated with exposure factors had to fulfill the following 
conditional conditions: r2 < 0.001 and a genetic distance of 
10,000 kb. In this study, SNPs significantly associated with 
exposure factors were extracted from the GWAS dataset of 
the outcome variables and the resulting IVs were examined 
to extract the following information Influence Allele, Allele 
Influence Magnitude (beta), Standard Error and p-value. 
The F statistics is used to test the strength of each IV and is 
calculated as follows F = R2 (N− 2)/(1− R2), where R2 is the 
proportion of the variance in the exposure factor explained 
by each IV and N is the sample size of the exposure dataset 
(Chen et al. 2022). When F > 10, there is no weak IV bias 
(Burgess and Thompson 2011).
Statistical analysis
The summary statistics of the exposure and outcome datasets 
were aligned so that the effect of SNP on exposure and the 
effect of SNP on outcome corresponded to the same allele. 
MR analyses were performed using inverse variance weight-
ing (IVW), weighted median, MR-Egger regression, sim-
ple mode, and weighted mode methods to infer causality. 
Among other things, the inverse variance weighting method 
is a standard approach in MR analyses for estimating the 
relationship between a single genetic instrumental variable 
and a phenotype. The contribution of each instrumental vari-
able (e.g., genetic variance) is weighted during the estima-
tion process, and the weighting is based on the inverse of the 
estimated variance of the effect of each instrumental variable 
on the exposure variable. Briefly, the effect of each instru-
mental variable was assigned a weight that was the inverse 
of its variance. This is done to enhance the precision of the 
estimates, especially when the precision of the estimates of 
the effects of different instrumental variables on the expo-
sure variable is inconsistent. The IVW method is a good 
choice when all instrumental variables are considered valid, 
i.e., they affect the outcome variable only by influencing the 
exposure variable and do not affect the outcome through 
any other means (Burgess et al. 2015; Hartwig et al. 2016). 
MR-Egger regression, on the other hand, is a method that 
incorporates Wald ratios into a meta-regression for estimat-
ing causal effects adjusted for any directional multinomial 
effects. MR-Egger regression is capable of detecting and 
correcting for cross-sectional confounders, and its intercepts 
can provide evidence of direct confounding. It allows for the 
presence of directional genetic pleiotropy in the included 
instrumental variables and indicates the presence of genetic 
pleiotropy when the regression intercept is non-zero and 
the p value is less than 0.05. MR-Egger regression is a 
good choice when there is a need to assess the Pleiotropy 
of instrumental variables or when there are doubts about 
the invalidity of instrumental variables(Bowden et al. 2015; 
Bowden et al. 2016). We used IVW as the primary method 
for MR (Zhang et al. 2022), and when each genetic variant 
satisfied the IVW assumption, the Wald estimates for each 
single nucleotide polymorphism were combined by means 
of meta-analysis to obtain an overall estimate (Zheng et al. 
2017). The IVW method gives the most reliable results when 
there is no horizontal polymorphism in IVW. Compared with 
the IVW method, the weighted median method improved the 
accuracy of the results, and complementary analyses using 
simple and weighted models further improved the accuracy 
of the results (Xiang et al. 2021).
Sensitivity analysis
Horizontal pleiotropy is a violation of the third IV hypoth-
esis when IVs affect outcomes through pathways that do not 
involve exposure. Violation of this criterion can severely 
compromise the accuracy of MR, leading to incorrect quan-
tification of causality, reduced statistical efficacy, and type 
I errors (Verbanck et al. 2018). We used the MR-Egger test 
to detect potential pleiotropy, provided that any pleiotropic 
effect of IVs on the results was independent of exposure. 
MR-Egger intercept  p value > 0.05 indicates that horizontal 
pleiotropy may not exist (Burgess and Thompson 2017). To 
assess the robustness of the results, we performed leave-
one-out sensitivity analyses, with each iteration involving 
the systematic exclusion of individual SNPs. We generated 
forest plots to visually explore potential pleiotropy.
Heterogeneity analysis
Heterogeneity refers to the variability between different IVs 
observed in MR studies. This variability suggests that each 
IV may contribute to the phenotype under study to a different 
extent, or it may only have a causal effect on the feature when 
interacting with other relevant SNPs. Therefore, the p value of 
the Cochran Q test was used to assess the heterogeneity among 
MR values. A p value of less than 0.05 indicates the presence 
of gender heterogeneity. The Mendelian randomized polyto-
mous residuals and outliers (MR-PRESSO) test was used to

detect and correct for horizontal polytomousness by remov-
ing outliers (Lin et al. 2022). MR-PRESSO is a method for 
assessing horizontal polymorphism in multi-instrument Men-
delian randomization using genome-wide summary associa-
tion statistics. It centers on improving the accuracy of causal 
inferences by detecting and processing horizontal pleiotropy 
(i.e., the phenomenon of genetic variation affecting multiple 
phenotypes), and identifying and correcting outliers (outliers). 
In statistical analyses, residuals refer to the difference between 
observed and predicted values. Multi-category residuals may 
refer to residuals that have many different distributions or char-
acteristics. Outliers, on the other hand, are those values that are 
significantly different from the majority of observed values, 
possibly due to measurement error, data entry error, or other 
unknown factors. The MR-PRESSO method consists of three 
main components: (1) Detection of Horizontal Multiplicity 
(MR-PRESSO Integral Test): this test is designed to detect 
the presence of horizontal multiplicity in the data. The signifi-
cance of horizontal pleiotropy is assessed by calculating the 
sum of the squared residuals of the effect of each genetic vari-
ant (e.g., SNP) with respect to the inverse variance weighted 
(IVW) result. The larger the sum of squared residuals, the 
more significant the horizontal pleiotropy. (2) Correction of 
horizontal pleiotropy by outlier correction (MR-PRESSO out-
lier test): After identifying genetic variants that may be subject 
to horizontal pleiotropy, the MR-PRESSO outlier test further 
analyses whether these variants are outliers. By comparing the 
distances (i.e., residuals) of individual genetic variants to the 
line of fit, variants with larger distances are more likely to be 
outliers. Once outliers are identified, they can be removed from 
the analysis to correct for the effects of horizontal pleiotropy. 
(3) Test for significant distortion in causal estimates before and 
after outlier removal (MR-PRESSO distortion test): this test is 
designed to examine whether there is a significant difference in 
the results of the causal estimates before and after the removal 
of outliers. By comparing the results before and after correc-
tion, it is possible to assess the extent to which outliers affect 
causal inference and to verify the validity of the correction 
method. Thus, the MR-PRESSO method provides researchers 
with a more accurate and reliable analytical tool by detecting 
and addressing these issues (Verbanck et al. 2018). Statistical 
analyses were performed in this study using  R (version 4.3.3) 
and the R package (TwoSampleMR and MR-PRESSO). The 
test level α was 0.05 (p < 0.05) and the difference was statisti-
cally significant.
Results
Screening results of SNPs
After strictly adhering to the IVs selection criteria, we per-
formed a complex instrumental variable selection process. 
First, we selected SNPs significantly associated with statins 
therapy (P < 5 × ­10–8) in the GWAS dataset. Second, we 
established the chain disequilibrium parameter (r2 = 0.001, 
kb = 10,000), which ultimately resulted in the selection of 
71 different SNPs. Third, we extracted data for the above 
selected SNPs from the summary statistics of the outcome 
traits osteoporosis and osteoarthritis, respectively, and also 
performed allelic matching, a harmonization process where 
SNPs with incongruent alleles and those that were uncor-
rectable for the ambiguous palindromic SNPs were removed. 
And MR-PRESSO was used to identify and remove outli-
ers, finally, we screened 30 SNPs about statins associated 
with OP as IVs; and also 56 SNPs about statins associated 
with OA as IVs. And, we calculated the F statistics for each 
SNP, and they all exceeded the noteworthy threshold of 10. 
This observation demonstrates the substantial instrumental 
strength of these SNPs, thus ensuring the robustness of our 
subsequent MR analyses.

Discussion
Exploring the causal relationship between exposure factors 
and osteoporosis and osteoarthritis disease is essential for 
disease prevention and the development of improved treat-
ment strategies. In this two-sample Mendelian randomiza-
tion study, we revealed that statins reduce the risk of osteo-
porosis and osteoarthritis in a European population, with a 
negative correlation between the two from a genetic predic-
tion perspective.
Previous studies have highlighted the positive shadow 
of statins on cartilage synthesis and metabolism (Bao et al. 
2023; Chamani et al. 2021; Ren and Zhou 2024). Our study 
further demonstrates the positive effects of statins in pre-
venting OA and OP. Several previous observational studies 
have explored the relationship between statins and osteopo-
rosis and osteoarthritis. In an experimental animal study on

(Goto et al. 2017). In addition, statins prevented cartilage 
degeneration after IL-1β stimulation and induced anti-

signaling pathway (Saberianpour et al. 2022). In addition, 
statins also attenuate the inflammatory response induced by 
sodium iodoacetate in a rat joint model (Pathak et al. 2015). 
In terms of clinical management of osteoporosis and osteo-
arthritis, previous studies have found that long-term use of 
statins reduces the incidence of osteoarthritis and slows its 
progression (Mohajer et al. 2024). Furthermore, an obser-
vational study conducted by Dutch scholars found that long-
term use of statins reduced the incidence of osteoarthritis 
and slowed its progression (Clockaerts et al. 2012). It has 
also been demonstrated that statins do not reduce the risk of 
hospitalization for osteoarthritis in the hip and knee. In addi-
tion, a Korean study demonstrated that statin use was associ-
ated with a significantly lower risk of osteoporotic fracture 
in an elderly population (Seo et al. 2023). In a population-
based cohort study in Taiwan, researchers found that statin

both men and women (Lin et al. 2018a, b). Whether statins 
can treat osteoporotic osteoarthritis remains controversial 
in the existing phase of research (Lin et al. 2018a, b; Wang 
et al. 2021). However, the present MR study provides a 
theoretical basis for previous observational studies, and the 
detection of specific related genes can predict their treat-
ment effects. It also provides important new evidence for 
the rational use of statins in patients with osteoporosis and 
osteoarthritis, which is important for disease prevention and 
the development of improved treatment strategies. However, 
some adverse effects of statins, such as gastrointestinal reac-
tions, cognitive dysfunction, hepatic dysfunction, muscle 
weakness and pain, and in severe cases, rhabdomyolysis; 
although the incidence of the above symptoms is still low, 
these adverse effects may limit its use in some special pop-
ulations (Attardo et al. 2022; Bellosta and Corsini 2018). 
Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) are needed to validate 
the efficacy of statins in the treatment of clinical OA and to 
avoid the serious adverse effects associated with oral statins.
For the application of statins to reduce the risk of osteo-
porosis and osteoarthritis, we can explain this causal rela-
tionship on the basis of existing biological mechanisms. 
The main pathogenesis of osteoporosis is a disorder of bone 
metabolism characterized by an imbalance between bone 
formation and resorption, which ultimately leads to a sig-
nificant decrease in bone mass and density (Zou et al. 2020). 
Osteoarthritis is characterized by an imbalance between 
altered homeostasis (anabolic) and catabolic (degenerative) 
metabolism within the articular cartilage mediated by the 
innate immune system, leading to synovial membrane hyper-
plasia, degeneration of the articular cartilage, disruption 
of the structural and functional integrity of the cartilage’s 
extracellular matrix, subchondral sclerosis, and formation 
of osteoclasts (Mukherjee and Das 2024). The mechanisms 
by which statins modulate osteoporosis and osteoarthritis 
have not been fully elucidated. However, several possible 
mechanisms have been proposed to elucidate the pathways 
by which statins affect bone metabolism and inflammation. 
First, statins increase bone morphogenetic protein-2 (BMP-
2) through the Ras-PI3K-Akt/MAPK signaling pathway 
(Ruan et al. 2012), and BMP-2 induces osteoblast differen-
tiation through Runt-related transcription factor 2 (Runx2) 
(Jensen et al. 2010; Nguyen et al. 2022). Second, statins 
inhibit the mevalonate pathway, preventing the synthesis of 
downstream products. As a result, FPP and GGPP synthe-
sis is blocked (Goldstein and Brown 1990), which nega-
tively regulates osteoblast differentiation (Lee et al. 2022; 
Weivoda and Hohl 2011). Third, statins inhibit osteoblast 
apoptosis via the TGFβ/Smad3 pathway (Heldin, Miyazono, 
and ten Dijke 1997). Fourth, statins inhibit osteoclastogen-
esis via the OPG/RANKL/RANK pathway (Tsubaki et al. 
2012). Based on the above mechanisms of action, statins are 
thought to be associated with bone anabolism. We also con-
firmed a causal relationship between statins use and reduced 
risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis in terms of genetic 
correlation and Mendelian randomization analysis. These 
results enhance our understanding of the role of statins in 
osteoporosis and osteoarthritis prevention and suggest that 
statins may be an effective prevention strategy for individu-
als at risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis.

validity of the results of our Mendelian randomization analy-

There are several limitations to this study. First, our focus 
on excluding SNPs associated with known confounders may 
have overlooked other unidentified confounders that may 
influence the relationship between statins and OA. This limi-
tation highlights the need for further research to identify 
these potential confounders. Second, although statins have 
shown potential benefit in the prevention of osteoarthritis, 
specific treatment regimens and dosages need to be deter-
mined by further research and clinical practice. Third, our 
study population was of exclusively European origin. This 
factor limits the generalizability of our findings to other pop-
ulations due to different disease patterns in different ances-
tral backgrounds. Fourth, there are several statins, each with 
different pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic properties. 
These differences, such as variations in absorption rates and 
tissue binding affinities, may affect the therapeutic efficacy 
of statins. Therefore, our future research program includes 
a focus on individual statins or different statins classes to 
understand their effects in more detail.
Conclusions
This study, at the genetic level, demonstrates that statins 
can reduce the risk of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis in the 
population in a causal manner. Therefore, this study pro-
vides potential evidence for the future use of statins in anti-
osteoporosis and osteoarthritis treatment. However, further 
research is essential to confirm our findings. This includes 
the conduct of large-scale RCTs focused on explicitly assess-
ing the beneficial effects and clear mechanisms of action of 
statins in the treatment of osteoporosis and osteoarthritis.
