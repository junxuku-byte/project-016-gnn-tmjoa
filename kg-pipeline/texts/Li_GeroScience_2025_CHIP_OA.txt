Abstract  Osteoarthritis (OA) is a common degener-
ative joint disease characterized by articular cartilage 
degeneration, joint inflammation, and bone hyper-
plasia. Clonal hematopoiesis of indeterminate poten-
tial (CHIP) refers to the presence of cancer-related 
variants in blood cells of individuals without malig-
nancies. However, it is unclear whether the proin-
flammatory state induced by CHIP affects the onset 
of OA. Thus, we investigated CHIP as a risk factor 
for OA. Here, we analyzed whole-exome sequence 
(WES) data from 45,380 UK Biobank participants 
without OA at baseline to identify CHIP. Multivari-
ate Cox regression models were used to evaluate the 
association between CHIP and incident OA as well 
as different sites of OA onset. Additionally, enrich-

Despite aging and obesity being common risk fac-
tors for both CHIP and OA, the direct links between 
these conditions have not been studies previously. 
Somatic mutation in myeloid cells has been associ-
ated with chemokine overexpression and inflam-
masome hyperactivation [16], suggesting potential 
contributions to OA pathogenesis through CHIP-
mediated immune dysregulation and low-grade 
inflammation [17]. Chronic inflammation may sus-
tain CHIP, with recent study demonstrating that 
interferon-gamma can drive the clonal expansion of 
DNMT3A-mutant hematopoietic stem cells, creat-
ing a feedback loop between CHIP and inflamma-
tory response in OA, ultimately leading to cartilage 
degeneration and clinical OA [18]. Therefore, we 
performed bioinformatics and statistical analysis on 
whole exome sequencing (WES) and proteomics data 
from the UK Biobank to explore whether CHIP may 
increase the risk of OA.
Patients and methods
Study population
UK Biobank (UKB) is a large prospective study that 
collected heath and genetic data from approximately 
500,000 participants aged 40–69 years between 2006 
and 2010. At the baseline visit, participants under-
went physical examination and provided detailed life-
style and health-related information via a touchscreen 
questionnaire. Blood samples were collected from 22 
regional assessment centers across the UK. Addition-
ally, UKB provided whole exome and whole genome 
sequencing data for a subset of participants [19]. Here, 
we analyzed all patients with OA in the UKB, except 
those with a confirmed diagnosis of OA at baseline. 
OA was defined using qualifying International Clas-
sification of Diseases (ICD) diagnostic code for 
osteoarthritis (polyarthritis, coxarthrosis, gonarthro-
sis, arthrosis of first carpometacarpal joint, and other 
arthrosis). The exclusion criteria included (1) missing 
WES data, (2) missing proteomic data, and (3) known 
OA at baseline (n = 7199). The detailed study flow 
chart refers to Supplementary Fig. 1.

the leading cause to pain and disability among joint 
diseases, according to the World Health Organiza-

ized by articular cartilage degeneration, bone hyper-
plasia, joint swelling and pain, and often affects the 
hands, knees, and hips [3]. Current OA treatments 
emphasize non-pharmacological strategies such as 
weight management and exercise, pharmacological 
therapies for symptoms relief, and surgical inter-
ventions [4]. However, the precise pathogenesis of 
OA remains unclear, though factors like mechanical 
stress, obesity, aging, and genetic predispositions all 
contributed to its development [5]. Recent research 
questions the historical view of OA as solely degen-
erative as senescent chondrocytes could release 
pro-inflammatory mediators which then stimulated 
production of cytokines from synovial cells, leading 
to low-grade inflammation and accelerated cartilage 
degradation. Both the innate and adaptive immune 
response also play roles in altering bone remodeling 
[6].
Clonal hematopoiesis (CH) refers to the clonal 
expansion of hematopoietic stem cells driven by 
somatic mutations. This phenomenon is increasingly 
common with age, identified in 10% of persons older 
than 70  years [7]. Clonal hematopoiesis of indeter-
minate potential (CHIP) is a subtype of CH defined 
by the presence of clonally expanded hematopoi-
etic stem cells at a variant allele frequency (VAF) 
of more than 2% without evidence of hematologi-
cal malignancies, dysplasia, or cytopenia. Although 
CHIP itself is not considered malignant, it increases 
the risk of subsequent hematological malignan-
cies by 0.5–1% per year and is associated with other 
adverse health outcomes including cardiovascular 
diseases [8, 9]. Common CHIP-related mutations 
occur in epigenetic regulators like DNMT3A, TET2, 
ASXL1, DNA damage repair genes PPM1D, TP53, 
regulatory tyrosine kinase JAK2, and mRNA splice-
osome components SF3B1 and SRSF2 [10]. CHIP is 
associated with a proinflammatory state which accel-
erates atherosclerosis. CHIP is influenced by genetic 
and acquired risk factors such as germline variants, 
age, lifestyle factors like smoking and obesity, and 
inflammation [11, 12]. Studies have shown that when 
mutant cells in peripheral blood exceed a VAF of 
10%, there is a higher incidence of hematological 
malignancies and non-malignant conditions, such as

WES and identification of CHIP mutations
DNA samples extracted from complete blood samples 
were sequenced at the Regeneron Genetics Center 
on the Illumina NovaSeq 6000 using 75-base-pair 
paired-end sequencing reads and 10-base-pair index 
reads. The DNA sequencing data was mapped to the 
GRCh38 genome reference with BWA MEM [20]. 
According to a series of quality control (QC) opera-
tions and Seal Point Balinese (SPB) protocol, samples 
showing difference between genetically determined 
and reported sex, high rates of heterozygosity or con-
tamination (FREEMIX score > 5%), low sequence 
coverage (less than 85% of targeted bases achieving 
20 × coverage), or sample duplicates were excluded 
[20, 21]. The reads aligned in the BAM files were per-
formed using the Picrad MarkDuplicates tool to mark 
all potential duplicate reads for downstream analyses.
As previously mentioned, CHIP refers to the detec-
tion of somatic gene mutations associated with mye-
loid malignancies in peripheral blood or bone mar-
row cells in the absence of hematological disorders 
with VAF ≥ 2% [8]. In this study, the VAF was cat-
egorized as follows: a VAF of less than 2% indicated 
the absence of CHIP, a VAF between 2 and 10% was 
defined as any clone, and a VAF of 10% or higher was 
considered indicative of a large CHIP clone. Previous 
studies have shown that larger clones may be associ-
ated with more adverse outcomes. Briefly, the Mutect2 
tool and various filtering steps were used to identify 
somatic variants. Variants calling and annotation on 
CRAM files gained from UKB (Fields 23143–23144) 
were performed using Mutect2, Genome Analy-
sis Toolkit (GATK) v4.4.0.0, and SnpEff (v5.1) [22, 
23]. Then, we applied a list of QC steps after somatic 
calling (Supplementary Table  1), and variants were 
removed if (1) variant allele frequency (VAF) ≤ 2%; 
(2) total read depth was < 20; (3) alternate allele depth 
(AD) ≤ 3; (4) both forward and reverse sequencing 
reads (F1R2 and F2R1) ≤ 1; (5) variants that had a 
median VAF ≥ 0.35 [24]. Sequencing artifacts and ger-
mline variants were excluded as previously reported as 
well as additional QC steps [25].
Blood proteomic
Baseline blood samples were collected in EDTA 
tubes, plasma was separated by centrifugation, 
and then the supernatant was aliquoted and stored 
at − 80  °C. Samples were shipped to Sweden for 
Olink Explore Proximity Extension Assay analysis 
using antibodies with unique oligonucleotides [26].
In total, proteomic analysis was conducted on 
plasma samples from 54,306 participants in the UKB 
in collaboration with 13 biopharmaceutical com-
panies. The experimenters were blinded to sample 
characteristics and clinical data. After a series of 
rigorous protein data processing and QC, 1463 spe-
cial proteins were screened, encompassing mainly 
cardiometabolic, inflammatory, oncological, and 
neurological proteins [27]. Protein expression lev-
els are reported as normalized protein expression 
(NPX) values. NPX values are estimated by dividing 
the counts for each sample and detection method by 
the counts of the extended control, followed by log-
transformation. Variability was adjusted using the 
median normalized counts of the extended control, 
the median NPX value of the specific batch, and the 
median NPX value of the specific test for each batch 
[28].
Clinical variables
Clinically plausible variables to influence the devel-
opment of OA were chosen from the UKB database to 
investigate their association with the risk of OA (Sup-
plementary Table  2). The following were included: 
demographics characteristics such as age, sex, ethnic-
ity, body mass index (BMI), Townsend deprivation 
index, and comorbid conditions in the disease includ-
ing hypertension, diabetes, CVD, and chronic kidney 
disease (CKD) to estimate potential health issues that 
OA individual might have. Lifestyle factors included 
cigarette and alcohol intake, and amount of physical 
activity per week were retrieved.
OA outcomes
Diagnostic data were linked to the UK’s electronic 
health record system, with OA diagnosis docu-
mented by hospital specialists, family doctors in 
primary care, or staff at the UK Death Registry. OA 
was classified using three-digit codes from the 10th 
Revision of the International Classification of Dis-
eases (ICD-10) for knee, hip, primary osteoarthri-
tis, and other OA-related diagnoses (Supplementary 
Table 3). The primary outcome was the identification 
of new OA cases, based on the earliest reported date

Statistical and analysis
Cox and subgroup analysis
We applied the chi-square test to compare differences 
between categorical variables. For differences in con-
tinuous variables, we performed descriptive analyses 
of the variables using Student’s t test. To investigate 
the relationship between CHIP and the risk of OA, we 
applied Cox survival analysis. Individuals were cat-
egorized into three groups based on VAF frequency 
as mentioned before: no CHIP, 2% < VAF < 10% and 
VAF > 10%. Then, to further explore the relationship 
between CHIP and OA risk, we utilized the multi-
variate Cox regression models. OA was categorized 
based on the site of onset and adjusted for various 
factors across three progressively refined models: 
Model 1 adjusted for age, sex, BMI, ethnicity, and 
social status; Model 2 also adjusted for smoking sta-
tus, alcohol frequency, and weekly physical activity; 
Model 3 further adjusted for hypertension, diabetes, 
CVD, and CKD. The reported results are presented as 
hazard ratios (HRs), 95% confidence intervals (CIs), 
and P values. Missing covariate data were addressed 
by creating separate categories. To account for poten-
tial outcomes arising from mortality events, we con-
ducted multivariable-adjusted competing risk regres-
sion models using Fine and Gray’s subdistribution 
hazard method.
In order to further investigate the potential effect 
modifications by various covariates on the associa-
tion between CHIP and OA, we performed subgroup 
analyses using the jstable package. Specifically, study 
participants were stratified based on predefined covar-
iates, such as age, sex, and BMI. Within each sub-
group, univariate Cox proportional hazards regression 
models were used to assess the risks of CHIP and OA 
events, and hazard ratios (HRs) with 95% confidence 
intervals (CIs) were calculated. Additionally, interac-
tion terms between CHIP and the respective subgroup 
covariates were included in the models to compute 
the P for interaction.
Enrichment and protein analysis
To account for the multiplicity of tests, the Bonfer-
roni correction was applied to evaluate significant 
associations, considering the total number of proteins 
analyzed (n = 1463). This method was employed to 
control the family-wise error rate, setting the thresh-
old for significance at P < 0.05 [29]. After applying 
the correction, we conducted enrichment analysis on 
the proteins that showed significant results in models 
1 and 2 (generalized linear model and Cox regression 
analysis) using the ClusterProfiler R package [30]. 
The significant genes identified in both model 1 and 
model 2 were intersected to obtain a common gene 
set. This overlapping set of genes was then used as 
input for enrichment analysis. Specifically, we per-
formed gene ontology (GO) enrichment, as well as 
pathway analyses for KEGG and Reactome data-
bases. Only pathways with an adjusted P value < 0.05 
were considered statistically significant and were sub-
sequently presented. This integrative approach ena-
bled us to systematically identify and highlight the 
key biological processes and pathways underlying the 
observed associations.
To gain deeper biological insights, we compared 
the significant proteins against the full Olink pro-
tein set as a background reference. The Fisher exact 
test was used to determine statistical significance, 
with p values adjusted for multiple comparisons 
using the Benjamini–Hochberg procedure to control 
the false discovery rate. To handle potential biases 
from missing covariate data, we first applied mul-
tivariate imputation by chained equations using the 
mice R package to impute covariates with a miss-
ing rate of ≥ 1% [31]. Then, we normalized the raw 
data by calculating z scores and employed multiple 
linear regression models to examine the association 
between CHIP and these proteins. Cox regression 
models were subsequently used to investigate the 
relationships between biomarkers and the occur-
rence of OA. Proteins found to be significant were 
considered potential mediators for further mediation 
analysis. The proportion mediated (PM) was esti-
mated using the mediation package [32], and 95%

CIs for the PM were computed through non-para-
metric bootstrapping with 1000 iterations.
All data were analyzed using R software (ver-
sion 4.2.2). OA diagnosis was defined as an event, 
and the censor date was the time from baseline to 
OA diagnosis. Correlations with P < 0.001 were 
considered statistically significant. A false discov-
ery rate (FDR) adjusted P value < 0.05 for analy-
ses involving proteins was considered statistically 
significant.

Association of CHIP with the risk of OA and 
different sites of OA
To verify the association between CHIP and OA, 
potential confounding factors were adjusted by Cox 
regression analysis (Table  2). After adjustment for 
demographic, lifestyle factors and comorbidities, 
CHIP was independently associated with increased 
risk of OA (HR: 1.19; 95% CI: 1.10–1.29; P < 0.001). 
A VAF > 10% exerted a more pronounced effect on 
the likelihood of developing OA (HR: 1.46; 95% CI: 
1.28–1.68; P < 0.001). CHIP increased the risk of 
OA regardless of the involvement sites. A consistent 
increase risk of OA was observed in polyarticular OA 
(HR: 1.64; 95% CI: 1.31–2.05; P < 0.001), hip (HR: 
1.36; 95% CI: 1.15–1.61; P < 0.001), knee (HR: 1.27; 
95% CI: 1.11–1.45; P < 0.001), and other OA (HR: 
1.30; 95% CI: 1.16–1.45; P < 0.001) except wrist-
palm OA (HR: 1.12; 95% CI: 0.62–2.02; P = 0.705). 
Moreover, a VAF > 10% markedly amplified this risk. 
The results from Cox regression analysis were con-
sistent after excluding OA occurring within 2  years 
of study entry, verifying the significant association 
between CHIP and polyarticular and hip OA (Supple-
mentary Table 9).

Discussion
CHIP is a precursor to myeloid malignancies and has 
been associated with increased mortality and adverse 
outcomes such as atherosclerosis and other malignan-
cies [34]. CHIP has recently been implicated in the 
pathogenesis of other systemic inflammatory diseases 
such as gout, rheumatoid arthritis, and osteoporosis 
[35–40]. Our study was the first to establish a clinical 
link between CHIP and OA utilizing WES and pro-
teomic data from over 45,000 participants from the 
UKB genetics study cohort. We identified a strong 
association between CHIP and incident OA, particu-
larly in individuals with large clones of VAF > 10%. 
After considering the competing risks of death, the 
results showed that CHIP still increased the risk of 
OA. Moreover, DNMT3A and JAK2 genes among 
major CHIP-affected genes had the most significant 
effect on OA. The genetic results suggest that the 
relationship between CHIP and the risk of OA is 
likely to be caused through a mutation in one of the 
DNMT3A and JAK2 genes, and further site knockout 
experiments will be needed to verify in the future. 
Polyarticular and hip OA were significantly associ-
ated with CHIP carrier status. Optimizing control of 
OA risk factors such as weight management might be 
especially important in CHIP carriers to prevent or 
delay the occurrence of OA. Consistent with previous 
analyses, our study identified an increased incidence 
of CHIP with age [7]. Common protein pathways 
enriched in CHIP were involved in inflammatory and 
immune response mechanisms, and some proteins 
like CD5, CD79B, CEACAM1, FOLR2, LILRA5, 
SIRPB1, and TXNDC15 showed regulatory functions 
in both OA and CHIP. We speculate that CHIP may 
influence OA through proinflammatory states and 
protein expression.
CHIP as a phenomenon that becomes increas-
ingly common with age, has significance far beyond 
the blood system itself. It exerts a profound impact 
on the musculoskeletal system by driving chronic, 
low-grade systemic inflammation. CHIP significantly 
increases the risk of osteoporosis and fractures [35], 
exacerbates the severity and progression of joint dis-
eases such as rheumatoid arthritis and osteoarthritis, 
and may even increase the risk of onset. In addition, 
it promotes the occurrence and development of sarco-
penia, and may also affect other inflammatory muscu-
loskeletal and connective tissue diseases [41].
Our findings also indicated that proteins associated 
with CHIP were significantly enriched in inflamma-
tory pathways, similar to those indicated in OA. This 
overlap suggested that the somatic mutations leading 
to CHIP might also influence OA progression through 
chronic low-grade inflammation. Similar findings 
have been observed in cardiovascular studies, where 
CHIP contributes to atherosclerosis and other inflam-
matory conditions [42]. Despite CHIP and OA shar-
ing some common enriched biological pathways, 
the mechanistic connections between them remain 
speculative. The shared pathways relating to immune 
response and extracellular matrix organization might 
imply that systemic inflammation could bridge these 
two conditions [39, 43]. Pathways analyses revealed 
that OA is more closely related to extracellular matrix 
and structural pathways, while CHIP was linked to 
immune signaling and membrane receptor activity 
[44]. This divergence suggests distinct underlying 
biological mechanisms, with OA primarily involving 
tissue degeneration and proinflammatory states, while 
CHIP is characterized by immune dysregulation. 
Common immunoregulatory and inflammatory path-
ways might bridge the interaction between CHIP and 
OA. For instance, CHIP might increase susceptibility 
to OA by altering the function of the immune system. 
As a consequence, these two pathological states may 
share a biological connection at the levels of immu-
nity and cellular homeostasis, warranting further 
investigation.
The comprehensive proteomics analysis in this 
study identified key proteins associated with OA, 
including CD5, CD79B, CEACAM1, FOLR2, 
LILRA5, SIRPB1, and TXNDC15. CD5 is a gly-
coprotein predominantly expressed on the surface 
of T lymphocytes and a subset of B lymphocytes. 
CD5 can regulate intracellular signals induced by 
antigen receptors on both T and B cells to influence 
T and B cell selection and hence immunotolerance 
[45]. CD79B is a component of the B cell receptor 
(BCR) complex and participates in BCR signaling, 
thus affecting the development and functioning of B 
cells [46]. CEACAM1 is mainly involved in cell–cell 
adhesion, signal transduction and immune regula-
tion, and is highly expressed in the tumor microenvi-
ronment [47]. FOLR2, encoding folate receptor β, is 
mainly expressed in the placenta and certain cancer 
cells, facilitating the uptake and metabolism of folate. 
Of note, overexpression of FOLR2 is often associated

with accelerated cancer progression and poor patient 
prognosis [48]. FOLR2 may indicate increased activ-
ity of CHIP-related inflammatory macrophages. 
LILRA5 is a leukocyte immunoglobulin-like recep-
tor exhibiting both activating and inhibitory functions 
in leukocytes and may play a role in eliciting innate 
immune responses [49]. SIRPB1 is an inhibitory 
receptor that is mainly expressed on myeloid cells. It 
regulates the activation and function of immune cells 
by binding to its ligand [50]. TXNDC15 is a thiore-
doxin-like protein that may play a role in protein 
folding and intracellular redox balance. It has been 
reported to be associated with Meckel syndrome. The 
abovementioned proteins function as seven media-
tion proteins of CHIP and OA, and may offer valuable 
insights as potential biomarkers or therapeutic targets 
for progression of CHIP and OA.
These proteins might play a role in the pathogene-
sis of both CHIP and OA as potential common molec-
ular targets. Subsequent gene knockout or overexpres-
sion experiments might verify the specific biological 
effects of these proteins in OA and CHIP. Both OA 
and CHIP involve multiple immune and inflamma-
tory pathways, which may be activated in the chronic 
inflammatory environment, contributing to the accel-
erated progression of OA by CHIP. The seven media-
tor proteins discovered in the study provide new 
insights into the relationship between CHIP and OA. 
CHIP mutations probably induce a long-term inflam-
matory state, thereby affecting the health and stabil-
ity of the joints, providing a favorable environment 
for the occurrence of OA. We postulated that CHIP 
is associated with a pro-inflammatory state, which 
might accelerate the articular cartilage degeneration, 
contributing to the development of OA.
Furthermore, aging serves as a shared risk factor 
for both CHIP and OA, with the prevalence of CHIP 
increasing significantly after the age of 50 years [51]. 
This age-related accumulation of somatic muta-
tions in hematopoietic stem cells may exacerbate the 
inflammatory environment within the joints, leading 
to an accelerated progression of OA in individuals 
with CHIP. This study reinforces the importance of 
considering CHIP as a contributor to the pathogenesis 
of non-hematological conditions such as OA, particu-
larly in aging populations. Further research is needed 
to elucidate the specific genetic mutations driving 
CHIP and their direct impact on joint health. Prospec-
tive studies could clarify whether CHIP serves as a 
risk factor for early-onset OA or is merely a concur-
rent condition associated with aging. Further under-
standing to this relationship could lead to novel thera-
peutic targets that address both the hematopoietic and 
joint degeneration associated with aging.
The study described the first systematic assess-
ment of the association between CHIP and OA, offer-
ing novel perspective on the broader impact of CHIP 
and expanding the scope of CHIP research in inflam-
matory diseases. Through mediation analysis, the 
study explored the possibility that CHIP may indi-
rectly affect OA through inflammatory and immune 
pathways, providing potential molecular targets and 
mechanism directions for subsequent studies, and 
providing a reference for clinical preventive strategies 
in CHIP carriers. Despite finding a significant asso-
ciation between CHIP and OA incidence, this study 
is an observational study and cannot conclusively 
determine whether CHIP directly causes OA. There-
fore, there are limitations in determining whether 
CHIP leads to OA development or OA development 
gives rise to CHIP. Furthermore, there were potential 
confounding factors, such as genetic background and 
detailed physical activity levels that were not fully 
accounted for. The biological mechanisms behind 
the observed associations and the causal relationship 
between CHIP and OA remain to be investigated. 
Future studies will focus on animal model construc-
tion and in vitro or in vivo mechanism experiments 
to establish causal associations as well as to better 
understand how CHIP could act as a driver for OA 
development. At the same time, target analysis of 
specific mutation sites and proteins will provide pos-
sible therapeutic tools for early prevention and prog-
nosis of OA. Understanding these connections could 
help future development of targeted therapies for OA 
in individuals with CHIP, potentially improving dis-
ease management and preventing progression in high-
risk populations. The link between CHIP and OA is 
likely to be an inflammatory state; therefore, CHIP 
has the potential to prevent the development of early 
OA or risk stratification, as CHIP affects the expres-
sion of inflammatory factors in the microenvironment 
and the immune system. If someone is found to be 
a CHIP carrier can be prevented to reduce the inci-
dence of OA. A growing body of clinical and in vivo 
evidence supports the potential that CHIP may lead 
to dysregulation of immune- and inflammation-
related pathways, and the functional consequences of

CHIP-associated mutations may have a potential role 
in the treatment of OA.
In conclusion, this study demonstrated a signifi-
cant association between CHIP and incident OA, 
suggesting that CHIP may exacerbate OA progres-
sion through inflammatory and immune response 
mechanisms. This discovery not only deepens our 
understanding of the role of CHIP in systemic inflam-
mation but also provides a theoretical basis for the 
development of OA interventions tailored for CHIP 
carriers. CHIP may provide a mechanistic explana-
tion for the clinical response and low-inflammatory 
state observed in OA. Further experimental and lon-
gitudinal studies are needed to validate the mecha-
nisms and to elucidate the specific molecular mecha-
nisms, as well as their causal relationship and clinical 
applicability.
