Can treatment with chondroitin and glucosamine 
sulphate prevent changes in the articular disc caused by 
temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis?

Abstract
Background: Chondroitin and glucosamine sulphates (CGS) are considered structure-­
modifying drugs and have been studied in the prevention, delay or reversal of 
structural morphological changes in joints caused by osteoarthritis.
Objective: The aim of the present study was to investigate the action of CGS on 
the progression of chemically induced osteoarthritis in the temporomandibular joint 
(TMJ) of rabbits by evaluating the serum levels of tumour necrosis factor (TNF-­α) and 
collagen in the articular discs.
Materials and Methods: A sample of 36 male rabbits was divided into three groups: 
control (CG), osteoarthritis (OG) and treatment (TG). The disease was induced by 
intra-­articular injection of sodium monoiodoacetate (10 mg/mL) in the OG and TG 
groups bilaterally. After 10 days, the TG animals received subcutaneous injection 
of chondroitin sulphates and glucosamine (7.5 mg/kg) and the OG and CG received 
saline solution (50 μL). Euthanasia times were subdivided into 40 and 100 days. 
Collagen quantification was performed by biochemical and histological analysis and 
for the quantification of serum levels of TNF-­α, an enzyme immunoassay was used.
Results: The TG showed an increase in the collagen area of the articular disc when 
compared to the CG and the OG. The increase collagen concentration in the discs 
did not show a statistically significant difference between the groups. Post-­treatment 
TNF-­α levels were significantly lower in TG compared to OG.
Conclusions: The results indicate that CGS treatment delayed the degeneration of 
the collagen in the TMJ articular disc and reduced serum TNF-­α levels, indicating a 
preventive effect on OA progression.

1  |  INTRODUCTION
Chondroitin and glucosamine sulphates (CGS) are considered slow-­
acting symptomatic drugs (SYSADOAs) that relieve pain and have 
good tolerability and safety. These compounds have been tested as 
structure-­modifying drugs (SMD) that may prevent, delay, or reverse 
morphological structural changes in the joint caused by osteoarthri-
tis (OA).1,2
Temporomandibular joint (TMJ) osteoarthritis leads to cartilage 
and articular disc degradation, affecting chewing and phonation 
functions. The temporomandibular joint disc plays a crucial role as a 
congruent agent, absorber and distributor of stress in the mandibu-
lar cinematic complex.3 Part of its mechanical properties have been 
related to the presence of a highly organised collagen network and a 
sparse amount of glycosaminoglycans (GAGs) in the solid extracellu-
lar matrix (ECM) of articular discs.1,4–7 During an inflammatory pro-
cess, as in OA, an imbalance in cytokines and growth factors present 
in the synovial fluid is observed, favouring the increase of inflamma-
tory cytokines such as tumour necrosis factor (TNF-­α).8 This increase 
induces the synthesis and release of proteases responsible for colla-
gen and proteoglycan depletion, reflecting in the cartilage degrada-
tion observed in osteoarthritis.1
The therapeutic options available for the management of OA are 
basically restricted to the use of anti-­inflammatory drugs and anal-
gesics, but their chronic administration is limited by their deleterious 
systemic effects.9 Moreover, conventional treatments are not sat-
isfactorily effective in controlling OA progression nor in regenerat-
ing damaged cartilage. For this reason, new therapeutic alternatives 
have been investigated for the management of degenerative dis-
eases such as osteoarthritis, seeking not only to control symptoms, 
but also to preserve joint structures and maintain the patient's qual-
ity of life.9–14 In the present study, we investigated the therapeutic 
effect of CGS on the progression of osteoarthritis in the articular 
disc of the temporomandibular joint of rabbits by analysing serum 
levels of TNF-­α and quantifying disc collagen.
2  |  METHOD
2.1  |  Experimental design
Thirty-­six male New Zealand rabbits (Oryctolagus cuniculus L.), 
adults, 4-­months-­old, weighing between 3 and 4 kg, were used. The 
animals were kept under ad libitum feeding based on water, rabbit 
food and leafy greens, in individual cages, with environments under 
12 h light/dark cycle, temperature (21°C ±1) and relative humidity 
between 40% and 60%. This study is in accordance with the Law 
11 794/2008, which establishes the procedures for scientific use of 
animals, according to current legislation in Brazil. Also, it was submit-
ted to the evaluation and approval of the Ethics Committee on the 
Use of Animals in Research (CEUA) of the Hospital de Clínicas de 
Porto Alegre (HCPA) with number 2018–0424.
The rabbits were randomly randomised between the control 
group (CG, n = 12), which received only saline solution injection into 
the TMJ, the osteoarthritis group (OG, n = 12) and the treatment 
group (TG, n = 12), which received an injection of sodium monoiodo-
acetate (MIA) into the TMJ, bilaterally. After 10 days, animals in the 
TG started treatment with subcutaneous injection of chondroitin 
sulphates and glucosamine. The CG and OG animals received saline 
solution by the same route. Each group was subdivided and cata-
logued into three other subgroups, according to the treatment and 
time of death of the rabbits at 40 and 100 days after the induction of 
OA (Figure 1). The times of death of the animals were defined based 
on the studies by Uebelhart et al.15 and Artuzi, et al.1,16
The animals were anaesthetised with a combination of Ketamine 
(20 mg/kg), Meperidine (3 mg/kg) and Midazolam (1 mg/kg), intra-
muscularly. After trichotomy, the zygomatic process was located 
by digital palpation and the extension of this process to the exter-
nal ear canal was marked on the skin with methylene blue. Then, 
5 mm distal and superior to the posterior limit of the zygomatic pro-
cess, the point of introduction of the needle for the infiltration of 
solutions was marked. Finally, a solution containing 0.5 mg of MIA 
(Sigma-­Aldrich, St. Louis, USA) dissolved in 50 μL of saline solution 
(10 mg/mL) was slowly injected into the temporomandibular joint, 
bilaterally, of rabbits in the OG and TG groups. The CG group re-
ceived the same amount of 50 μL of saline solution in the TMJ. The 
osteoarthritis induction model followed the protocol of Artuzi et al.1 
According to the established model, the animals did not present clin-
ical changes indicating suffering, pain or weight loss, and the use of 
analgesic medication was not necessary at this stage.
2.2  |  Treatment
The TG animals received, subcutaneously, an injection of 0.1 mL/kg 
of Chondroton® every 3 days, which corresponds to approximately 
7.5 mg/kg of chondroitin sulphate and 7.5 mg/kg of glucosamine 
sulphate. The administration of this medication was started 10 days 
after the induction of OA. The doses were calculated according to 
the recommended dosage in the drug package insert and according 
to the dose used in studies previously performed by Artuzi et al.1,16 
Due to the probable variation in the weight of the animals and the 
need to adjust the dose of the medication, the animals' weight was 
controlled on a weekly basis. The times 0, 10, 40, and 100 days cor-
responded, respectively, to the time at which OA was induced, treat-
ment started, and the animals died. The animals in the CG and OG 
groups received, subcutaneously, only one volume of saline solution, 
according to their body weight.
To quantify TNF-­α, blood samples (10 mL) were collected from 
all animals, through the lateral auricular vein, at 10, 40 and 100 days. 
The animals were sedated with Ketamine (20 mg/kg), Meperidine 
(3 mg/kg) and Midazolam (1 mg/kg), intramuscularly.

According to the predetermined times (40, 100 days), the animals 
were anaesthetised with an association of Ketamine (20 mg/kg),

2.4  |  Quantification of collagen in the articular disc 
by biochemical analysis
The collagen concentration in the articular discs was determined 
by the Sircol™ Soluble Collagen Assay (Biocolor, UK), following the 
manufacturer's instructions. Disc samples were weighed on a pre-
cision balance and stored in 1.5 mL Eppendorf Protein Lobin tubes. 
For extraction of acid-­pepsin-­soluble collagen, specimens were di-
gested with 0.5 M acetic acid containing 0.1 mg/mL pepsin (P7012; 
Sigma-­Aldrich, USA) at a 1:20 ratio and incubated at 4°C for 48 h. 
The supernatant was removed after centrifugation at 3000 rpm 
for 10 min (Hettich–Universal 320R Centrifuge) and transferred to 
another Eppendorf tube. To each tube of soluble collagen, 1 mL of 
Sircol dye reagent was added and mixed by inversion and gentle 
agitation on a shaker (KJ-­201BS Oscillator) for 30 min and cen-
trifuged at 12000 rpm for 10 min. The precipitated dye-­collagen 
complex was washed and drained with salt-­acid washing reagent. 
Soon after, alkaline reagent was added and mixed by vortexing. 
The absorbance of the samples was measured at 555 nm using a 
microplate reader (Spectra Max® M3–Hexis Scientific). A collagen 
calibration standard, provided in the kit, was used to obtain the 
standard curve (15, 30 and 50 μg). The collagen concentration was 
measured in μg/mg.
2.5  |  Image capture and histological analysis
Sagittal 0.4 μm sections of the temporomandibular joints along 
the postero-­anterior axis, including the anterior, middle, and 
posterior portions, were obtained and stained using PicroSirius 
Red according to the manufacturer's protocol (EasyPath). Images 
were captured with a polarised microscope (Olympus-­BX51TF) 
coupled to a camera device (Olympus, Q-­Colour 5). Settings were 
standardised and captures were taken at 4× magnification, where 
three fields for each sample were selected and images stored in 
TIF format.

Variables were described by mean and standard deviation and compared 
over time and between groups using two-­way Analysis of Variance 
(ANOVA) for analysis of collagen concentration and area of collagen oc-

3  |  RESULTS
3.1  |  Area occupied by collagen in the articular disc
There was a statistically significant effect of group (treatment) 
(F(2,29) = 5.163, p = .014), where it was observed that, regardless of 
the time assessed, there is an increase in the total area of collagen 
(cm2) in the TG when compared to the others, which did not differ 
(Table 1). We also observed a treatment effect, where there was an 
increase in the total collagen area (cm2) in the articular disc of the TG, 
when compared to the others. Furthermore, TG showed histologi-
cally greater preservation of joint structures and better organisation 
of collagen fibres when compared to OG, which showed histological 
signs of disc degeneration and less collagen (Figures 2 and 3). There 
was no time effect in the groups studied (F(1,24) = 0.144, p = .708) or 
interactions between the Group and Time (F(2,24) = 2.051, p = .151).
3.2  |  Collagen concentration (μg/mg)
There was no significant group x time interaction effect 
(F(2,34) = 0.192, p = .826), as shown in Table 1, that is, the behaviour 
of the groups was similar between times. When only the effect of 
time was evaluated (F(2,34) = 13.796, p = .001), a statistically signifi-
cant reduction was observed between the times 40 (15.9 ± 4.7) and 
100 days (10.3 ± 4.0) (p = .001) with the passage of time. Regarding 
the groups, no significant differences were seen between the treat-
ments (F(2,34) = 1.001, p = .380).

4  |  DISCUSSION
In the present study, collagen levels were analysed in the TMJ ar-
ticular disc of rabbits that had chemically induced osteoarthritis and 
were subsequently treated with CGS. The increase in collagen area 
observed in TG compared to OG and CG suggests a preventive ef-
fect of CGS on osteoarthritis progression, since after inflammation 
induction it maintained the quantity and organisation of collagen 
fibres in the articular discs, indicating delayed degeneration of the 
structures. This is the first study to evaluate the structural effect of 
CGS in articulation, observing the collagen composition of the disc, a 
fundamental component for TMJ functionality.
Glucosamine and chondroitin are structural molecules of the 
disc and articular cartilage and are fundamental for the synthesis 
of proteoglycans and glycosaminoglycans (GAGs).17–19 Studies 
show that changes in structure and composition in the disc alter 
its nanomechanical properties and affect its ability to absorb loads 
during daily activities such as chewing, talking, and yawning.3,5,7,20 
In addition, the arrangement of collagen fibres in the articular disc 
influences force distribution, helping to reduce stress concentra-
tion in the directly loaded region.5,6 In 2019, Cui et al, showed that 
the presence of inflammation causes a disarrangement and loos-
ening of the collagen molecule, generating a porosity between its 
bundles, reducing the modulus of elasticity of the inflamed disc 
region and impairing the ability to resist external stress.20 For this 
reason, the maintenance of the organisation of collagen fibres of 
the articular disc has been shown to be as important as its bio-
chemical composition.6 The bioavailability of a substrate for the 
synthesis of proteoglycans and GAGs through CGS supplementa-
tion, while joint tissues are exposed to the inflammatory processes 
of osteoarthritis, would be an interesting alternative, because be-
sides its symptomatic benefits, it would promote a protective ef-
fect of the structures. Moreover, OA treatment with CGS in TMJ 
has shown better effects in reversing degenerative changes than in 
other joints, possibly due to the presence of mesenchymal cells in 
fibrocartilage, evidencing its potential as DME.1,21 In 2020, Bi et al, 
observed the existence of a specific population of stem cells in 
human condyle fibrocartilage, whose chondrogenic potential was 
regulated by SOX9 expression. In patients with TMJ osteoarthritis

activate nuclear factor (NF)-­kB, whose signalling pathway may 
regulate the expression of this gene.2 In the present study, CGS 
were shown to reduce TNF-­α levels even after induction of inflam-
mation, which could indicate their influence in preventing tissue 
degradation by avoiding SOX9 suppression.
To evaluate the collagen concentration between groups, a bio-
chemical analysis was performed and statistically significant dif-
ferences were found between times 40 and 100, where the former 
showed higher collagen concentrations. This can be explained by the 
increased synthesis of ECM components, such as proteoglycans, in 
the early stages of OA as an attempt at tissue repair.22 In relation 
to the groups, although the collagen concentration (μg/mg) of the 
TG100 and TG40 showed higher concentrations when compared to 
OG40 and CG100, the difference was not significant, possibly due to 
the small sample size between the subgroups.
In the present study, TNF-­α levels increased significantly in the OG 
group, which had the disease induced and was treated only with saline 
solution. The same group also showed histological signs of disc degener-
ation and less collagen. This result is in agreement with previous studies 
showing increased levels of TNF-­α in the synovial fluid of patients with 
temporomandibular disorders.17 Moreover, serum levels of TNF-­α after 
treatment with CGS reached levels close to those found in the CG group 
and both were lower than in the OG group, showing that the treatment 
was effective in reversing the increase in TNF-­α in the TG to levels close 
to the pre-­intervention time, suggesting an anti-­inflammatory effect of 
CGS. TNF-­α is considered a protagonist in degenerative processes, be-
cause it acts as an initiating cytokine and activates signalling cascades, 
recruiting immune cells and activating neutrophil migration and the 
expression of proteolytic enzymes.23,24 The positive effects of CGS on 
TNF-­α levels have been reported to modulate inflammation of joint tis-
sues affected by osteoarthritis and stimulate tissue recovery by proteo-
glycan synthesis and inhibition of NF-­kB.1,9,11,15,17,25,26 Activation and 
translocation of NF-­kB is associated with increased expression of pro-
teolytic enzymes and pro-­inflammatory cytokines by chondrocytes and 
synoviocytes, and the anti-­inflammatory effects of CGS are reported to 
interfere with this process.27,28
It is important to point out that the clinical efficacy of CGS is 
still controversial, since the studies are highly heterogeneous, with 
different study designs, dosages, and product presentation, mak-
ing it difficult to reach a precise conclusion about their effects.29 
The routes of administration, oral and intravenous, have differ-
ent bioavailability and the formulations differ between countries, 
where in the USA it is considered a nutritional supplement and 
in some European countries, a medication subject to quality con-
trols.12,25,30,31 On the other hand, the advantages of CGS over other 
medications are: rare gastrointestinal and cardiac adverse reactions, 
as observed in anti-­inflammatory drugs; preservation and recovery 
of joint tissues, improving jaw function, and consequently, postpon-
ing the need for surgical interventions.
It should also be noted that studies in patients are analysed at various 
stages of disease, tissue repair and different ages, with great variability in 
samples. The animal model, as in the present study, allows standardisa-
tion of sample characteristics, such as weight, age and sex, as well as the 
times of disease induction and treatment. Rabbit TMJ is anatomically 
and histologically similar to the human joint. The rabbit condyle is cov-
ered by secondary cartilage and fibrous tissue. Furthermore, similar dis-
tribution of collagen and proteoglycans is observed. The most notable 
morphological difference between rabbit and human TMJ is the shape 
of the articular surface of the condyle and the retrodiscal area, since the 
animals do not have a posterior glenoid wall, which facilitates access and 
manipulation for experiments without the need for surgical access. For 
this reason, the rabbit is a good animal model for experimental studies 
of human TMJ diseases and therapies.16,32
In conclusion, the present study showed that CGS can influence 
the treatment of OA in rabbit TMJ, reducing the serum levels of 
TNF-­α and increasing the amount of collagen in the articular discs, 
indicating a preventive and anti-­inflammatory effect. However, fur-
ther studies that are TMJ-­focused and have similar experimental 
protocols are needed to corroborate the effects of CGS.
