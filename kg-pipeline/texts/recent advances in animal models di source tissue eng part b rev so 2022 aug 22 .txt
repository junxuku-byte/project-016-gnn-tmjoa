Abstract 
Temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJOA) is a gradual degenerative jaw joint 
condition. Until recent years, TMJOA is still relatively unrecognized and ineffective to be 
treated. Appropriate animal models with reliable detection methods can help researchers 
understand the pathophysiology of TMJOA and find therapeutic options. Here, we 
summarized common animal models of TMJOA created by chemical, surgical, mechanical, 
and gentical appraoches. The relevant pathological symptoms and induction mechanisms 
were outlined. In addition, different pathological indicators  Furthermore, emerging 
therapeutic regimens, such as intra-articular drug delivery and tissue engineering-based 
approaches to treat TMJOA based on these animal models, were summarized and 
updated. Understanding the physiology and pathogenesis of the TMJOA, together using 
various ways to diagnose the TMJOA were elaborated, including imaging techniques, 
molecular techniques for detecting inflammatory cytokines, histochemical staining and 
histomorphometry measures.with a more reliable diagnosis, will enable the development 
of new prevention and more effective treatment strategies and thereby improve the life 
quality of TMJOA patients.  
Keywords 
Temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis, Animal model, Diagnose, Treatment, New 
methods

1. Introduction 
The temporomandibular joint (TMJ) is involved in mastication, swallowing, breathing, 
speaking, and other common activities of daily living. TMJ osteoarthritis (OA) is a gradual 
degenerative condition in the jaw joint, and affects 8 to 16 percent of the population 
worldwide, with an annual health expense of more than $80 billion.1,2 Orofacial pain, joint 
noises, and limited jaw movement are typical symptoms.  
The typical pathological changes of TMJOA are progressive cartilage deterioration, 
subchondral bone remodeling, and synovitis. The etiology of TMJOA is complex and multi-
factorial. The root cause of TMJOA is assumed to be mechanical overload on joint 
components that surpasses physiological resistance, such as excessive loading on healthy 
articular cartilage or normal loading on the damaged articular cartilage, which disrupts 
cartilage matrix homeostasis and leads to TMJOA.3,4  
Although hundreds of researches have looked into the mechanisms that cause TMJOA, it is 
still poorly understood due to various pathogenic variables and clinical presentations. 
Different animal models have been developed to study the pathological causes of TMJOA. 
These models help elucidate the mechanics of TMJOA and evaluate therapeutic efficacy 
even though they cannot reflect all clinical features.5   
A reliable diagnosis of TMJOA is recognized as essential to confirm the successful 
establishment of relevant animal models and follow-up treatment. The radiographic 
characteristics of the condyle or articular eminences, such as bone erosive degradation, 
osteophyte formation, and cyst-like alterations, are frequently used to diagnose TMJOA in 
the clinic.6,7 Pathological diagnosis of TMJOA based on histochemical staining and 
histomorphometry measurements and molecular techniques for detecting various 
inflammatory factors were developed recently to assess the success of animal models and 
the efficacy of novel therapeutic approaches.  
Several treatments are currently available to alleviate symptoms of TMJOA.8 These 
therapies efficiently reduce signs and symptoms in most cases, but their therapy effect on 
the degenerative cartilage and subchondral bone is insufficient. Emerging therapeutic

2. Animal Models of TMJOA 
Animal models of TMJOA are essential for researching the pathophysiology and testing the 
efficacy of novel therapy options.9 Various attempts have been carried out to produce 
TMJOA animal models, including naturally occurring, chemical induction, surgical 
operation, mechanical stimulation, and genetically modified models. In this section, we 
summarized the different TMJOA animal models according to the different causes of 
TMJOA (Table 1& Figure1). 
2.1 Naturally occurring models of TMJOA 
As a degenerative disease, the prevalence of TMJOA rises with age. Zheng et al10 found a 
naturally occurring TMJOA model using aging mice. In their investigation, 60-week-old 
mice showed degeneration and decreased bone volume in cartilage and more TRAP-
positive cells in the subchondral bone. Besides, Ukita et al11 also found exposed 
proliferative layer in the damaged TMJ cartilage of the 20-month-old mice. Using the aging 
mice with spontaneous TMJOA can help researchers to study the pathological relationship 
between aging and cartilage and subchondral bone lesions in TMJOA.

acetate (MIA), vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF), collagenase, and formalin, are 
commonly used to create TMJ inflammatory models. Most chemical induction methods 
centered on the inflammatory part of TMJOA. They appear to be straightforward to

2.2.1 Chemical Induction by CFA and MIA 
CFA contains heat-killed mycobacterium tuberculosis, which is thought to produce acute 
inflammation. MIA causes chondrocyte death by decreasing the activity of glyceraldehyde-
3-phosphate dehydrogenase. Cui et al12 investigated the alterations of condylar cartilage 
and subchondral bone in CFA-induced inflammation in rats. Reduced cartilage thickness, 
fewer chondrocytes, and decreased proteoglycan expression were found in the articular 
cartilage of inflammatory TMJs. The RANKL/OPG ratios in the cartilage and subchondral 
bone were also found to increase during the CFA injection. Jiang et al injected 50 μl of MIA 
(4 mg/kg) into the upper compartment of the TMJ of rats. They discovered that the rats' 
condylar structure degraded over time, decreasing chondrocyte quantity and percentage 
of proteoglycan area in cartilage.13 Wang et al investigated rats after injecting different 
doses of MIA for 12 weeks. They discovered that injecting 0.5 mg or more MIA into the 
TMJ resulted in OA-like lesions in 4 weeks. Condylar breakdown progressed throughout 
time, beginning with chondrocyte death and progressing through cartilage matrix 
disorganization, subchondral bone erosion, and osteophyte development.14 By changing its 
concentration, the MIA-induced model of TMJOA has the benefit of being simple to modify 
the severity of the joint lesions.  
2.2.2 Chemical Induction by VEGF and collagenase 
VEGF is a pro-angiogenic growth factor and implicated in the breakdown of articular 
cartilage and the progression of TMJOA by activating osteoclasts. Subchondral bone 
destruction, noticeable cartilage degradation, and subchondral bone resorption with a 
substantial number of osteoclasts were found in VEGF-injected mice. The results indicate 
that VEGF can cause TMJOA by causing cartilage and subchondral bone destruction.15 
TMJOA was also generated by intra-articular injection of reagents to degrade or inactivate 
collagen. This model was induced by injecting 12.5 μl of collagenase into the upper cavity 
of mice TMJ. Li et al. discovered that the mice's joints had an uneven surface, and 
inflammation increased dramatically.16 In a rat model, formalin has also been used to

2.3 Surgical Operation of TMJOA 
Disc perforation, disc displacement, discectomy and condylectomy are surgical procedures 
for modeling TMJOA. Rather than rats and mice, larger animals such as rabbits, lambs, and 
monkeys are used in these animal models.  
2.3.1 TMJOA by Disc Perforation 
The rabbit is frequently used as a disc perforation model. Embree et al. produced a 2.5 mm 
defect in rabbits via a unilateral TMJ disc puncture operation. They discovered condylar 
cartilage degeneration and heterotopic ossification in experimental condyles after disc 
perforation. Their findings showed that disc perforation could result in TMJOA and 
aberrant mandibular development.18  
2.3.2 TMJOA by Disc Deformation  
Extra-articular surgery was used by Liu et al to unilaterally move the rabbits’ jaw in a 
posterosuperior direction, resulting in joint disc deformation. Synovial hyperplasia and 
adhesion in joints, as well as a significant deformation of the joint disc, were discovered in 
the early postoperative phase. The authors concluded TMJOA by this method 
recapitulated typical OA pathological manifestations.19  
2.3.3 TMJOA by Disc Displacement 
Anterior disc displacement is widely recognized as one of the causes of TMJOA, and it is 
used in animal surgeries as a surgical model. Adult rabbits were surgically induced with 
anterior disc displacement by Xu et al. After exposing the zygomatic arch suture, a hole 
was created under the anterior of the zygomatic arch. The disc's anterior portion was then 
sewed to the hole. In this way, increased chondrocyte death resulting in a reduction in cell 
density was observed after surgical disc displacement.20

2.3.4 TMJOA by Discectomy 
Furthermore, a partial discectomy was performed on one side of the TMJs to create 
TMJOA mice. Cohen et al21 found degeneration in articular cartilage after 4 weeks. 
Reduced proteoglycan at 12 weeks and articular cartilage loss at 16 weeks were observed. 
These findings revealed that the mice with partial discectomy led to early-onset TMJOA-
like articular cartilage degeneration.  
2.3.5TMJOA by Condylectomy 
Condylectomy is also a surgical procedure to induce TMJOA. Vapniarsky et al22 performed 
condylectomy in minipigs, they found the operation induced subchondral cysts and large 
osteophytes formation with the extensive irregular cartilage surface. Besides, Gomez et 
al23 used the same way to create TMJOA mice. They revealed that condylectomy caused 
discontinuous cartilage and bone loss, without cartilage regeneration and extracellular 
matrix formation. 
2.4 Mechanical Stimulation of TMJOA 
It has been proven that disrupted occlusion can cause a masticatory system imbalance, 
which can be an approach to creat TMJOA. Bite raising and malocclusion are two common 
ways in which altered loading can be generated in animal models. These methods are 
affordable, efficient, and repeatable to induce TMJOA mice models (Table 2).  
2.4.1 Stimulation of TMJOA by Malocclusion 
Another prevalent method to create TMJOA is unbalanced malocclusion. Zhang et al24 and 
Sun et al25 bonded a metal tube to the rat’s left maxillary incisor and another metal tube to 
the left mandibular incisor to create an aberrant dental occlusion. Meanwhile, Jiao et al26 
mesially relocated the first and third molars of the left maxilla and distally relocated the 
first and third molars of the right mandible. These two models led to the subchondral bone 
and cartilage deterioration in the mandibular condyle and increased chondrocyte death. 
These two models can also cause chondrocyte mortality and OA-like lesions in rat TMJ 
cartilage.

2.4.2 Stimulation of TMJOA by Bite Raising 
Given that the bite raising can lead to TMJOA, Matias et al and Jung et al have developed 
two different models. By placing a U-shaped stainless steel wire on the molar teeth, Matias 
et al27 established a bite-raising animal model. Meanwhile, Jung et al28 developed an 
animal model by building up resin on the left maxillary molars of rats. Both of them 
observed the development of TMJOA with a decreased thickness of the condylar cartilage 
and increased apoptosis in the cartilage and subchondral bone.  
2.5 Gene Modification of TMJOA 
One major step forward in creating TMJOA animal models was the development of gene 
editing techniques, which allowed for targeted modification of the animal's genome. These 
animals are useful for investigating the function of a speciﬁc gene and its interaction with 
other components in the temporomandibular joint. According to a recent study, the short 
stature homeobox 2 (Shox2) gene plays a crucial role in TMJ development. TMJ defects 
and premature articular disc erosion, symptoms known as TMJOA, are shown in Shox2-
depleted mice[29]. Besides, Proteoglycan 4 (Prg4)-null mice model30 showed abnormal 
cartilage, osteophytes formation in the glenoid fossa. Discoidin domain receptor (DDR)-1 
knockout mice31 had bone surface fissures, proteoglycan degradation, chondrocyte cluster 
development, and other classic TMJOA histology findings.  
3. Diagnosis of TMJOA 
It is critical to use various pathological indexes to diagnose TMJOA for assessing the 
efficacy of TMJOA models and evaluating the efficacy of innovative treatments. Imaging 
techniques including micro-CT and MRI, molecular techniques to detect inflammatory 
cytokines, histochemical staining, and histomorphometry measures are the most used 
diagnosis approaches in animal studies.

form, and bone mineral density (BMD) were generally found in the TMJOA condylar

concomitant pro-inflammatory cytokines such as TNF-α, IL-1β, and several other cytokines 
were found to be up-regulated in TMJOA. Inflammation causes an increase in these

3.3 Diagnosis of TMJOA by Histochemical Staining and Histomorphometry Measures 
According to numerous research, synovitis, lower cartilage thickness, fewer chondrocytes, 
and down-regulated proteoglycan expression have all been reported in the condylar 
cartilage in TMJOA models. Increased osteoclast activity and larger bone marrow cavity 
were found in the inflammatory TMJ, with immature new bones, uneven trabecular bone 
structure, and unusual condylar shape. These histomorphological changes can be 
measured by histological stainings.  
Hematoxylin & Eosin (HE) staining revealed that in TMJOA models, synovial cells had 
evident hyperplasia, and the inflammatory synovium contained numerous mononuclear 
cells and lipid droplets. In addition, the number of chondrocytes and the thickness of the 
condylar cartilage were decreased.33 Proteoglycan alterations in the cartilage matrix can 
be determined by T.B. and S.O staining, and proteoglycan amounts were all significantly 
reduced in the TMJOA models18,14 (Figure 2E). The change in trabecula bone shape and the 
degree of bone mineralization were observed using Masson trichrome staining[53]. Through 
it, irregular and disordered trabecular bone structures and large marrow cavities can be 
observed in the TMJOA models.14 The osteoclast activity was estimated by tartrate-
resistant acid phosphatase (TRAP) staining33,54, and the chondrocytes apoptosis can also be 
examined in situ using a TUNEL assay.55 Wang et al56 reported that following TMJOA 
induction, TUNEL-positive chondrocytes were identified diffusely in the corresponding 
region. In addition, the upregulation of cartilage degradation markers involved in the 
pathology of TMJOA such as VEGF, matrix metalloproteinase (MMP)-3, MMP-9, and bone 
morphogenetic protein (BMP)-7, can be measured by histochemical stainings in the 
cartilage matrix of TMJOA57-61 (Figure 2F).

4. Conservative Treatment of TMJOA 
TMJOA treatment aims to inhibit cartilage and subchondral bone deterioration, stimulate 
condyle remodeling, reduce joint discomfort, and restore TMJ functions.62 Manual 
movement of the TMJ63 and splints have been frequently applied to relieve muscular 
strain, rectify mandibular malalignment, and reduce pain.64,65 Pharmacological treatments 
such as NSAIDs, muscle relaxants, corticosteroids, and antidepressants are also used to 
treat TMJOA pain. However, they are commonly connected to side effects such as nausea 
and dizziness.66 If conservative therapy fails, simple non-surgical therapies such as 
arthrocentesis, arthroscopy, and hyaluronic acid (HA) injections might be used to alleviate 
TMJOA symptoms.67-69 HA can restore the viscoelasticity of the synovial fluid in an 
inflamed joint and has lubrication, shock absorption, and joint protection effects.70 
However, the half-life of HA is short, leading difficulty in sustaining a long-term therapeutic 
efficacy. 
5. Advanced Treatment of TMJOA 
Although the conserved treatments listed above can slow disease progression to some 
extent, they are unable to completely heal the degraded cartilage or subchondral bone 
defects, as well as degenerative disc disease. These lesions frequently cause severe 
symptoms, including pain, edema, and dysfunction. Aiming this, novel drug delivery 
systems and tissue engineering therapies to retore the TMJ tissues gained increasing 
attention.  
5.1 Treatment of TMJOA By Novel Delivery Systems of Therapeutic Agents 
The half-life of most intra-TMJ injectable medications in the tissue is short, causing rapid 
clearance from the site of injection and multiple injections to boost the therapeutic 
effects. Controlled-release drug delivery systems can address these difficulties by 
providing medications in a regulated spatiotemporal manner which have been designed 
for the TMJOA treatments.

5.2 Treatment of TMJOA By Tissue Engineering Strategies  
Due to the low regenerative capacity of the condyle, current treatments for TMJOA are 
mostly symptomatic relief targeted and lack long-term efficacy. Lately, there has been a 
surging interest in cellular therapy for severe kinds of TMJOA, such as disc thinning, disc 
perforation, and condyle osteochondral destruction. Bioactive components, including cells 
and growth factors, are commonly used in such regeneration therapy systems.75-77

Ahtiainen et al. implanted polylactide discs, embedded with ADMSCs to the rabbits’ 
defective TMJ discs.82 They found ADMSCs promoted cartilaginous tissue formation and 
bone regeneration in the TMJ. Moreover, a recently published study in 2020 reported that 
stromal vascular fraction (SVF), a component of lipoaspirate that contained 32% ADMSCs, 
is more feasible in cellular therapy for TMJOA.83 The SVF reduced inflammatory cytokines, 
released by the synoviocytes from TMJOA tissue. Given the minimal manipulation and low 
cost84, SVF is worth being more investigated as a new cell source to treat TMJOA in the 
future. Besides, mesenchymal stem cells from oral tissues, including dental pulp stem cells 
(DPSCs), periodontal ligament stem cells (PDLSCs), and deciduous teeth stem cells (DTSCs), 
have been proposed as promising cells in the regeneration systems.12,55,85 In an 
investigation by Cui et al, they injected the DPSCs into the articular cavity of the rat to 
treat the TMJOA induced by CFA injection. They found that local injection of DPSCs can 
relieve inflammatory pain, decrease cartilage degradation, induce bone regeneration and 
downregulate the expression levels of TNF-α, MMP3, and MMP1.12 Induced pluripotent 
stem cells (iPSCs) are additional possible stem cell sources. iPSCs have potential in 
regenerative medicine because of their ability to be induced into various cell lineages 
including chondrocytes and they are non-invasively harvested and can be transplanted 
autologously without immune rejection.86 Although the use of iPSCs in TMJ disc tissue

5.2.2 Scaffolds in Tissue Engineering Strategies for TMJOA 
Cell injection alone showed a low retention rate of transplanted cells in the joint due to 
severe anoikis and hypoxia.89-91 Besides, the TMJ disc is under high mechanical stress and 
in motion. Therefore, scaffolds with adequate mechanical strength and long-term stability 
to ensure the high retention of transplanted cells are desired for cell-based tissue-
engineered TMJ constructs.   
Chitosan, alginate, collagen, and fibrin are the natural materials used in TMJ disc scaffolds. 
Chitosan shares structural similarities with numerous naturally occurring 
glycosaminoglycans found in cartilage.92 Alginate hydrogel characteristics can be designed 
to control stem cell fate and activity.93 Bousnaki et al developed a kind of chitosan/alginate 
(Ch/Alg) scaffold embedded with DPSCs. They found the scaffold promoted DPSCs 
adhesion, viability, and proliferation, ultimately resulting in a storage modulus comparable 
to the human TMJ discs.94 Collagen is a major component of native discs. In an 
investigation by Wang et al95, the discs of rabbits regenerated and the joint's normal 
function was restored when the natural disc was removed by discectomy and 
subsequently replaced with reconstituted collagen templates embedded with ADMSCs. 
Moreover, Chen et al96 used decellularized bovine distal femurs as scaffolds seeded with 
ADMSCs to regenerate the full-thickness cartilage in minipig joints after ramus-condyle 
unit ectomy (Figure 3B).

5.2.3 Growth Factors in Tissue Engineering Strategies for TMJOA  
Soluble chemicals or growth factors are frequently elaborated to improve the biological 
characteristics of cells. For example, NEL-like molecule-1 (NELL-1) was employed to 
promote the chondrogenic differentiation of BMMSCs during pre-treatment.104 Zhu et al 
observed that the NELL-1-modified BMMSCs showed more vigorous and well-organized 
fibrocartilage regeneration than those without NELL-1.104 The transforming growth factors 
(TGF)-family82,98,105 and connective tissue growth factor (CTGF) can control the growth and 
function of the imported cells and newly formed tissues.106 In a study by Tarafder et al98, 
CTGF and TGF-β3 were delivered with MSCs and PCL scaffolds. Compared to the control 
group, the experiment group significantly prevented arthritic changes of TMJ condyles, 
increased fibrocartilaginous recovery, and promoted multiphase fibrocartilaginous 
tissue formation in a disc perforation model. Moreover, platelet derivative growth factor 
(PDGF), basic fibroblast growth factor (bFGF), and insulin-like growth factor 1 (IGF-1)107 
were reported having the capacity to boost the proliferation rate of TMJ-disc derived cells, 
glycosaminoglycans formation, and collagen and hyaluronic acid synthesis108 (Table 3) . 
6. Limitations and Future Perspectives 
TMJOA is difficult to be treated in the clinic due to the unique anatomy of TMJ and the 
complex etiology of this disease. Despite that different TMJOA models have been 
developed and specific mechanisms were revealed, each model has its advantages and 
shortcomings, and the majority of current models are single-factor models that do not 
adequately depict the etiology and progression of TMJOA. Therefore, it is feasible to 
create different multi-factor animal models for various situations, depending on the 
research purpose for investigating the neurobiological and molecular causes of TMJOA.5

Conventional detection methods, including imaging techniques, molecular techniques for 
detecting inflammatory cytokines, histochemical stainings and histomorphometry 
measures, are mostly used to diagnose TMJOA. These methods, however, are not specific 
and efficient in detecting TMJOA. For instance, inflammation also happens due to the 
infection in the temporal space, which is adjacent to the TMJ. Besides, the histological 
staining protocols vary in different labs, and the staining results are not objective, which 
may influence the diagnostic accuracy. Also, the conventional methods to diagnose TMJOA 
cannot be conducted in a high-throughput manner. In recent years, novel methods have 
been developed to detect diseases with high accuracy and specificity. For example, 
fluorine 18 fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG) is easily taken up by metabolically active 
inflammatory cells.116 Positron emission tomography (PET) identified increased uptake of 
FDG in periarticular lesions of patients with active symptoms, demonstrating it to be a 
more sensitive alternative to Micro-CT in detecting arthritic changes in the condyle. On the 
other hand, single-cell sequencing (SCS) can be used to obtain the information on 
genomes, transcriptomes, and epigenomes of individual cells, allowing researchers to 
identify new or rare cell types and compare healthy and disease-related tissues at single-
cell resolution. Therefore, new diagnostic techniques such as SCS can be well-developed to 
diagnose TMJOA more specifically and efficiently. 
According to this literature review, significant progress has been made in cell-based 
therapy of the TMJOA. However, several challenging issues have yet to be resolved. One of 
the most significant challenges in cell-based therapy is the insufficient understanding of 
therapeutic mechanisms and the difficulty in maintaining the precise and prolonged 
persistence of implanted cells in the TMJ. More recent studies have noted that secretomes 
by implanted cells89, particularly exosomes, play a significant role in cell-based 
regenerative constructs. Several reports suggest that exosomes produced from stem cells 
trigger the migration of progenitor cells, promote cartilage and bone regeneration, and 
pain reduction in TMJOA animal models.55 Therefore, exosomes with advanced delivery 
systems can be a more potential and novel strategy for treating TMJOA.

7. Conclusion 
Appropriate animal models can help researchers better understand the pathophysiology of 
TMJOA and find therapeutic options. The currently developed models and diagnostic 
methods have their benefits and limitations. In the future, it is necessary to induce 
different multi-factor animal models and clinical symptoms relevant models with advanced 
diagnostic techniques of higher accuracy and specificity to depict the neurobiological and 
molecular causes of TMJOA. The limitations of current TMJOA treatments inspire us to 
develop new drug delivery systems and tissue engineering strategies to treat TMJOA. 
However, several challenging issues have yet to be resolved. Increasing the effectiveness 
of cell-based therapy and tissue engineering products and understanding more about the 
therapeutic mechanisms will be the focus of future investigations on the treatment of 
TMJOA.
