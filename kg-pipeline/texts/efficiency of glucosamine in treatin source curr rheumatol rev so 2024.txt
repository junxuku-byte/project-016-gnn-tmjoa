Abstract: Background: Temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJ OA) is a chronic disease
that is a consequence of undue occlusal forces and is characterized by irreversible damage to the
articular surfaces. Symptomatic slow-acting so-called nutraceutical drugs have been proposed as a
treatment for osteoarthritis in comparison to non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs).
Oral glucosamine and chondroitin, slow-acting drugs, have been found to reduce pain and in-
crease mouth opening in patients with TMJ OA. However, there is limited scientific evidence to
confirm their clinical effectiveness.
Aim: This systematic review was conducted to bolster the evidence supporting the assessment of
the efficacy of glucosamine in the context of temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJ OA).
Methodology:  This  review  identified  four  review  articles  from  databases  like  Medline  (via
PubMed), Web of Science, Scopus, and EMBASE till September 2023 after screening at the title,
abstract, and full-text level. They were assessed for risk of bias with the JBI risk of bias assess-
ment tool.
Results: This review with meta-analysis focused on pooled estimate mean differences, revealing
non-significant  but  discernible  effects  of  glucosamine  on  maximum  mouth  opening  (SMD  =
0.288, p = 0.15) and pain reduction (SMD = 0.217, p = 0.476) in TMJ-related disorders.
Conclusion:  Compared  to  control  groups  with  ibuprofen  and  tramadol,  glucosamine  showed
slightly more favourable outcomes. However, the variability in methodology and study characteris-
tics warrants further longitudinal studies to confirm its efficacy.
Keywords: Osteoarthritis, glucosamine, systematic review, temporomandibular joint, splint therapies, chronic disease.
1. INTRODUCTION
Osteoarthritis (OA) is a chronic, prevalent disease that
mostly  affects  joints  that  bear  weight,  such  as  the  knees.
When  the  dental  specialty  is  concerned,  the  temporo-
mandibular joint with increased loading was more prone to
osteoarthritis for a few decades. Temporomandibular disor-
ders are currently recognised as a major cause of non-dental
pain in the orofacial region [1]. It is  typified  by  a  gradual

osteophytes and calcium deposition. Conventional treatment
includes  analgesics  or  non-steroidal  anti-inflammatory
drugs; however, lifestyle changes should also be recommend-
ed,  such  as  weight  reduction  and  specific  exercises.  Glu-
cosamine  and  chondroitin,  not  classified  as  a  drug,  are
classed as nutraceuticals, often self-administered by patients
with OA [2]. Both agents are produced endogenously in the
human body and are essential components of cartilage.
Since glucosamine was more frequently used in practice
than chondroitin in many of the literature articles, our re-
view  prioritised  glucosamine  over  chondroitin  for  assess-

cans by acting as a substrate for glycosaminoglycan synthe-
sis [3]. Oral glucosamine is one of the symptomatic slow-act-
ing drugs for osteoarthritis. Glucosamine may inhibit the pro-
duction or activity of inflammatory molecules, such as cy-
tokines (e.g., interleukin-1β, tumour necrosis factor-α) and
prostaglandins, thereby reducing inflammation and its detri-
mental effects on joint tissues [4] Matrix metalloproteinases
(MMPs) are enzymes that degrade extracellular matrix com-
ponents, including collagen and proteoglycans, in joint tis-
sues. Excessive MMP activity contributes to cartilage break-
down and inflammation in osteoarthritis. Glucosamine has
been reported to inhibit MMP production or activity, thereby
helping to preserve the structural integrity of cartilage and re-
duce inflammation within the joints. Alongside, it has been
shown to possess antioxidant properties, scavenging ROS
and reducing oxidative damage to joint tissues [5].
Based on the literature, there is not enough scientific evi-
dence to unambiguously confirm the clinical effectiveness
of glucosamine supplements in the treatment of temporo-
mandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJ OA). The most impor-
tant aspect affecting oral glucosamine's clinical efficacy in
treating TMJ OA was the total administration time. Adminis-
tration  of  oral  glucosamine  for  a  longer  period,  i.e.,  3
months, led to a significant reduction in TMJ pain and a sig-
nificant increase in maximum mouth opening [6]. It also re-
sulted  in  long-term  anti-inflammatory  effects  within  the
TMJs.
Research examining the effectiveness of glucosamine in
temporomandibular  joint  osteoarthritis,  especially  in  the
form of systematic reviews (SRs), has grown considerably
over the past decade. A network meta-analysis examining
non-invasive  pharmacological  approaches  for  temporo-
mandibular joint (TMJ) disorders highlighted the favourable
outcomes associated with glucosamine-based management
strategies. While the comparative efficacy did not reach sta-
tistical significance, glucosamine demonstrated potential as
an effective intervention for TMJ disorders, contributing pos-
itively to prognosis and symptom alleviation [7]. However,
the effectiveness of existing evidence remains inconclusive,
and to our knowledge, no comprehensive literature has been
conducted like any umbrella review. Hence, the objective of
this study was to perform a systematic review of systematic
reviews of randomized controlled trials along with quantita-
tive data synthesis to evaluate the efficiency of glucosamine
specifically in the context of temporomandibular joint os-
teoarthritis.
2. MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1. Review Question
This systematic review was conducted for the following
research question: “Does glucosamine have any influence in
improvising the functional parameters of patients with tem-
poromandibular joint osteoarthritis in comparison with any
other drugs/ placebo?”. The study protocol can be accessed
through the International Prospective Register of Systematic
Reviews, the PROSPERO database, with the following regis-
ter number: CRD42023472857.
2.2. Inclusion and Exclusion Criteria
The eligibility criteria were decided considering the fol-
lowing PICOS definition:
• (P) Population: Patients with osteoarthritis in Tem-
poromandibular joint.
• (I) Intervention: Glucosamine was used for the treat-
ment of osteoarthritis.
• (C) Comparison: Other drugs or placebo.
• (O) Outcomes: Increase in function.
• (S) Study design: Systematic reviews of Randomized
controlled trials (RCT).
Original studies that addressed the “Subjects Interven-
tion Control Outcome” parameters as per PICOS were eligi-
ble for inclusion. Systematic reviews of ex-vivo cell studies,
case reports/series, and observational studies were all exclud-
ed.
2.2.1. Type of the Study
This review included reviews of human randomized ex-
perimental trials, comparing the effect of glucosamine with
any other placebo or positive control in improving the func-
tional parameters of the oral cavity.
2.3. Search Strategy for Article Identification
Two  independent  researchers  conducted  an  electronic
search for the systematic reviews via four databases, namely
Medline (via PubMed), Web of Science, Scopus, and EM-
BASE, till September 2023. The search strategy for each
database is summarised in Table 1. Only language-related
limits were applied to the search, and only publications pub-
lished  in  English  were  taken  into  consideration  for  this
study.

2.4. Screening of Articles
Upon pooling the articles from various databases, consid-
ering the similarities in title, author, and year of publication,
the duplicates were removed. The titles and abstract level of
screening  were  performed  independently  by  two  authors
(S.S. and P.K.). Studies that failed to abide by the specified
PICOS criteria were excluded following the author's agree-
ment. Disagreements related to the selection of articles were
resolved via discussion and consultation with a fourth author
(G.S). Then, the full text of the eligible articles was screened
individually by two authors (S.S. and P.K), and the disagree-
ment was resolved via discussion.
2.5. Data Extraction
For all studies that fulfilled the eligibility criteria, data
extraction was performed by two authors (S.S and P.K) with
the parameters in concordance with the Joanna Briggs Insti-
tute  (JBI)  methodological  guide  for  umbrella  review  [8],
which  included:  (a)  author  and  year,  (b)  study  name,  (c)
study setting, (d) participants, (e) sources searched, (f) test
group, (g) comparison group, (h) range of years included, (i)
number of studies included in a review, (j) appraisal instru-
ment, (k) outcomes assessed, (l) results/ findings, (m) signifi-
cance direction, (o) heterogeneity, (p) period of evaluation,
and (q) inference, these are tabulated in Tables 2 and 3.
2.6. Risk of Bias
The  studies  were  independently  assessed  for  Risk  of
Bias  (ROB)  using  the  JBI  systematic  review  critical  ap-
praisal  tool  [9]  by  two  review  authors,  and  disagreement
among them was resolved by the fourth author (G.S).
2.7. Effect Measure and Statistical Analysis
Comprehensive Meta-Analysis software version 3.0, Bio-
stat Inc. USA [10] was used for meta-analysis of selective
studies which has outcomes comparable to those of systemat-
ically reviewed systematic review articles. In our analysis,
we drew upon quantitative pooled estimate values from two
of  the  four  systematic  review  studies  included  in  our  re-
search. These pooled estimates represent mean differences
in  key  clinical  parameters,  specifically  maximum  mouth
opening and pain reduction. The random effect model was
selected because of the expected heterogeneity between the
included studies. Statistical heterogeneity was estimated us-
ing I
2 analyses, interpreting an I
2 value greater than 50% as a
significant  level  of  heterogeneity  [11].  Statistical  signifi-
cance was determined as p < 0.05 for all analyses.
3. RESULTS
3.1. Study Selection and its Characteristics
The two authors (S.S. and P.K.) were independently in-
volved in the study selection. After screening at the title and
abstract level, followed by full-text screening based on the
inclusion and exclusion criteria, four articles were finally
processed  for  data  extraction  (Fig.  1).  Based  on  the  JBI
methodological guide, the data extracted are tabulated in Ta-
bles 2 and 3. Table 3 includes the summary of outcome mea-
sures from the studies included in the review. The predomi-
nant studies evaluating TMJ-related complaints were in the
functional parameters of maximum mouth opening limit and
pain during its action. The predominant geographical loca-
tions where the systematic reviews were conducted are from
the USA and Europe. The mean period of administration of
glucosamine varies from 2.3 months to a maximum of 6.18
months.
3.2. Main Outcome of the Study
Among the 4 systematic reviews included in our review,
only one study by Ruiz-Romero et al., 2022 [12] performed
a quantitative meta-analysis result with 3 RCTs included in
it, which suggests a noteworthy upward trend in the improvi-
sation of the mouth opening function. However, there was
no effect on pain relief from glucosamine administration.
Out of all the review articles that were included, only
one [6] that was published recently had the most articles (n
=  8)  to  produce  the  evidence.  The  maximum  mean  fol-
low-up period was 6.8 months, and this suggests that there
was progress in both pain reduction and mouth-opening func-
tion.

In a study by Melo et al., 2018 [13], it was mentioned

A Cochrane review involving numerous pharmacologi-

taken on this topic of interest, which collectively support the
usage of glucosamine as an adjunct to conventional mea-
sures in the management of TMJ disorders.
3.3. Risk of Bias
The JBI critical appraisal tool for systematic review was
used to assess the risk of bias of the individual systematic re-
views included in this review.
In summary, 11 criteria were considered for the assess-
ment of ROB on the nominal scale of three sections: yes (p-
resent), no (absent), and not applicable. Among the 4 studies
evaluated for ROB, predominantly all the evaluated criteria
were present in all the included studies. The sole criterion
that is not applicable to the included studies was the likeli-
hood  of  measuring  the  publication  bias,  the  reason  being
that a minimum of three studies would be needed and few pa-
pers would include just qualitative evidence synthesis. Thus,
the risk of bias can be considered low with all the included
studies (Table 4).

4. DISCUSSION
The present review considers the systematic reviews of
randomized controlled trials that evaluated the influence of
glucosamine in TMJ osteoarthritis. Glucosamine hydrochlo-
ride (GH) may decrease inflammation and pain by inhibiting
the synthesis of some joint-degenerating products, such as
prostaglandin E2 and interleukin- 1b, and thus may also pre-
vent cartilage damage [14]. Chondroitin is a glycosaminogly-
can and a major proteoglycan in joint cartilage. It can main-
tain viscosity in joints and stimulate cartilage repair and col-
lagen synthesis by inhibiting lysosomal enzymes [15]. Al-
though NSAIDs and some lifestyle changes were commonly
advised for patients with TMJ osteoarthritis, glucosamine,
and chondroitin, classed as over-the-counter supplements or
nutraceuticals,  are  regularly  self-administered  by  patients
with OA. It is customary in a few other nations as well to
prescribe these as supplemental measures to complement tra-
ditional medical care. Glucosamine hydrochloride is the first
glucosamine product to be licensed as a medicine in the UK
for symptomatic relief of mild to moderate osteoarthritis of
the  knee.  Patients  with  osteoarthritis  widely  use  glu-
cosamine as a nutraceutical in the USA and as a pharmaceu-
tical in Europe [16]. Table 2 depicts this in a clearer way:
most of the systematic studies we included were conducted
mostly in European and American nations where using glu-
cosamine to treat osteoarthritis is more common.
There were numerous controlled trials evaluating glu-
cosamine  (oral,  intramuscular,  or  intravenous  administra-
tion) in comparison with placebo in patients with knee OA.

Still, those results cannot be replicated similarly for TMJ dis-
orders because the articular surface of TMJ consists of dense
fibrous connective tissue (fibrocartilage) when compared to
the hyaline cartilaginous articular surfaces of other synovial
joints [17]. The first clinical trial on evaluating the effects of
glucosamine on TMJ disorders, published in the year 2001,
revealed  that  in  comparison  with  the  ibuprofen,  the  GS
group showed a significant improvement in pain and all the
evaluated functional parameters [18]. Glucosamine was eval-
uated only in approximately seven clinical trials from 2001
to  2019  to  assess  its  efficiency  on  osteoarthritis  of  TMJ,
which provided inconclusive efficiency on TMJ osteoarthri-
tis [4, 19-24]
Treatment efficiency with glucosamine was greatly af-
fected  by  one  parameter,  the  total  administration  period,
which was even strengthened with evidence from two syste-
matic reviews of our review by Melo et al., 2018 [13] and
Derwich et al., 2023 [6], that a minimum of 3 months of ad-
ministration is necessary for effective action of glucosamine
in osteoarthritis when it is compared against the non-steroi-
dal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) or any other placebo
drugs. As far as safety is concerned, there are a few unusual
side effects of glucosamine, including [25, 26] heartburn,
diarrhoea, nausea, and epigastric pain, and there are no docu-
mented interactions with other medications that exist for glu-
cosamine. Thus, in clinical practice, glucosamine and chon-
droitin sulphate can be highly suggested for TMJ osteoarthri-
tis.
4.1. Limitations
The limitations of this review include the fact that only
four systematic reviews were included in the analysis, which
may limit the generalizability of the findings. Additionally,
the  studies  included  in  the  systematic  reviews  varied  in
terms of their methodology, sample size, and study duration,
which may have introduced heterogeneity in the results. Fur-
thermore, the limited number of clinical trials specifically
evaluating glucosamine for TMJ disorders highlights a gap
in the existing literature, warranting caution in extrapolating
findings to this specific patient population.
Despite the acknowledged limitations, the evidence syn-
thesized  from  this  review  indicates  that  glucosamine  can
serve as a safe and effective adjunctive therapy in managing
TMJ osteoarthritis (OA) with no reported adverse events.
While initial clinical trials have demonstrated notable pain
improvement, further research is imperative to ascertain its
consistent efficacy, especially in the context of TMJ disor-
ders,  given  the  distinct  fibrocartilaginous  composition  of
TMJ articular surfaces. Consequently, glucosamine warrants
inclusion in the comprehensive treatment regimen for TMJ
OA patients, and clinicians should prioritize conducting lon-
gitudinal studies to rigorously evaluate its therapeutic effica-
cy.
CONCLUSION
The latest systematic review underscores the clinical ap-
plicability of glucosamine and chondroitin in managing tem-
poromandibular  joint  (TMJ)  osteoarthritis  (OA),  with
notable  improvements  in  functional  indicators,  such  as
mouth opening and pain reduction. Importantly, their lower
risk  of  side  effects  compared  to  traditional  NSAIDs  or
COX-2 inhibitors, even with long-term use, highlights their
potential as safer alternatives in clinical practice. When com-
bined  with  minimally  invasive  therapies  or  conventional
splints, these nutraceuticals offer a promising adjuvant ap-
proach for enhancing patient outcomes in TMJ OA manage-
ment. However, investigations into potential mechanisms of
action and patient-specific responses are essential for refin-
ing personalized treatment strategies. Overall, the evidence
supports the consideration of glucosamine and chondroitin
sulphate  as  valuable  additions  to  the  armamentarium  for
TMJ OA management, paving the way for improved patient
care and outcomes in clinical practice.
