1. Introduction 
 
The purpose of this PhD project was to investigate the effect of systemic glutamate on 
craniofacial pain conditions. The project was designed to determine if excessive intake of MSG 
could be identified as a significant risk factor for healthy individuals to induce myofascial pain 
or headache and for patients who suffer from musculoskeletal and craniofacial pains to 
exacerbate ongoing clinical pain. 
Whether a specific food in daily meals can affect health or worsen ongoing clinical 
conditions is highly debatable, even though recently more attention to diet has been given as a 
risk factor of diseases. However, there is striking paucity of studies to clarify the effect of a 
certain food or substance included in daily meals on health. 
The main outcome of this PhD project provides reliable and significant information 
regarding intake of MSG for both healthy individuals and patients suffering from chronic 
myofascial pain. The information this PhD project provides will help both healthy people and 
patients with chronic myofascial pain to maintain their Quality of Life in terms of choosing 
daily diet without anxiety.  
 
 
2. Background  
 
2.1. Temporomandibular disorders 
Tempomandibular disorders (TMD) are chronic musculoskeletal pain conditions in the 
craniofacial region. Regarding its epidemiology, TMD is encountered by approximately 10% of 
the population, with a higher prevalence in women1. Myofascial pain is one of the diagnostic 
groups in Research Diagnostic Criteria for Temporomandibular Disorders (RDC/TMD)2 and 
is the most common classification of all TMD3. It is characterized by a regional, dull, aching 
muscle pain which could be accompanied by referred pain4, 5. Myofascial pain, which can be 
associated with limitation of jaw range of motion, often leads patients to seek treatment1. Due 
to myofascial pain, not only masticatory function but also social life and work life can be 
disturbed6, 7. Several risk factors have been discussed for myofascial TMD pain8-10, for 
 
 
 
11
example, age, dental occlusion, craniofacial morphology, bruxism, stress, depression and 
comorbid pain conditions. This complexity of the risk factors has made the pathophysiology of 
TMD difficult to be unraveled11. Although there has been little focused on diet, certain food 
items have been suggested as a risk factor for diseases. For example, red wine and chocolate 
have been claimed as a risk factor for headache12, 13. Related to myofascial pain, vitamin D 
deficiency has also been reported to be associated with muscle weakness and muscle pain14. 
However, so far, there are not enough studies that can provide reliable clear evidence to show 
relevance of specific food to myofascial TMD. 
 
2.2. Monosodium glutamate in diet 
Monosodium glutamate (MSG) is an artificial food additive used mostly in Asian food. 
Many snacks and fast foods also contain high levels of saturated fat, sodium, and simple 
sugars, and, in addition, various additives such as MSG intended to enhance their taste.  
MSG was first isolated in 1908 from seaweed by a Japanese researcher who discovered 
its effect to enhance flavor15, 16. The unique flavor that MSG can elicit is well-known by its 
name, Umami, which means savory taste in Japanese17. Effects of MSG on human health have 
been controversial since the 1960s when the so-called Chinese restaurant syndrome was 
claimed18. Symptoms of the Chinese food restaurant syndrome include headache, chest 
pressure, palpitation, asthma, numbness and dizziness19, 20. Therefore, the Joint FAO/WHO 
Expert Committee on Food Additives (JECFA) established acceptable daily intake for MSG as 
120 mg/kg body weight/day. However, initial studies where safety of MSG was doubted had 
some problems with their methodology such as small sample size, bias, and not controlled. 
Studies on MSG carried out afterwards revealed adequate safety of MSG21, 22. Following the 
results, at the 31st meeting of JECFA held in 1987, acceptable daily intake for MSG was 
changed to “not specified”. The Food and Drugs Administration also categorized MSG as 
“Generally Recognized As Safe”. Nowadays, there are still many studies being performed to 
test if MSG causes various symptoms such as asthma and headache23-28. However, there has 
not been a definite explanation for the potential pathophysiological effects on craniofacial 
pain sensitivity. 
 
 
 
12
As MSG is a salt of glutamic acid, once it is ingested, MSG is divided into glutamate 
and salt. Glutamate exist in natural food, such as meat, fish, and vegetables. Once divided, 
there is no difference between glutamate originally from MSG and natural food. According to 
a Danish large database provided by the National Food Institute at Technical University of 
Denmark (http://www.foodcomp.dk/), tuna, ham, and peanuts contain glutamate in the 
amount of 3400, 4000, 6000 mg/100g, respectively. Almost all foodstuffs are listed in the 
database, showing that it is nearly impossible to avoid the intake of glutamate from daily 
meals. The estimated median daily consumption of glutamate in the Western diet from all 
sources is around 12 g which equals about 170 mg/kg body weight/day of glutamate for an 
individual weighing 70 kg29.  
Considering the daily frequent consumption of MSG in the present age, it seems 
necessary to investigate the effect of MSG on our health in a systematic way. In this PhD 
thesis, “MSG” is used as a name of artificial food additive, where as “glutamate” is used as a 
general term of glutamate regardless of its origin that is either from MSG or natural food. 
 
2.3. Glutamate as a risk factor of craniofacial pain 
Consumption of certain foods is thought to precipitate or aggravate other chronic 
craniofacial pain conditions30-33. As mentioned in the previous section, MSG is a food additive 
that enhances flavor.  
 Many past studies revealed a characteristic of glutamate to induce pain when injected 
into muscles due to activation of peripheral N-methyl-d-aspartate (NMDA) receptors located 
in the muscle tissue34, 35. Injection of glutamate into the masseter muscle of healthy subjects 
causes experimental muscle pain with features comparable to the pain reported in myofascial 
TMD34, and also produces similar mechanical sensitization reported by myofascial TMD 
patients. Therefore, experimental designs based on glutamate injection into the masticatory 
muscles can provide an appropriate model for studying aspects of myofascial TMD pain34. 
Since an elevated interstitial glutamate level in patients with myofascial TMD has been 
reported36, a similar elevation of intramuscular glutamate level induced by injection of 
glutamate may mimic symptoms of myofascial TMD pain in healthy individuals. 
 
 
 
13
However, it is not only local administration of glutamate that causes hypersentitization 
to mechanical stimuli on the masseter muscle in healthy participants37. Tissue glutamate 
concentrations can be elevated by the consumption of food containing excessive amounts of 
MSG. This elevated local tissue concentrations of glutamate by ingestion of MSG is also able 
to influence muscle pain sensitivity37. Thus, excessive intake of MSG could be considered as a 
risk factor that can exacerbate ongoing clinical pain in myofascial TMD patients. Since 
skeletal muscle serves as a major storage site for dietary glutamate38, myofascial TMD 
patients with higher interstitial glutamate level in the masseter36 might have much more 
severe side effects or show a higher sensitivity to mechanical stimuli on the muscles, e.g. the 
masseter muscles than healthy individuals when high dose of MSG is ingested. Previous 
research indicates that doses of up to 1 g/kg of MSG do not significantly cross the blood-brain 
barrier39, 40, which suggests that the effects of systemically administered MSG consumption 
could be mediated by peripheral rather than central nociceptive mechanisms. It is therefore 
conceivable that a high dietary MSG intake may elevate glutamate concentrations in skeletal 
muscles, which could be one life-style related factor contributing to pain sensitivity in chronic 
musculoskeletal pain conditions including TMD. 
Once MSG is ingested, skeletal muscles, including the masseter muscle, store a 
significant amount of systemically administered glutamate intramuscularly with a 7-8 fold 
increase of concentration, though the elevation of the glutamate concentration is short-
lasting38. Acute ingestion of MSG has been reported to be associated with adverse symptoms 
that include general weakness, muscle tightness/tenderness, flushing/sweating, headache, 
paresthesia, arrhythmia and tachycardia in healthy subjects19, 29, 38, 41. In healthy subjects, a 
single oral ingestion of 150 mg/kg of MSG resulted in subjective reports of peri-cranial muscle 
tenderness and increased headache42. In addition, elevated glutamate levels in plasma, 
platelet and saliva are also found in migraine and tension-type headache patients compared to 
healthy controls43-47. These findings suggest that MSG consumption could trigger more types 
of craniofacial pain than just headaches. Indeed, total dietary consumption of glutamate 
ranges from 50 to 200 mg/kg/day48, 49. Even though it is not known whether increased dietary 
consumption of glutamate causes accumulation of glutamate in the masseter muscle to alter 
 
 
 
14
pain sensitivity, regular consumption of MSG may result in tolerance to the acute sensitizing 
effects of MSG in healthy individuals42.  
For myofascial TMD patients, chronic daily administration of excessive MSG might 
lead to the accumulation of MSG in masticatory muscles, which could be involved in 
maintenance of chronic myofascial pain.  
 
2.4. A theoretical muscle nociceptor  
The overall basic theoretical idea in this project is that high dietary intake of MSG can 
elevate blood glutamate concentration leading to increased interstitial glutamate with an 
effect on NMDA receptors to excite and sensitize muscle nociceptors50-52. Peripheral NMDA 
receptors are located in deep craniofacial tissues such as the masseter muscles52, 53. Fig.1. 
illustrates a theoretical muscle nociceptor and the potential action of MSG. Increased 
interstitial glutamate due to high dietary intake could activate NMDA receptors, which leads 
to excitation and sensitization of the muscle nociceptors. The concentration of interstitial 
glutamate in the muscle may be regulated, in part, through excitatory amino acid transporters 
(EAATs) on myocytes as well as on the nociceptor (Fig. 1). These transporters clear glutamate 
from the extracellular space, where it may be packaged into vesicles for re-release from the 
nociceptor terminal ending.   
 
The four studies included in this PhD thesis were designed and performed to gain new insight 
into how MSG is involved in myofascial pain conditions. All studies were performed as 
randomized, double-blinded, placebo-control trials, which can demonstrate effects of 
systemic administration of MSG on both healthy individuals and myofascial TMD patients 
systematically.

3. Aims 
 
Study I: To assess the impact of 5 days of consumption of 150 mg/kg MSG on craniofacial 
pain sensitivity in healthy participants. 
 
Study II: To determine the relationship between 150 mg/kg daily MSG consumption, jaw 
muscle / serum glutamate concentrations and muscle pain sensitivity in healthy subjects. 
 
Study III: To investigate how the difference in interstitial concentration of glutamate in the 
masseter muscle effects muscle pressure sensitivity under experimental pain condition in 
healthy subjects. 
 
Study IV: To compare under controlled conditions if acute 150 mg/kg MSG administration 
will elevate muscle / serum glutamate concentrations and affect muscle pain sensitivity in 
TMD patients.  
 
 
4. Hypotheses 
 
Study I: Daily administration of 150 mg/kg MSG for 5 consecutive days will result in 
measurable increases in jaw muscle pressure sensitivity, associated with increased reports of 
adverse effects such as muscle tenderness and headache. 
 
Study II: Daily oral administration of 150 mg/kg MSG for 5 consecutive days will elevate 
muscle / serum glutamate concentrations and be associated with increased reports of adverse 
effects. 
 
Study III: Increased interstitial glutamate concentration by systemic administration of 150 
mg/kg MSG would cause measurable increases in jaw muscle pressure sensitivity under the 
experimental pain condition. 
 
 
 
17
 
Study IV: Acute administration of 150 mg/kg oral MSG will elevate muscle / serum glutamate 
concentrations and be associated with increased reports of adverse effects (muscle 
tenderness, headaches) and measurable increases in jaw muscle pressure sensitivity in TMD 
patients compared to healthy individuals.   
 
 
5. Materials and Methods 
 
All studies were conducted at the Orofacial Pain and Jaw Function, Department of 
Dentistry, HEALTH, Aarhus University, Aarhus, Denmark. The study protocol was approved 
by the local ethics committee (approval No. 20060040 – amendment No. 2 of March 2010). 
The studies were conducted in accordance with the Declaration of Helsinki. 
All participants, both healthy subjects and patients, were able to read and understand a 
thorough written and verbal description of the experiment they received before signing an 
informed consent form. All participants abstained from participation as a volunteer in other 
medical research projects during participation in the present study. 
All registration and data collection were performed by the same female investigator (Shimada, 
A). All participants were financially compensated (150 DKK/hour).  
 
5.1. Healthy participants (I, II, III) 
Seventy-four healthy participants participated in total (see Table 1). Exclusion criteria 
were: orofacial pain, chronic illness, e.g. uncontrolled hypertension, allergy to MSG, asthma, 
diabetes mellitus, serious psychological or physical illness, body mass index > 2542. They were 
recruited by an ad posted at the Aarhus University and through a webpage 
(www.forsoegsperson.dk). 
 
5.2. Myofascial TMD patients (IV)

of Orofacial Pain and Jaw Function, Department of Dentistry, HEALTH, Aarhus University 
(see Table 1).

criteria were fulfilled: (1) adults over 18 years old; (2) pain-free participants except for the

5.3. Experimental protocol 
Study I: This study was performed as a double-blinded, placebo-controlled cross-over trial. 
Each subject participated in 10 (2 x 5) sessions (Monday through Friday in two consecutive 
weeks). In one week, the subjects received MSG, and in the other week, placebo, in 
randomized order. 
 
Study II: This study was performed as a randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled 
study. Fig. 2 shows the experimental protocol of Study II. Each participant was randomly 
allocated to either the MSG group (N = 22) or the placebo group (N =10) and participated in 5 
sessions through 5 consecutive days. The participants stayed in the same group throughout 
the 5 experimental days.  
 
Study III: This study was performed as a randomized, double-blinded, cross-over trial. Fig. 3 
shows the experimental protocol of this study. Each participant participated in 2 sessions with 
at least 1-week interval. 
 
Study IV: This study was performed as a randomized, double-blinded, cross-over trial. The 
participants participated in 2 sessions with at least 1-week interval. The experimental design 
of each session is same as Day 1 and 5 in Study II (Fig. 2). 
 
 
The examiner and the participants were blinded to the type of ingested substance 
(MSG or placebo) or type of injections (glutamate or IS). The examiner was blinded until all 
the data collection was finished. The randomization was performed by a research assistant. 
Prior to each session, the participants were asked to fast 3 hours in Study I and 2 hours in 
Study II - IV before the experiment.

5.4. Administration of MSG 
Oral administration of MSG was applied in all studies. In addition, local intramuscular 
administration of glutamate was applied in Study III. 
All participants drank 400 mL sugar-free lemon soda (Spirit light®, Coop, Denmark), 
to which either MSG (150 mg per kg bodyweight) or NaCl (24 mg per kg bodyweight) was 
added42. The soda contained carbon dioxide, acidity regulators, natural lemon aroma, a 
preservative and artificial sweetener (aspartame). The amount of NaCl was decided to create a 
similar taste of the soda with MSG42. The drink was prepared in a separate room by a research 
assistant. 
To induce experimental muscle pain in the masseter and temporalis muscles in study 
III, a sterile solution of glutamate (0.5 M, 0.2 mL) was injected, 30 min after the systemic 
administration of MSG or placebo. On the contralateral side, isotonic saline (IS: 0.9 %, 0.2 
mL) was injected as a control. The injections were performed manually over a 10-second 
period with a 27-gauge hypodermic needle and a disposable syringe55. 
 
5.5. Glutamate sampling 
For analysis of glutamate level after the single/repeated intake of MSG, dialysate, 
saliva, and plasma sample were collected. 
 
5.5.1. Microdialysis 
Intramuscular microdialysis was applied to analyze the concentration of glutamate in 
the masseter muscle (Study II and IV). The participants sat on a dental chair comfortably in a 
supine position and were asked to avoid clenching and head movement during the 
experiment. After 30 min of surface skin anesthesia (EMLA cream, AstraZeneca)56, a standard 
venous catheter (BD Venflon TM Pro Safety, 18GA, 32 mm) was inserted into the masseter 
muscle until the muscle fascia was penetrated with a light resistance57. To place the catheter 
intramuscularly, the length was adjusted so that the inserted part would be 15 mm from the 
skin surface, and the needle was removed. The catheter was then withdrawn approximately 3 
mm to place the edge of the catheter inside the muscle tissue at a depth of 12 mm. A sterile 
microdialysis probe (MAB 7, Microbiotech AB, Stockholm, Sweden) with a membrane length 
 
 
 
23
of 13 mm, a shaft length of 31 mm and a diameter of 0.4 mm was inserted into the muscle 
through the catheter to a depth of 22 mm from the skin surface to ensure that the whole 
membrane protruded outside the catheter57. The probe was connected to a 2.0 mL syringe set 
on a microinfusion pump (MAB 40, Microbiotech/se AB) and perfused (2.0 µL/min) with 
sterile phosphate buffered-isotonic saline (pH = 7.4).  The dialysates were collected into 
plastic vials attached to the skin of the neck (Fig. 4).  A dialysate sample (40 µL) was collected 
every 20 min and immediately frozen (-80 °C). The first 80 min of the microdialysis served as 
stabilization period (trauma phase) and the sample obtained after 100 min was regarded as 
baseline sample. The probe and the catheter were removed after the 10th dialysate sample 
was taken57 (Fig. 2). The “interstitial concentration” of glutamate in the masseter muscle in 
Study I and IV was considered as the concentration of glutamate in the dialysate samples 
following the method of previous studies36. This concentration may not be the absolute real 
concentration of glutamate in the interstitial of the muscle but the present study was aiming 
to assess increases on the relative changes of the “interstitial concentration”. 
 
5.5.2. Saliva samples 
Whole resting saliva samples (1 mL) were collected at baseline and 30 min after 
ingestion of MSG in Study I, II, and IV. All participants were asked to collect daily with the 
drooling method before (baseline) and 30 min after the ingestion42. To shorten time for the 
collection of saliva, in Study III, the saliva samples were collected by a commercially available 
collection kit, SalivetteTM (Sarstedt AG & Co, Nümbrecht, Germany)58, 59. A kit of SalivetteTM 
consists of a plastic tube that has a partition dividing upper and lower part, a cotton roll 
sheathed in the upper part of the tube, and a lid. The participants were asked to chew the 
cotton roll for 1 min and put it back into the tube. There was a small hole in the partition of 
the tube so that saliva absorbed into the cotton roll was stored in the bottom part of the tube

6. Data analysis and statistics 
 
All data are presented as means ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Levels of P less 
than 0.05 were considered statistically significant. Statistical analyses were performed using 
STATISTICA (version 5.1; Stat Soft, Inc., USA).  
 
Study I: Spontaneous pain, PPT, PPTol, BP, HR, and glutamate concentration were analyzed 
with 3-way repeated measurement analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with intervention (two 
levels: MSG vs. placebo), day (five levels: Day 1-5 in each week) and time (four levels: 
Baseline, 15, 30, 50 min) as factors. To observe relative changes through the 5 experimental 
days, PPT and PPTol measures were normalized to the baseline value on Day 1. When 
appropriate, post hoc Tukey Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) tests with corrections for 
multiple comparisons were performed. Since this study was designed as a cross-over trial 
where each participant had 2 different sessions, occurrence of side effects was compared 
between weeks with a McNemar´s test.  
 
Study II: Similar to Study I, to observe relative changes through the 5 experimental days, the 
mean PPT values at each measurement, as well as PPTol values and all the values of 
autonomic parameters, were normalized to the baseline on Day 1 for each participant. A 4-
way ANOVA model with repeated measures was used to analyze all the outcome parameters. 
The factors in the ANOVA were intervention (two levels: MSG vs. placebo), sex (two levels: 
male vs. female), day (five levels: Day 1-5) and Time (five levels: Baseline, 15, 30, 45, 60 min 
after the intervention). Separate 4-way ANOVA was applied to analyze the changes of 
glutamate concentration in dialysate, blood and saliva. Similarly, the factors in these ANOVAs 
 
 
 
27
were intervention (two levels: MSG vs. placebo), sex (two levels: male vs. female), day (two 
levels: Day 1 and 5 for dialysate and blood, five levels:  Day 1-5 for saliva) and Time (ten levels: 
M1-10 for dialysate, two levels: B/S1 and B/S 2 for blood and saliva). When appropriate, post 
hoc Tukey HSD tests with corrections for multiple comparisons were performed. 
Furthermore, Pearson correlation analysis was used to analyze the correlation of increase 
ratio of peak glutamate concentration to baseline level between the dialysate, saliva and blood 
samples. The increase ratio of each sample was calculated by dividing peak value by baseline 
value. Occurrence of adverse effects was compared between groups with Chi-square tests. 
Student’s t-test was used to evaluate the possible difference in daily average glutamate 
consumption between the MSG and placebo groups for the experimental period.  
 
Study III: A paired t-test was performed to test a significant difference in the mean Numeric 
rating scale (NRS) scores between the glutamate and IS injections. For analysis of glutamate 
concentration in the saliva samples, a 3-way ANOVA was used with sex (two levels: male vs. 
female), intervention (two levels: MSG vs. placebo) and time (six levels: Baseline, 30, 45, 60, 
75 min after the systemic intervention, and the Post) as main factors. A 4-way ANOVA model 
for repeated measures was used to analyze the PPT and PPT values of the masseter and 
temporalis. The factors in the ANOVA were sex (two levels: male vs. female), session (two 
levels: MSG vs. placebo), type of the injections (two levels: glutamate vs. IS), time (five levels: 
Baseline, 15 min after the oral intervention, 5 min and 10 min after the injection, and the 
Post). For analysis of the PPT, as well as the autonomic parameters, a 5-way ANOVA was 
performed, in which sites of the injections (two levels: masseter vs. temporalis) were added as 
a factor. When appropriate, post hoc tests were performed with Tukey HSD tests with 
correction for multiple comparisons. Occurrence of adverse effects was compared between 
groups with Fisher’s exact test, because the total number of data was relatively small (N = 32). 
 
Study IV: Baseline values of all outcome parameters were compered with Student’s t test 
between the patient group and the healthy controls. Interstitial concentration of glutamate 
was analyzed with 3-way ANOVA model with repeated measures with group (patients vs. 
healthy controls), intervention (two levels: MSG vs. placebo) and time (10 levels: every 20

min, from 20 to 200 min after the intervention) as factors. When appropriate, post hoc Tukey 
HSD tests with corrections for multiple comparisons were performed. For glutamate 
concentration in saliva and plasma samples, a t test was performed to test if there is a 
significant difference between baseline and 30 min after the ingestion in each session, as well 
as between groups.  
A 2-way ANOVA was performed to analyze the change of pain intensity with intervention (2 
levels) and time (8 levels) as factors in the patient group. Another 3-way ANOVA was applied 
to analyze the relative change in PPT/PPTol values. The mean PPT values at each

normalized to the baseline on each session for each participant to see relative changes

8. Discussion 
 
8.1. Change in glutamate concentration after ingestion of MSG in healthy 
individuals 
 
After ingestion of MSG (150 mg/kg), interstitial glutamate levels in the masseter 
muscle increased 7-fold with a peak after 1 hours of ingestion, whereas saliva and plasma 
glutamate level increased 5-fold and 6-fold, respectively. However, repetitive intake of high 
doses of MSG did not elevate the baseline interstitial concentration of glutamate in the 
masseter muscle through the 5 consecutive experimental days in Study II. There was a 
correlation between the increases in levels of glutamate in the samples between saliva and 
blood after MSG administration. However, the glutamate concentration between dialysate 
and saliva or plasma samples was not significantly correlated. This indicates that glutamate 
concentration in saliva does not reflect interstitial concentration of the masseter. Therefore, 
measurement of saliva glutamate concentration cannot be used to estimate glutamate 
concentrations in the masseter muscle. However, in Study III, in which a time-dependent 
change in saliva glutamate level after ingestion of MSG was observed, the pattern of increase 
in the glutamate level in saliva was similar to that in interstitial glutamate level. The lack of 
correlation of an increase ratio, which was calculated by dividing peak value by baseline value, 
between interstitial and saliva glutamate levels, might be explained by the difference in 
amount of glutamate taken up by the muscles after ingestion. Therefore, the fact that 
glutamate level in saliva samples increased can still indicate increased interstitial glutamate 
level. 
Baseline glutamate concentrations in saliva did not increase over the 5 days of MSG 
intake, however, the salivary glutamate concentrations after MSG ingestion increased over the 
5 days period66. Since glutamate has an approximate half life of about 30 min, it could be 
expected that it would be completely cleared from the blood within 4 hours of MSG 
ingestion38. A previous study showed that, 30-45 min after a single oral administration of 150 
mg/kg MSG, glutamate concentration in blood reached a peak of a little over 400 M38. This 
suggests that salivary glutamate concentrations are about 25-50% of blood concentrations. It 
has been suggested that as much as 40% of 150 mg/kg MSG administrated orally is taken by 
 
 
 
47
and stored in skeletal muscle38, 67. Increased peak levels in the saliva over the 5 days observed 
in Study I could reflect saturation of storage capacity in the skeletal muscle66. In a animal 
study, it was suggested that after systemic administration of MSG (50 mg/kg i.v.) glutamate 
concentration in the masseter muscle increased rapidly, associated with significant 
mechanical sensitization of muscle nociceptors37. Oral MSG consumption seems to increase 
glutamate concentration in the masseter muscle of human subjects and this might underlie 
the mechanical sensitization of the masseter muscle seen in Study I66.  
There was a higher dialysate glutamate concentration measured after MSG ingestion 
on the last day compared to the first day, although this increase was significant only 40 min 
after MSG ingestion. Our finding of increased peak concentrations of glutamate after 5 days of 
daily MSG ingestion suggests that the rate at which the masseter muscle is able to remove 
glutamate from the interstitium decreases. This is consistent with the idea that repeated 
administration of MSG results in intracellular accumulation of glutamate in the masseter 
myocytes. Nevertheless, the lack of change in the baseline interstitial glutamate 
concentrations over the same time period suggests that this level of increased MSG 
consumption, at least over the short term, does not overwhelm the capacity of skeletal 
muscles to clear glutamate from the interstitial space.  
 
8.2. Effect of repeated intake of MSG on intensity of pain and muscle pain 
sensitivity in healthy individuals 
Study I and II were conducted to investigate whether, compared to a single 
administration, repeated intake of MSG could affect muscle pain sensitivity in healthy 
individuals. 
In Study I, daily intake of this amount of MSG did lead to a sustained mechanical 
sensitization of the masseter muscle that lasted for the duration of the MSG administration66. 
The pain sensitizing effect of MSG observed only in the masseter muscle suggested that the 
effect was site-specific66. The masseter and the temporalis muscles are different in terms of 
their response properties, which may underlie the finding of a selective mechanical sensitizing 
in the masseter muscle due to MSG ingestion66. It has been reported that elevated glutamate 
concentrations induce mechanical sensitization of nociceptors located in the masticatory 
 
 
 
48
muscle through activation of peripheral NMDA receptors37, 68. Futhermore, a lower expression 
of NMDA receptors by nociceptors has been found to innervate the temporalis muscle and it 
has been found that the response of nociceptors in the temporalis to peripheral NMDA 
receptor activation is less robust than those in the masseter69, 70. Hence, there could also be a 
lower expression of peripheral NMDA receptors by nociceptors that innervate the temporalis 
muscle in healthy individuals, which results in the masseter muscle being more sensitive to 
MSG ingestion than the temporalis muscle34, 66.  
Different from Study I, the same oral administration of MSG (150 g/kg for 5 
consecutive days) did not have a robust effect on PPTs or PPTols in the masseter muscle in 
Study II, although the increase in glutamate concentration should have been sufficient to 
mechanically sensitize muscle nociceptors37. The variability in PPT/PPTol measurements 
might make it difficult to detect the amount of change expected. This could explain the lack of 
effect of elevated glutamate concentrations on muscle pain sensitivities assessed by the 
algometer. However, the same ingestion caused a sustained mechanical sensitization of the 
masseter muscle. This suggests that sensitivity to MSG may depend on individual metabolic 
factors, as well as site specificity which was shown in Study I. Considering the results in Study 
I and II collectively, the effect of oral administration of excessive MSG on muscle pain 
sensitivity appears to be small.  
It has been demonstrated that there is gender differences in muscle pain sensitivity, 
which can explain a higher prevalence of TMD in women than in men71, 72. However, there was 
no significant difference between men and women in PPT/PPTol in Study II where the effect 
of repeated intake of excessive MSG on muscle sensitivity was tested in healthy participants. 
It has been speculated that the gender-related difference in muscle sensitivity could be related 
to estrogen mediating increase in the expression of NMDA receptors by nociceptors in the 
maseter muscle32. The lack of gender differences in Study II could be explained by too low 
concentration of interstitial glutamate in the masseter muscle that did not reach adequate 
levels to induce activation of peripheral NMDA receptors.  
Since interstitial glutamate concentration in the masseter muscle of myofascial TMD 
patients has been reported to be higher than in healthy individuals, Study III was performed 
to investigate whether MSG consumption alters muscle pain and mechanical sensitivity 
 
 
 
49
produced by an experimental pain stimulus that has some of the characteristics of muscle 
pain reported by myofascial TMD patients36. The main finding in Study III was that systemic 
intake of a substantial amount of MSG does not influence either pain intensity or pressure 
pain sensitivity in the masseter and temporalis muscles into which glutamate injections were 
made.  
 
Among various risk factors for TMD, it was hypothesized in this project that daily food 
consumption, especially excessive MSG intake, might change muscle sensitivity to 
experimental pain. A significantly higher interstitial concentration of glutamate could trigger 
severe muscle pain more often in TMD patients than in the healthy individuals36. However, 
the systemic administration of MSG in Study III did not result in higher glutamate-evoked 
pain intensity, compared to the placebo sessions. Also, consistent with past studies35, 72, a 
lower value of the PPT in the masseter was observed after injections, but this occurred 
regardless of the session73, 74. Thus, in this model of TMD-like myofascial pain, no evidence 
was found that MSG alters pain sensitivity.  
Unexpectedly, it was found in Study III that the systemic intake of MSG decreased 
mechanical pain sensitivity in the trapezius muscle after glutamate was injected into either 
the masseter or temporalis muscles. The trapezius muscle is one of the most reported referred 
pain areas in TMD patients75. Glutamate injection into the craniofacial region caused 
decreased sensitivity to pressure stimuli in the trapezius muscle after ingestion of MSG. 
Hypoalgesia in a heterotopic site of painful muscles has been previously observed76, 77.  
Glutamate-evoked pain may have recruited descending noxious inhibitory control, which 
could have decreased the excitability of spinal dorsal horn neurons that receive sensory input 
from the trapezius muscle78. This mechanism could explain the decrease in the PPT in the 
trapezius muscle, even though it is located outside the painful area in the temporalis or 
masseter muscle.   
 
8.3. Effect of repeated intake of MSG on autonomic system of healthy individuals 
In both Study I and II, there was a significant effect of ingestion of MSG on BP and HR. 
A correlation between increased dietary intake of glutamate over a 5 year period and an 
increase in sBP has recently been found, which has been shown to be more pronounced in 
 
 
 
50
women, compared to men66, 79.  In addition, it is important to consider whether increases in 
BP induced by MSG ingestion account for the increased incidence of headaches observed in 
Study I66, which is consistent with other previous studies38, 42. Although there are earlier 
studies in which it was suggested that elevated BP causes headaches80-82, more recent findings 
do not indicate a strong association between hypertension and headaches66, 83. No difference 
in mean sBP has been reported in women with chronic daily headache and women without 
this condition66, 84. These findings indicate that hypertension may not be the main reason for 
MSG-induced headaches and that other mechanism can be thought to exist66.  
It has also been shown that glutamate dilates intracranial and extracranial blood 
vessels through a peripheral NMDA receptor mechanism that involves the release of nitric 
oxide53, 66, 85, 86. It is likely that many therapeutically employed vasodilators appear to cause 
headaches as one of side effects66, 87. For example, once infused, nitroglycerin reliably induces 
a headache, which is thought to be due to dilation of extracerebral arteries66, 88, 89. Recently, 
infusion of calcitonin gene related peptide, a potent neuropeptide vasodilator and migraine 
headache inducer, to migraine headache patients was shown to induce vasodilation of arteries 
only on the ipsilateral side where the migraine headache pain existed66, 90. It was speculated 
that it could be vasodilation of extracranial blood vessels through peripheral NMDA receptor 
activation that mediates MSG headaches66, 91. Alternatively, a previous study revealed that 
oral administration of high dose of MSG elevated blood norepinephrine level in rats with a 
peak at 2 min92. Therefore, the increased plasma glutamate level caused by the MSG ingestion 
in Study II might have led to higher levels of norepinephrine, resulting in higher BP in the 
MSG group.  
 
Decreased HR after the ingestion in both the MSG and the placebo group in Study I 
was expected simply due to resting on a chair during the sessions. Nevertheless, HR in the 
MSG group was decreased to a slower rate compared to the placebo group within the same 
sessions. This could be explained by increased norepinephrine level induced by the MSG 
ingestion. Also, there has been interest in the use of glutamate as a positive inotrope, so it is 
also likely that glutamate increased cardiac output70. When BP goes up, the baroreceptor 
reflex lowers HR, which may also explain the changes observed in Study II. 
 
 
 
51
 
It has been found previously that oral ingestion of 150 mg/kg MSG increases sBP by 5-
10% for 30-60 minutes, with a more variable effect on dBP and HR, although HR usually 
declines42, which is consistent with results in Study I. In Study III, it was found that sBP and 
dBP were significantly increased by intramuscular injection of glutamate into the masseter or 
temporalis muscle. It is possible that the increase observed in both sBP and dBP 5 min after 
glutamate injections was due to the muscle pain produced by the injections. In rats, injection 
of NMDA, a selective glutamate receptor subtype agonist, into the temporalis muscle was 
shown to significantly increase mean BP70. This effect is likely due to a combination of the 
nociceptive input from the injection of NMDA into the muscle, coupled with a positive 
inotropic effect of NMDA on the heart after it is cleared from the muscle into the systemic 
circulation. It is uncertain whether the increased BP observed in Study III after intramuscular 
glutamate injection in healthy subjects is a result of pain alone, or is also reflects a positive 
inotropic effect of the injected glutamate on the heart. 
 
8.4. Adverse effects caused by ingestion of MSG 
Throughout this series of studies, the most striking adverse effect caused by ingestion 
of MSG was headache. In accordance with a past study, more frequent reports of adverse 
events were observed after ingestion of MSG than placebo42. 
In Study I, daily intake of MSG caused headaches and dizziness for at least 1 out of 5 
days in 57% of subjects. Although tolerance to most side effects of MSG administration 
appeared to occur, as evidenced by a decrease in the frequency of side effect complaints, the 
frequency of headache reports remained relatively constant over the 5 days of MSG ingestion. 
This suggests that tolerance to the headache inducing effects of MSG may not occur. Related 
to plasma norepinephrine level, intake of high dose of MSG could reduce the activity of the 
vagus nerve, which influences the entire gastric system93. The symptom of nausea reported in 
Study II could also be related to the change in the norepinephrine level due to excessive 
ingestion of MSG.  
Ingestion of MSG has been suggested to induce adverse effects, also called the Chinese 
restaurant syndrome, since the first report in 196818. Since then, the possible effects of MSG 
have been widely discussed and conflicting results have been reported18, 41. However, MSG has 
 
 
 
52
been classified as “generally recognized as safe” by the Food and Drug Administration in the 
United States. Also, several studies where self-identified MSG-sensitive participants were 
included showed no significant response caused by a high dose of MSG26, 94. Taking earlier 
literature into account, the relationship between MSG administration and the more frequent 
occurrence of headache and nausea reported should be interpreted with caution, even though 
the experiment was performed in a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled manner. 
Since interstitial glutamate concentration of myofascial TMD patients has been reported to be 
significantly higher than healthy controls36, recruitment of patients with myofascial TMD or 
tension-type headache would be required to clarify if consumption of MSG worsens symptoms 
in chronic pain conditions. 
 
8.5. Effect of excessive MSG intake on myofascial TMD pain 
8.5.1. Baseline data comparison between myofascial TMD patients and healthy 
conrtols 
 
Comparison of data at baseline revealed several significant differences that can 
characterize the myofascial TMD patients compared to the healthy controls, such as higher 
intensity of ongoing pain, lower PPtol values in the masseter and the trapezius (see Table 2). 
However, interstitial glutamate baseline concentration in the masseter muscle did not show a 
significant difference between the TMD patients and the healthy individuals. Since a previous 
microdialysis study showed significantly higher level of glutamate in the masseter muscle in 
myofascial TMD patients at the same time point of the baseline of Study IV 36, it has been 
considered that higher interstitial glutamate level could be involved in maintenance of chronic 
pain condition. However, the analyzed values of glutamate cannot be directly compared due 
to a difference of methodology of the analysis in these studies, because the sensitivity of the 
analyzer kit for the analysis of glutamate level used in Study II is higher than that used in the 
previous study36, which suggests a higher accuracy of the data obtained in Study II 36, 95. Since 
a tendency of elevated interstitial glutamate levels in the patient group was observed also in 
Study IV, future studies is needed where more myofascial TMD patients are included to 
confirm the elevated interstitial glutamate level in muscles in chronic myofascial pain 
conditions. 
 
 
 
53
One of the interesting findings in Study IV is the lower PPT in a remote muscle, i.e. the 
trapezius muscle in the TMD patient group. Although there are many studies in which higher 
sensitivity of the masseter muscles of myofascial TMD patients was demonstrated, the 
hypersensitization in a different area from the painful sites was also shown in Study IV. The 
trapezius muscle is frequently associated with trigger points96. Palpation over the trapezius 
muscle can indeed cause referred pain in the craniofacial region97. This lower PPTol observed 
in the trapezius, which is probably caused by both peripheral and central sensitization 
mechanisms98, may imply that excitation and sensitization of muscle nociceptors induced by 
higher interstitial glutamate level could occur in muscles where trigger points related to 
referred pain are located. Some studies have been done with the microdialysis technique to 
analyze several biomarkers associated with pain and inflammation, such as bradykinin, 5-HT 
in trigger points 99-101. Although higher concentrations of the biomakers were observed, the 
methodology is questionable. Since the concentration of each biomarker few minutes after 
insertion of a needle for microdialysis was analyzed, the observed higher concentration of the 
biomakers can be due to a traumatic reaction. A pilot study for this PhD project showed that, 
for analysis of interstitial glutamate concentration with the microdialysis technique in the 
masseter muscle requires at least 100 min of stabilization period. In addition to this, too few 
participants participated to confirm the presence of trigger points99, 100.  
 
 Other significant findings in the TMD patients that differed from the healthy controls 
observed in Study IV were elevated autonomic parameters, such as dBP and HR. Glutamate 
signaling via NMDA receptors is a well-known factor in a very complex regulation of 
autonomic system such as BP and HR102, 103. Similarly to a past study where elevated 
interstitial glutamate concentration in TMD patients was shown36, a tendency of elevation 
interstitial glutamate level was observed in study IV. It could be speculated that this irregular 
glutamate level could be one of the effectors that disturb the autonomic system. It has been 
known that the paraventricular nucleus of the hypothalamus plays a critical role in the 
regulation of the cardiovascular system104. Past animal studies showed blockage of NMDA 
receptors caused a decrease in mean arterial BP and HR105. Elevation of the autonomic 
parameters in the TMD patients group might be considered as a possible characteristic of a 
chronic pain condition. 
 
 
 
54
 
8.5.2. Effect of acute MSG administration to myofascial TMD patients 
 
Oral administration of MSG increased the intensity of present pain of the TMD 
patients in Study IV. Although, so far, both single and repeated (daily for 5 consecutive days) 
oral administration of 150 mg/kg MSG did not cause a high intensity of pain in healthy 
individuals42, the intensity of the present pain in the myofascial TMD patients increased 
significantly in Study IV. The interstitial glutamate level in the masseter muscle increased 619 
% in the TMD patients and 636 % in the healthy controls, respectively, suggesting that similar 
increases of interstitial glutamate levels have different effects depending on whether 
spontaneous myofascial pain exists. On the other hand, the MSG ingestion did not influence 
muscle pain sensitivity in Study IV, even though the interstitial glutamate level in the 
masseter increased significantly in the TMD patients. In animal experiments, even a modest 
increase in tissue glutamate level could excite and sensitize muscle nociception37. Many 
studies have suggested hypersensitization of the masseter muscle induced by glutamate 
injection34, 106, proposing that elevated tissue glutamate concentration would cause the 
excitation and the sensitization of NMDA receptors. The results of Study IV, showing no 
significant change in muscle sensitivity in both the TMD patient group and the healthy 
controls, suggest that, unlike injection of glutamate that can give a very high concentration of 
glutamate acutely in a local area of the muscles, the local concentration of glutamate in the 
muscle may not reach the adequate levels to sensitize NMDA receptors in order to induce 
mechanical muscle sensitization after oral adminstration. Since only 2 male TMD patients 
participated, it was difficult to test gender difference in Study IV. Interestingly, lifestyle habits 
including diet have been reported as an important factor in migraine attack in women32. In a 
future study, it seems important to recruit more male TMD patients to clarify the gender 
difference in pain sensitivity related to diet. 
Furthermore, more frequent reports of headache as an adverse effect of high dose of 
MSG was observed by the MSG ingestion in Study IV. Headache as an adverse effect after the 
ingestion of 150 mg/kg body weight has been consistently shown in a previous study42 and 
through this series of study (I - IV). The average daily consumption of glutamate in the 
Western diet is estimated to be around 12 g, which nearly equals to the average dose of MSG 
 
 
 
55
for the participants in Study IV 29. A single fast food meal of a hamburger and milk shake 
contains approximately this amount of glutamate and has been shown to elevate serum 
glutamate concentrations for about 6 hours, with peak concentrations reaching ~250% of 
baseline concentrations by 2 hours after consumption29. Actually, by the ingestion of MSG in 
Study IV, the plasma glutamate level increased 262 % in the TMD patient group and 414 % in 
the healthy controls, respectively. This high increase could easily be associated with adverse 
symptoms including headache19, 38, 41. Elevated glutamate levels in migraine and tension-type 
headache patients compared to healthy controls have also been reported43-47, suggesting that 
excessive ingestion of MSG, which leads to elevated systemic glutamate levels, is likely to be a 
risk factor for headache. What should be emphasized here is that the duration the participants 
in Study IV had headache was acute, following the curve of interstitial glutamate 
concentration. The headache induced by MSG ingestion in the TMD patients disappeared no 
later than 60 min after the intervention, whereas the healthy participants who had headache 
after the ingestion of MSG stopped to report 45 min after the intervention. Moreover, all the 
participants in this study were asked to drink the soda diluted 150 mg/kg body weight within 
3 min in the MSG session. Even though the amount of MSG was almost equal to that in an 
ordinary fast food meal, it is obvious that the intervention of this study was considerably 
quicker than a real-life situation in which consumption time would be slower. Therefore, it 
should be emphasized that an acute single intake of 150 mg/kg body weight MSG caused only 
transient symptoms including headache.

9. Critical remarks and limitations 
 
All studies described in this PhD thesis were conducted as randomized, double-blind, 
placebo-controlled trials. One of the difficult complications/obstacles common in these four 
studies was how to mask the taste of MSG administered drinks along side placebo drinks. 
However, the MSG was dissolved in sugar-free lemon soda which had been found masks the 
taste of MSG in a previous study42 and thus subjects were not able to distinguish the two 
drinks by taste alone. At the end of each 5 day session, participants were asked to answer 
which substance they thought was mixed in the soda. As a result, the vast majority of the 
participants in Study I could identify the substance correctly66. The fact that adverse effects 
occurred more frequently in the MSG week can explain why they could recognize the 
substance correctly. Nevertheless, this result does show that the blinding procedure cannot be 
completely reliable. Also, due to this partial lack of the blinding, psychophysical assessments, 
such as PPT and PPTol, and subjective reports of side effects might have been influenced66. 
However, it should be mentioned that of two different masticatory muscles used for 
measurement of PPT and PPTol, significant differences were detected only in the masseter 
muscle. This suggests that no systematic bias affected their responses, even under the 
situation where participants could recognize the substance they were receiving66. In addition, 
since reports of adverse effects such as headache could be observed even in the placebo week, 
it was more confusing for participants to clarify adverse reactions induced by MSG 
ingestion94, even though the occurrence of the adverse effects in the placebo week were 
significantly lesser than the MSG week66.  
To analyze interstitial glutamate concentration, microdialysis was applied in Study II 
and IV. Unlike saliva or blood samples, the collection of dialysate samples is a time-
consuming technique, since it demands a considerable amount of stabilization time while the 
glutamate concentration becomes stable after its increase due to traumatic reaction caused by 
a needle insertion. Based on a past study36 and the pilot study for this PhD project, a 100 
minutes stabilization period was used in Study II and IV, which resulted in a total of 4.5 hours 
of experimental procedure. In addition to the issue of time, the invasive nature of the 
microdialysis technique causes limitations in the number of participants. Nevertheless, 
 
 
 
57
several other microdialysis studies107-109 have been able to detect significant changes in 
dialysate concentrations, and it is therefore believed that the sample size was adequate in this 
regard107-109. However, the result of the PPT/PPTol measurements did show some subtle 
indications that a marginal difference between groups could possibly have been demonstrated 
with a larger sample size. Such a marginal difference would probably not be considered 
clinically relevant, however, in addition to its invasiveness that was subjectively and relatively 
higher, especially for TMD patients, since the study design was made based on Study I for 
participants on 4.5 hours of experimental procedure in total, the sample size of Study IV 
resulted in 12 in each group. Nevertheless, under this limitation, baseline pain intensity could 
differentiate the TMD patients and the healthy controls significantly. For further studies to 
confirm that interstitial glutamate level is significantly higher in myofascial TMD patients 
compared to healthy individuals, a larger number of the TMD patients needs to be recruited. 
 
Another methodological consideration would be the use of artificial sweeteners. The 
soda used in all the studies contains an artificial sweetener, aspartame. A similar adverse 
neurological effect of aspartate, which is produced during breakdown of aspartame110, to 
glutamate has been studied. Since both glutamate and aspartate play a role as excitatory 
neurotransmitters in the central nervous system111, 112 and could cause adverse effects such as 
dizziness and headache, this is considered as a limitation of this study. However, due to the 
high amount of glutamate given to the intervention group, we do not consider that the equal 
amount of aspartame given to both groups would be adequate to cause a blunting of the 
potential effect being studied.    
 
It could be argued that data obtained from the food diary may not always be 
sufficiently reliable. The diary was applied in Study II and IV to control for dietary glutamate 
intake in case of differences in daily baseline concentrations of glutamate. Since participants 
in the placebo group did not consume significantly larger amounts of MSG throughout the 5 
experimental days, it is argued that the dietary glutamate intake did not influence the 
allocation of the two groups in both Study II and IV. Although the glutamate levels in the 
baseline on each day did not show any significant differences, an overnight fast would be 
preferred for further studies to standardize baseline condition. Compared to Study II, the 
amount of the daily average glutamate consumption was much less in Study IV, even though 
 
 
 
58
there was no significant difference between the groups in each study. Study II was performed 
in autumn, whereas Study IV was performed in all seasons. Also, 5 experimental days for the 
participants in Study II included a weekend, whereas all experiments in Study IV were 
performed in weekdays. Since there seems to be a tendency that dietary habits differs 
depending on days of the week and seasons of the year113, 114, this difference might have 
occurred.  
Finally, based on this result that significantly higher interstitial concentration of 
glutamate in the masseter was observed from 40 min after excessive intake of MSG, 
associated with significantly higher glutamate concentration in saliva 30 min after the intake 
in Study II, the protocol of Study III was designed, so that all four injections were applied 
while interstitial glutamate concentration in the masseter would be significantly higher than 
the baseline. Although there was no correlation between the peak level of glutamate in 
dialysate samples and saliva samples in Study II, the interstitial glutamate level increased, 
keeping at a significantly higher concentration, after glutamate level in saliva reached a 
significantly higher level than baseline. Therefore, the MSG and placebo sessions in this study 
could be differentiated from each other, in terms of the interstitial glutamate level, as well. 
Regarding the application of the injections, the glutamate injection could induce experimental 
muscle pain in both the masseter and temporalis. Although the IS injection, used as a control 
in Study III, was not completely pain-free 115, a significant difference in pain intensity between 
the glutamate and IS injections was shown.

10. Conclusion 
 
Study I suggests that healthy individuals who consume elevated amounts of MSG in their diet 
are more likely to suffer from headaches and have masseter muscle sensitivity, two symptoms 
associated with myofascial TMD. Although far from definitive, the present findings may 
indicate that there is a link between diet and susceptibility to the development of chronic 
orofacial pain conditions that include TMD and headache.  
 
Study II suggests that daily consumption of 150 mg/kg MSG for 5 continuous days does not 
result in sustained elevation of interstitial glutamate concentration in skeletal muscle tissue, 
nor in increased muscle pain sensitivity, in healthy subjects. However, ingestion of repeated 
high dose MSG did lead to increased frequency of headache and nausea.  
 
Study III suggests that excessive intake of MSG does not affect on muscle pain sensitivity of 
glutamate-induced painful muscles in healthy participants. Similar to the results in Study I 
and II, the most significant effect of systemic MSG administration was dBP. The excessive 
intake of MSG might need to be taken more into account in a perspective of hypertension than 
chronic craniofacial pain. 
 
Study IV suggested that the interstitial glutamate concentration in myofascial TMD patients 
could be elevated by acute intake of 15o mg/kg MSG more than in healthy individuals. 
However, the elevation of interstitial glutamate level did not induce mechanical muscle 
sensitization but increased the intensity of present pain in myofascial TMD patients. Since 
both TMD patients and healthy controls reported headache as an adverse effect after the 
ingestion of MSG, acute intake of MSG could be a possible risk factor of headache, even 
though the duration of headache was transient.  
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
60
11. Future perspectives 
 
  
This series of study suggested that it might be preferable for myofascial TMD patients 
to choose diet with low MSG, even though MSG is not a considerable risk factor for myofascial 
TMD pain. This information may be able to be applied for the management of pain in other 
common chronic musculoskeletal pain conditions simply by altering the diet and control MSG 
consumption.  
Furthermore, the microdialysis studies will allow identification of other possible 
biomarkers for chronic muscle pain. The microdialysis technique can be used to detect 
combination of several biomarkers involved in the maintenance of chronic pain conditions.

12. English summary 
 
Temporomandibular disorders (TMDs) are chronic musculoskeletal pain conditions in 
the craniofacial region, and are encountered by approximately 10% of the population. Several 
risk factors have been discussed for myofascial TMD pain, but, so far, there has been little 
focus on life-style related factors, such as diet. 
This PhD thesis describes a series of randomized, double-blinded and placebo-
controlled studies, whose purpose was to evaluate if excess intake of the food additive, 
monosodium glutamate (MSG), can cause musculoskeletal pain in the craniofacial region, 
and, furthermore if it could be a risk factor for TMD patients. Effects of MSG on human health 
have been controversial since the so-called “Chinese restaurant syndrome” was discussed. 
Symptoms of this rather peculiar syndrome, however, have some similarities to TMD patients 
such as headache and myofascial pain.  
Since glutamate is an essential neurotransmitter for nociceptive signaling, the 
interstitial concentration of glutamate in masseter muscles after both a single and repetitive 
consumption of high-dose MSG was measured in healthy participants and in TMD patients. 
Interestingly, it was found that interstitial glutamate concentration was significantly elevated 
40 min after intake of MSG. Furthermore, repeated oral intake of MSG for 5 consecutive days 
caused elevation of glutamate levels in saliva from the second day. This elevated interstitial 
glutamate level in the masseter did not effect on muscle sensitivity in healthy participant as 
measured by jaw muscle pressure sensitivity. This indicates that systemic intake of MSG does 
not interfere with the normal nociceptive signaling, even though local injection of MSG into 
the masseter induced significant elevated muscle sensitivity compared to injection of saline. 
Also adverse effects such as headache were reported after oral intake of MSG. In myofascial 
TMD patients, a single acute intake of MSG increased the intensity of ongoing clinical pain, 
although it did not increase muscle sensitivity.  
In conclusion, this study indicates that MSG could be a negative factor for TMD 
patients since it intensify their ongoing pain. Therefore, it might be beneficial for such 
responsive TMD patients to choose diets with low levels of either artificial MSG or natural 
occurring glutamate. Oral intake of MSG did not induce an increase in mechanical 
 
 
 
62
sensitization in the masseter muscles in TMD patients nor in healthy subjects, as opposed to 
direct injection into the muscle, which gives significant increased muscle sensitivity.

Abstract 
 
A randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled study was performed to investigate how 
ingestion of monosodium glutamate (MSG) affects muscle pain sensitivity before and after    
experimentally induced muscle pain. Sixteen healthy adult subjects participated in 2 sessions with at 
least 1-week interval between sessions. In each session, two injections of glutamate (Glu, 0.5 M, 0.2 
ml) and two injections of saline (0.9 %, 0.2 ml) into the masseter and temporalis muscles, respectively, 
were undertaken, with a 15 min interval between each injection. Injections of saline were made 
contralateral to Glu injections, and done in a randomized order. Participants drank 400 mL of soda 
mixed either MSG (150 mg/kg) or NaCl (24 mg/kg, placebo) 30 min before the intramuscular 
injections. Pain pressure thresholds (PPT), autonomic parameters, pain intensity and the McGill Pain 
Questionnaire (MPQ) were assessed prior to (baseline) and 30 min after ingestion of soda, as well as 
5min and 10 min after the intramuscular injections and at the end of the session. Whole saliva samples 
were collected prior to and 30, 45, 60, and 75 min after the ingestion of soda.  MSG administration 
resulted in a significantly higher Glu level in saliva than administration of NaCl, and was associated 
with a significant increase in systolic blood pressure. Injections of Glu were significantly more painful 
than injections of NaCl, however, ingestion of MSG did not change the intensity of pain reported.  Glu 
injections also significantly increased systolic and diastolic blood pressure, but without an effect of 
MSG ingestion. Injections of glutamate into the masseter muscle significantly reduced the PPT, 
however, MSG did not significantly alter this effect.  Interestingly, PPT was significantly increased in 
the trapezius after MSG ingestion and intramuscular injection of Glu. In conclusion, increased systemic 
Glu levels after ingestion of MSG did not significantly alter pain sensitivity to experimental pain 
stimuli.   
 
Introduction 
 
We have previously proposed that injection of glutamate (Glu) into the masseter muscle of 
healthy subjects causes experimental muscle pain with features comparable to the pain suffered in 
myofascial temporomandibular disorder (TMD)1. Specifically, intramuscular injection of Glu produces 
pain in healthy subjects that is not different in intensity and quality from pain reported by patients with 
myofascial TMD1. Injection of Glu into the masseter muscle also produces punctate mechanical 
sensitization which is similar, though of a lesser magnitude, to that reported by myofascial TMD 
patients. We have suggested that experimental designs based on glutamate injection into masticatory 
muscle can provide an appropriate model for studying myofascial TMD pain1. 
One of the reasons that intramuscular Glu injections may replicate symptoms of myofascial 
TMD pain in healthy subjects is that interstitial Glu concentrations have been shown to be elevated in 
patients with myofascial TMD2. One way that Glu concentrations can be elevated is by consumption of 
excessive amounts of monosodium glutamate (MSG) containing foods. In healthy subjects, a single 
oral ingestion of 150 mg/kg of MSG resulted in subjective reports of peri-cranial muscle tenderness 
and increased headache3. Oral administration of 150 mg/kg of MSG increases interstitial Glu 
concentration in the masseter muscle by up to 750% over baseline concentrations for a period of about 
90 minutes4. Recent work indicates that repeated daily administration of MSG orally to healthy 
individuals leads to more frequent reports of adverse effect, such as headache and nausea, and resulted 
in a transient but significant lowering of pressure pain threshold and tolerance in the master muscle5.   
The aim of this randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled, cross-over study was to 
investigate whether prior oral ingestion of MSG could alter pain and mechanical sensitivity induced by 
intramuscular injection of Glu into the masseter muscle. We hypothesized that increased interstitial Glu 
concentration in the masseter muscle after oral ingestion of MSG would increase Glu-evoked pain and 
jaw muscle mechanical sensitization compared to placebo. 
 
Materials and Methods 
Participants 
 
Sixteen healthy adult (> 18 years old) participants (8 women and 8 men, mean age ± SD: 24.9 ± 
4.7 years old, mean bodyweight ± SD: 62.1 ± 13.7 kg) participated in this randomized, double-blinded, 
placebo-controlled study. They were recruited by an advertisement posted at Aarhus University and 
through a webpage (www.forsoegsperson.dk). Exclusion criteria were: orofacial pain, any chronic 
illness, e.g. uncontrolled hypertension, allergy to MSG, asthma, diabetes mellitus, body mass index > 
25. Informed consent was obtained from all participants. The study protocol was approved by the local 
ethics committee (approval No. 20060040 – amendment No. 2 of March 2010) and informed consent 
was obtained from all participants. The study was conducted in accordance with the Declaration of 
Helsinki. 
Study design 
This study was performed as a randomized, double-blinded, cross-over trial. Randomization 
was performed by a research assistant. The examiner was blinded until data collection on all 
participants was completed.  
Figure 1 shows the experimental protocol of this study. Each participant participated in 2 
sessions with at least 1-week interval between sessions. The participants were asked to fast, at least, 2 
hours before the experiment4. Sessions began with the participants sitting comfortably on a dental chair 
in supine position. The participants first drank 400 mL sugar-free lemon soda (Spirit light®, Coop, 
Denmark) mixed with either MSG (150 mg per kg bodyweight) or sodium chloride (NaCl; 24 mg per 
kg bodyweight; placebo), in a randomized order3,4. Experimental muscle pain in the masseter and 
temporalis muscles was produced by injection of a sterile solution of glutamate (Glu: 0.5 M, 0.2 mL), 
30 min after the systemic administration of MSG or placebo. On the contralateral side, isotonic saline 
(IS: 0.9 %, 0.2 mL) was injected as a control. The injections were performed manually over a 10-
second period with a 27-gauge hypodermic needle and a disposable syringe6. Both the examiner and 
participants were blinded to the order and type of the injections that were given. Injection of Glu or IS 
were randomized for each subject.  A research assistant prepared both the injections and the drink in a 
separate room. 
Saliva samples 
To analyze the Glu concentration in whole saliva, saliva samples were collected 30, 45, 60, and 
75 min after the oral administration of MSG, as well as baseline and post-session. The saliva samples 
were collected by a commercially available collection kit, SalivetteTM (Sarstedt AG & Co, Nümbrecht, 
Germany)7, 8. A kit of SalivetteTM consists of a plastic tube that has a partition dividing upper and lower 
part, a cotton roll sheathed in the upper part of the tube, and a lid. The participants were asked to chew 
the cotton roll for 1 min and put it back into the tube. There was a small hole in the partition of the tube 
so that saliva absorbed into the cotton roll was stored in the bottom part of the tube after the tube was 
centrifuged at 1000 g for 2 min. The sample was stored at -80°C. 
Pressure pain thresholds (PPT) and autonomic parameters 
PPTs on the masseter (right and left), the temporalis (right and left), trapezius (right), and 
thenar (left) muscles were measured with an alogometer (Somedic, Sweden), at baseline, 15 min after 
oral administration of MSG, 5 and 10 min after each injection, and at the end of the session (90 min 
post oral ingestion of MSG). Sites assessed for PPT on each muscle are as follows: masseter; the most 
prominent point during contraction, approximately 2 cm superior of the mandibular border; temporalis, 
the most prominent point during contraction in the anterior area of the muscle; trapezius; the point 
halfway between C7 and acromion; thenar, the middle of thenar eminence. The diameter of the 
algometer was 1 cm and rate of increase pressure was 30 kPa/s9,10. The PPT was measured three times 
at each time point. At the same time point as the PPT measurements, heart rate (HR), systolic and 
diastolic blood pressure (sBP, dBP) were measured with a digital blood pressure monitor (UA-767 
puls; A&D Medical, Abington, UK)3,4. 
Pain characteristics and adverse effects 
All participants were instructed to rate any pain on a 0-10 numeric rating scale (NRS) at time 
points of 15 min after the oral MSG administration and 5 and 10 min after the local Glu injection, as 
well as baseline and end of session. The participants were asked to fill in the McGill Pain 
Questionnaire (MPQ) to obtain a qualitative description of the pain induced by the injection and to 
draw their maximum distribution of perceived pain on lateral views (from the right and left side) of the 
face11. The pain area was digitized and expressed as arbitrary units (Sigma scan Pro 4.01.003)1,12.  
Statistical analysis 
A paired t-test was performed to test a significant difference in the mean NRS scores between 
the Glu and IS injections. For analysis of Glu concentration in the saliva samples, a two-way ANOVA 
was used with session (two levels: MSG and placebo) and time (six levels: Baseline, 30, 45, 60, 75 min 
after the systemic intervention, and the Post) as main factors. A 3-way ANOVA model for repeated 
measures was used to analyze the PPT values from the masseter, temporalis, trapezius and thenar, as 
well as the autonomic parameters. The factors in the ANOVA were session (two levels: MSG and 
placebo), type of the injections (two levels: Glu and IS), time (BL, 15 min after the oral intervention, 5 
min and 10 min after the injection, and the Post). When appropriate, post hoc tests were performed 
with Tukey Honestly Significant Difference test with correction for multiple comparisons. The 
occurrence of adverse effects was compared between groups with Fisher’s exact test. The data are 
presented as mean ± standard errors of the mean (SEM). P < 0.05 was considered statistically 
significant. 
Results 
Experimental pain 
In both the MSG and placebo sessions, the mean pain intensity 5min after Glu injection into the 
masseter or temporalis muscles was significantly higher than the IS injection into the same muscles (P 
< 0.001) (Fig. 2). The pain intensity produced by Glu injections had significantly decreased by 10 min 
post injection in both muscles (P < 0.001). There was no significant difference in the pain intensity at 
all the time points between the MSG and placebo sessions. Glu injection into either muscle resulted in 
pain area drawings that were significantly larger than those drawn after IS injection into the same 
muscle (P < 0.001) (Fig. 3). There was no effect of pre-ingestion of MSG on the pain drawing area (P < 
0.001). 
Glutamate concentration in saliva 
The ANOVA analysis of the Glu concentration in saliva samples showed a significant effect of 
session (P = 0.013, F = 8.656), time (P < 0.001, F = 5.406), and interaction between session and time 
(P = 0.004, F = 3.922). Post hoc analysis showed that the Glu concentration increased significantly 30 
and 45 min after the systemic administration of MSG, compared to the baseline level (30 min: P = 
0.048, 45 min: P = 0.035). Moreover, the Glu concentration in the MSG session was significantly 
higher than the placebo session though the entire experiment (P < 0.05) (Fig. 4). 
PPT and autonomic parameters 
The ANOVA analyses of the PPT values and autonomic parameters (sBP, dBP and HR) are 
shown in Table 1. Five minutes after injection of Glu into the masseter muscle, the PPT was 
significantly decreased regardless of the systemic intervention (MSG: P = 0.017, placebo: P = 0.007; 
Fig. 5A). In the placebo session, the PPT value in the masseter muscle 5 min after the Glu injection 
was significantly lower than that after the IS injection (P = 0.004). On the other hand, the PPT value in 
the trapezius increased significantly 5 and 10 min after the Glu injection in the MSG session (5 min: P 
= 0.049, 10 min: P = 0.011; Fig. 5C). Ten minutes after the Glu injection, the PPT value of the 
trapezius in the MSG session was significantly higher than that in the placebo session (Fig. 5C).  
The Glu injection significantly increased sBP 5 min after the injection in both the MSG and 
placebo session (MSG: P < 0.001, placebo: P = 0.006; Fig. 6A). In the MSG session, sBP 5 min after 
the Glu injection was significantly higher than that after the IS injection (P = 0.003; Fig. 6B). There 
was a significant increase in dBP 15 min after the systemic MSG intervention prior to injection of Glu 
(P = 0.001), which lasted until 5 min after the first Glu injection (P < 0.001; Fig. 6B). dBP was also 
significantly increased 5 min after the Glu injection after the systemic placebo intervention, (P < 
0.001). The increase of dBP 5 min after the Glu injection showed a significant difference, compared to 
that after the IS injection in both MSG and placebo sessions (MSG: P < 0.001, placebo: P = 0.025; Fig. 
6B). A significant effect of the systemic MSG intervention was also observed in HR (Fig. 6C). Even 
before the injections, 15 min after the systemic intervention, HR in the MSG session was higher than in 
the placebo session (P = 0.008). In the placebo session, HR 5 min after the IS injection decreased 
significantly, compared to the baseline (P = 0.038), which also differed significantly from that in the 
MSG session (P = 0.039). 
Adverse effects 
  
The percentage of participants reporting adverse effects 15 min after systemic administration 
are shown in Table 2. The frequency of participants who reported dizziness in the MSG session was 
significantly higher than in the placebo session (P < 0.043).  
 
 
 
Discussion 
The present study was performed to investigate whether MSG consumption alters muscle pain 
and mechanical sensitivity produced by an experimental pain stimulus that has some of the 
characteristics of muscle pain reported by myofascial TMD patients. The main finding in this study 
was that systemic intake of a substantial amount of MSG does not influence either pain intensity or 
pressure pain sensitivity in the masseter and temporalis muscles into which Glu injections were made. 
Unexpectedly, the systemic intake of MSG decreased mechanical pain sensitivity in the trapezius 
muscle after Glu was injected into either the masseter or temporalis muscles.  
Methodological consideration 
 
In a previous study, healthy subjects who ingested 150 mg/kg of MSG had significantly 
elevated interstitial concentrations of Glu in their masseter muscles between 40 and 80 minutes after 
ingestion4.  The average peak masseter muscle interstitial concentrations of Glu after a single ingestion 
of MSG was ~ 75 M, and the Glu concentration remained above 50 M for at least 60 min4. The 
present study was designed so that all four intramuscular injections are applied while interstitial Glu 
concentrations in the masseter would be significantly higher than baseline. Although there was no 
correlation between peak levels of Glu in dialysate samples and saliva samples, Glu concentrations in 
saliva and muscle both increase after oral administration of MSG in the previous study4. Therefore, the 
salivary Glu levels measured in this study could differentiate the MSG and placebo sessions, and 
indicate that subjects would have had substantially increased muscle interstitial concentrations of Glu 
after ingestion of MSG. Thus, the lack of difference in pain intensity and mechanical sensitization 
when the two sessions were compared is unlikely to be a result of failure of the subjects to be exposed 
to increased systemic levels of Glu.  Further, it has been shown that when the concentration of 
interstitial Glu to in the masseter muscle was increased to ~75 M, mechanical sensitization of the 
muscle nociceptors was maintained until the concentration dropped below 30 M13. Thus, a 
concentration of Glu necessary to sensitize masseter muscle nociceptors would have been maintained 
for the entire MSG session.   
 
It is possible that injection of a different noxious substance, rather than Glu, into the masseter or 
temporalis muscle might have resulted in a different outcome. For example, the pain intensity and area 
of pain drawings were both significantly greater when capsaicin was injected into the masseter muscle 
25 min after injection of Glu (1 M), when compared with injection of isotonic saline14. However, no 
difference in mechanical sensitization was seen when capsaicin injections were preceded by Glu 
injections.  There is also the potential for prolonged maintenance of elevated Glu concentrations in 
skeletal muscles to cause desensitization of Glu receptors after ingestion of MSG14,15, which might 
explain why we failed to see an effect of MSG on Glu evoked masseter muscle pain. In anesthetized 
rats, repeated injection of Glu into the same temporomandibular joint at intervals of less than 30 
minutes resulted in substantially decreased reflex jaw muscle responses, which suggests that clearance 
of Glu out of the muscle may be required to restore full nociceptive responses17.   
Effect of systemic glutamate administration on perceived pain intensity and PPT 
 
Among various risk factors for TMD, we hypothesized that daily food consumption, especially 
excessive MSG intake, might change muscle sensitivity to experimental pain. A significantly higher 
interstitial concentration of Glu2 could trigger severe muscle pain more often in TMD patients than the 
healthy individuals. However, the systemic administration of MSG in this study did not result in higher 
glutamate-evoked pain intensity, compared to placebo. Also, consistent with past studies, a lower value 
of the PPT in the masseter was observed in this study, but this occurred regardless of the session18,19. 
Thus, in this model of TMD-like myofascial pain, we found no evidence that MSG alters pain 
sensitivity.  
 
The trapezius muscle is one of the most reported referred pain areas in patients with TMD20. 
Glu injection into the craniofacial region caused decreased sensitivity to pressure stimuli in the 
trapezius muscle after ingestion of MSG. Hypoalgesia in a heterotopic site of painful muscles has been 
previously observed21, 22.  Glu evoked pain may have recruited descending noxious inhibitory control 
(DNIC), which could have decreased the excitability of spinal dorsal horn neurons that receive sensory 
input from the trapezius muscle23.  This mechanism could explain the decrease in the PPT in the 
trapezius muscle, even though it is located outside the painful area in the temporalis or masseter 
muscle.   
Effect of MSG administration on autonomic parameters 
 
In previous studies, it has been found that oral ingestion of 150 mg/kg MSG increases sBP by 
5-10% for 30-60 minutes, with a more variable effect on dBP and heart rate, although heart rate usually 
declines3,5 . In the present study, it was found that sBP and dBP were significantly increased by 
intramuscular injection of Glu into the masseter or temporalis muscle. It is possible that the increase 
observed in both systolic and diastolic blood pressure 5 min after Glu injections was due to the muscle 
pain produced by the injections. In rats, injection of NMDA, a selective Glu receptor subtype agonist, 
into the temporalis muscle was shown to significantly increase mean blood pressure24. This effect is 
likely due to a combination of the nociceptive input from the injection of NMDA into the muscle, 
coupled with a positive inotropic effect of NMDA on the heart after it is cleared from the muscle into 
the systemic circulation. It is uncertain whether the increased blood pressure observed in the present 
study after intramuscular Glu injection in healthy subjects is a result of pain alone, or is also reflects a 
positive inotropic effect of the injected Glu on the heart. 
Conclusion 
The present study suggests that excessive intake of MSG does not greatly affect muscle pain sensitivity 
in healthy subjects. Similar to results in previous studies, the most significant effect of systemic MSG 
administration was to increase blood pressure. As Glu injection into the masseter muscle can replicate 
some of the symptoms reported by patients with myofascial TMD, this study suggests that elevated 
dietary intake of MSG would likely not affect pain sensitivity in TMD patients. Future research on the 
relationship between MSG consumption and pain in TMD is required to confirm this assumption.

Abstract 
 
A randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled study was conducted to investigate how 
single ingestion of monosodium glutamate (MSG) affects interstitial glutamate concentration and 
muscle pain sensitivity in myofascial temporomandibular disorder (TMD) patients.  
Twelve patients of myofascial TMD patients and 12 sex- and age-matched healthy controls 
participated in 2 sessions with at least 1 week interval between sessions. In each session, all 
participants drank MSG (150 mg/kg) or NaCl (24mg/kg) diluted with a 400 mL soda. The 
concentrations of glutamate in dialysate, plasma, and saliva samples before and after the ingestion were 
assessed. Pressure pain threshold (PPT), pressure pain tolerance (PPTol), and autonomic parameters 
(systolic and diastolic blood pressure, heart rate) were measured. All participants were asked to report 
adverse effects after the ingestion. Pain intensity was scored with numeric rating scale (NRS), and the 
McGill Pain Questionnaire (MPQ) was filled out by each participant.  
Interstitial glutamate concentration in the masseter in the TMD group was higher (not 
significant) at baseline and increased significantly 40-100 after the ingestion. Although the interstitial 
glutamate concentration in the healthy controls also increased, 40 and 80 min after the ingestion, the 
concentration of the TMD group was significantly higher than that of the healthy controls. However, 
this increase in the interstitial glutamate concentration did not cause hypersensitivity in the masseter 
muscle, but increased intensity of present pain of the TMD group. The ingestion of MSG caused 
headache as an adverse effect in both groups. 
In conclusion, excessive MSG ingestion could be an exacerbation factor of myofascial TMD pain.  
 
 
 
Introduction 
Temporomandibular disorders (TMD) are musculoskeletal conditions that embrace a number of 
clinical problems involving the masticatory muscles, the temporomandibular joint (TMJ), and the 
associated structures1,2. More than half of patients of TMD have problems with muscles, such as 
myofascial pain and impairment of masticatory function. Myofascial pain is the most noticeable 
symptom that leads patients to seek treatment, associated with limitation of jaw range of motion1. Even 
though many studies on myofascial TMD pain have been carried out, its complexity many risk factors 
are involved interferes a definite understanding of the pathophysiology3-8.  
Glutamate, an excitatory amino acid known as one of neurotransmitters in the central nerve 
system, has been widely noticed in the field of pain research, since many past studies revealed its 
characteristic to induce pain when injected into muscles due to activation of peripheral N-methyl-d-
asparatate (NMDA) receptors located in the muscle tissue9,10. It is not only local but also systemic 
glutamate that causes hypersentitization to mechanical stimuli on the masseter muscle in healthy 
participants11, suggesting that local tissue concentrations of glutamate may influence muscle pain 
sensitivity. Therefore, excessive intake of glutamate could be considered as a risk factor that can 
deteriorate present pain in myofascial TMD patients. Since skeletal muscle serves as a major storage 
site for dietary glutamate12, myofascial TMD patients with higher interstitial glutamate level in the 
masseter14 might have much more severe side effects or show more clear sensitivity to mechanical 
stimuli on the muscles, e.g. the masseter muscles, than healthy individuals, when high dose of MSG 
was ingested. 
The aim of this study was to examine under controlled conditions if acute MSG administration 
will elevate muscle/serum glutamate concentrations and affect muscle pain sensitivity in myofascial 
TMD patients more than in healthy individuals. We hypothesize that acute administration of oral MSG 
will elevate muscle, serum and saliva glutamate concentrations and be associated with increased 
reports of adverse effects (muscle tenderness, headaches) and measurable decreases in jaw muscle 
pressure sensitivity in myofascial TMD patients compared to healthy individuals.   
 
 
 
 
 
 
Materials and Methods 
Patients and healthy controls 
 
Twelve patients (2 men, 10 women, mean age ± SD = 29.0 ± 8.8y, mean body weight ± SD = 
76.3 ± 14.6 kg) of myofascial TMD, recruited from the Section of Orofacial pain and jaw function, 
Department of Dentistry, Aarhus University, and 12 healthy sex- and gender-matched controls (2 men, 
10 women , mean age ± SD =  28.9 ± 10.7y, mean body weight ± SD = 62,5 ± 13.0 kg) participated in 
this study. The healthy controls were recruited by an ad posted at the Aarhus University. The 
myofascial TMD pain patients were eligible to participate in this study if the following criteria were 
fulfilled: (1) adults over 18 years old; (2) pain-free participants except for the masseter muscle; (3) 
myofascial TMD patients diagnosed as myofascial TMD 1a or 1b from RDC/TMD14. The patients 
were required to have ongoing pain with a characteristic pain intensity of more than 2 out of 10 on a 
visual analog scale (VAS) prior to the experiment. Inclusion criteria for the healthy participants were 
no presence of muscle pain in the masticatory muscles, no history of TMD problems. Exclusion criteria 
for all participants in this study were presence of systemic musculoskeletal pain disorders, such as 
fibromyalgia, or inflammatory joint disease, such as rheumatoid arthritis; serious psychological or 
physical illness, previous report of adverse reactions to MSG, and asthma. Informed consent was 
obtained from all the patients and the healthy controls before the inclusion. The study was approved by 
the local ethics committee (approval No. 20060040 – amendment No. 2 of March 2010) and conducted 
in accordance with the Helsinki Declaration. 
Study design 
 
This study was performed as a randomized, double-blinded, cross-over trial. The participants 
participated in 2 sessions with at least 1-week interval. Prior to each session, the participants were 
asked to fast 2 hours before the experiment. The participants sat on a dental chair comfortably in a 
supine position and drank 400 mL sugar-free lemon soda (Spirit light®, Coop, Denmark) with which 
either monosodium glutamate (MSG, 150 mg/ kg bodyweight) or NaCl (24 mg/kg body weight) as 
placebo was diluted15. Both an examiner and the participants were blind to the type of ingested 
substance (MSG or placebo). The code of the randomization was broken when all data collection was 
completed.  
Glutamate sampling 
To analyze glutamate level, dialysate, saliva, and blood samples were collected in this study. A 
venous blood sample (4 mL) and a resting whole saliva sample (1 mL) were collected at baseline and 
30 min after the intervention in each session16. Hence, four blood samples and four saliva samples were 
taken from each participant in total. The blood samples were centrifuged (2900 g at 4 ℃ ) for 10 min. 
Plasma (1 mL) was pipetted into a plastic Eppendorf tube and stored at -80 ℃, together with the saliva 
samples until analysis. Intramuscular microdialysis was applied in the masseter muscle. The detailed 
procedure was described in our previous study16. Briefly, after 30 min application of surface skin 
anesthesia (EMLA cream, AstraZeneca, Copenhagen, Denmark), a standard venous catheter (BD 
Venflon TM Pro Safety, 18 GA, 32 mm, BD, Albertslund, Denmark) was inserted into the masseter 
until the muscle fascia was penetrated with a light resistance. After the needle removal, the position of 
the catheter was adjusted so that the edge of the catheter inside the muscle tissure was placed at a depth 
of 12 mm. A sterile microdialysis probe (MAB 7, Microbiotech AB, Stockholm, Sweden) was inserted 
into the muscle through the catheter, connected to a 2 mL syringe, and perfused (2.0 μL/min)  with 
sterile phosphate buffered-isotonic saline (pH = 7.4). A dialysate sample (40 μL) was collected every 
20 min and immediately stored in a freezer (- 80 °C). Stabilization period (trauma phase) was taken 80 
min and a sample obtained after 100 min after the dialysis started was regarded as baseline sample. 
Following the method of previous studies13,16, the concentration of glutamate in the dialysate samples 
was considered as the interstitial glutamate level in the masseter muscle. In addition to the dialysate 
samples, a venous blood sample (4 mL) and a resting whole saliva sample were collected at baseline 
and 30 min after the ingestion in each session. After the centrifugation (2900 rpm at 4 °C) for 10 min, 1 
mL of plasma was pipetted into a plastic tube and stored in a freezer (- 80 °C), together with the 
dialysate and saliva samples. Glutamate levels of all samples were analyzed in the same procedure as 
the previous study16. 
Pressure pain thresholds (PPT) and pressure pain tolerance (PPTol) 
A pressure algometer (Somedic, Hörby, Sweden)15,16 was used to measure PPT and PPTol of 
the masseter muscles (on the contralateral side of the microdialysis side), trapezius (right), and thenar 
(left) at baseline, every 15 min after the ingestion, and the post-session. The measurement of PPT was 
repeated three times, where as a single measurement of PPTol was performed at each time point. 
Autonomic parameters 
 
At the same time when PPT and PPTol were measured, sytolic and diastolic blood pressure 
(sBP and dBP) and heart rate (HR) were measured with a digital blood pressure monitor (UA-767 puls; 
A&D Medical, Abington, UK)15,16. 
Food diary 
To assess the amount of glutamate taken outside the laboratory, both patients and healthy 
controls were asked to write down the consumption of food they had in a previous day of the 
experiment. Based on the food diary, the amount of glutamate was calculated using a Danish large 
database (http://www.foodcomp.dk/) provided by the National Food Institute, Technical University of 
Denmark16. 
Statistics 
Baseline values of all outcome parameters were compered with Student’s t test between the 
patient group and the healthy controls. Interstitial concentration of glutamate was analyzed with 3-way 
repeated measurement (RM) analyses of variance (ANOVAs) with group (patients vs. healthy 
controls), intervention (MSG vs. placebo) and time (every 20 min, from 20 to 200 min after the 
intervention) as factors. When appropriate, post hoc Tukey Honestly Significant Difference (HSD) 
tests with corrections for multiple comparisons were performed. For glutamate concentration in saliva 
and plasma samples, t test was performed to test if there is a significant difference between baseline 
and 30 min after the ingestion in each session, as well as between groups.  
A 2-way ANOVA was performed to analyze the change of pain intensity with session (2 levels) 
and time (8 levels) as factors in the patient group. Another 3- way ANOVA was applied to analyze the 
relative change in PPT/PPTol values. The mean PPT values at each measurement, as well as PPTol 
values and all the values of autonomic parameters were normalized to the baseline on each session for 
each participant. The factors in the ANOVA were group (2 levels), session (2 levels), and time (8 
levels). 
Occurrence of adverse effects between the sessons (MSG/placebo) was tested with McNemar 
test in each group (Patients/Healthy controls), whereas Fisher's exact test was used to test if there was a 
significant difference in occurrence of adverse effect between groups. 
Student’s t test was used to evaluate the possible difference in daily average glutamate 
consumption between the patient group and the healthy control groups in each session. Also, between 
sessions, the glutamate consumption was compared with paired t test within a participant. 
All data are presented as means ± standard error of the mean (SEM). Levels of P less than 0.05 
were considered statistically significant. 
 
 
Results 
Glutamate concentration 
Baseline interstitial glutamate level of the patients was higher than the healthy controls, which 
did not show a significant difference (P = 0.203, t test, Table 1).  Three-way ANOVA showed a 
significant effect of session, time, and interaction between session and time. Furthermore, a post hoc 
test show glutamate level increased significantly in both the patients and healthy control in the MSG 
session 40-100 min after the ingestion, for which significant glutamate levels was seen between session 
in both groups. 40 and 80 min after the ingestion, the interstitial glutamate level of the patient group 
was significantly higher than that of the healthy controls (Fig. 1). The baseline glutamate level in 
plasma was also higher in the patient group than the healthy participants (P = 0.102, t test), whereas the 
baseline level in saliva was lower in the patient group than the healthy controls (P = 0.376, t test). In 
plasma samples, glutamate levels of both groups increased significantly 30 min after the ingestion in 
the MSG session (patients: P < 0.001, healthy controls: P = 0.004) and were significantly higher than 
those in the placebo session (patients: P < 0.001, healthy controls: P = 0.002) (Fig. 2). In saliva 
samples, glutamate level of both groups also increased significantly 30 min after the ingestion in the 
MSG session (patients: P = 0.003, healthy controls: P = 0.002), which also showed a significant 
difference, compared to those in the placebo session (patients: P = 0.003, healthy controls: P < 0.001) 
(Fig. 2).  
Pain intensity, Pressure pain thresholds (PPT) and pressure pain tolerance (PPTol) 
All patients participated under a condition muscle pain over 2 out of 10 NRS existed. For the 
patients, the ingestion of MSG cause a significant increase in pain intensity, compared to the placebo 
intervention, although pain drawing did not show any significant difference between the sessions. The 
significant effect of MSG was observed 15-60 min after the ingestion (Fig. 3). Baseline PPT value in 
the masseter of the patients was lower than that in the healthy controls, however the difference was not 
significant. On the other hand, baseline PPTol values in the masseter and trapezius of the patients were 
significantly lower than those of the healthy controls. Regarding relative change in PPT/PPTol values, 
there was no significant effect of MSG (Fig. 4-6).  
The Only significant effect was a significant increase in diastolic BP of the healthy controls15 
min after the MSG ingestion. The diastolic BP of the healthy controls was significantly lower in 
baseline, compared to the patient group. Baseline HR of the patients group was also significantly 
higher than that of the healthy participants (Fig. 7, 8).  
Adverse effec 
Headache is the most often reported adverse effect in the MSG session for both patients and 
healthy controls, which occurred significantly more often than the placebo session (Patients: P = 0.041, 
Healthy Controls: P = 0.013). Patients reported dizziness as an adverse effect in the MSG session 
significantly often than the placebo session (Fig. 9). 
Glutamate consumption 
The daily average glutamate consumption of the patients’ group in the MSG and placebo 
sessions were 12.40 ± 1.38 g and 12.9 ± 1.63 g, respectively. The glutamate consumption of the healthy 
control group in the MSG and placebo sessions was 10.94 ± 0.75 g and 10.01 ± 1.12 g, respectively. 
Student’s t test showed that the daily average glutamate consumption between groups was not 
significantly different in both sessions (MSG: P = 0.339, placebo: P = 0.199). In addition, there was no 
significant difference in the daily average glutamate consumption between sessions in each group 
(Patients: P = 0.640, Healthy controls: P = 0.405).  
 
Discussion 
 
This is the first report of effect of systemic glutamate on myofascial pain of myofascial TMD 
patients with microdialysis technique. We hypothesized that acute ingestion of excessive MSG would 
elevate muscle, serum and saliva glutamate concentration and be associated with increased reports of 
adverse effects and measurable decreases in jaw muscle pressure sensitivity in myofascial TMD 
patients compared to healthy individuals. The findings of this study demonstrate that interstitial 
glutamate concentration in the TMD patients was elevated significantly more than that in the healthy 
controls after excessive single MSG ingestion. However, the increase in systemic glutamate 
concentration did not cause peripheral sensitization in the masseter muscles. 
Characteristics of myofascial TMD patients 
 
Comparison of data at baseline revealed several significant differences that can characterize the 
myofascial TMD patients, compared to the healthy controls. However, interstitial glutamate baseline 
concentration in the masseter muscle did now show a significant difference between the TMD patients 
and the healthy individuals. Since a previous microdialysis study showed significantly higher level of 
glutamate in the masseter muscle in myofascial TMD patients at the same time point of the baseline of 
this study13, it has been considered that higher interstitial glutamate level could be involved in 
maintenance of chronic pain condition. The analyzed values of glutamate cannot be compared due to a 
difference of methodology of the analysis in these studies. However, Sensitivity of an analyzer for the 
analysis of glutamate level in this study is more higher than that used in the previous study, which 
suggested more accuracy of the data obtained in this study13,16,17. Since a tendency of elevated 
interstitial glutamate level in the patient group was observed also in this study, future study where more 
myofascial TMD patients are included to confirm the elevated interstitial glutamate level in muscles in 
chronic myofascial pain condition. 
One of interesting findings in this study is lower pressure pain tolerance in a remote muscle, i.e. 
the trapezius muscle in the patient group. Although there are many studies in which higher sensitivity 
of the masseter muscles of myofascial TMD patients was demonstrated, the hypersensitization in a 
different area from painful site was also shown in this study. The trapezius is one of the well-known 
trigger points18. Palpation over the trapezius causes referred pain in the craniofacial region. This lower 
PPTol observed in the trapezius, which probably caused by both peripheral and central sensitization 
mechanism19, may imply that excitation and sensitization of muscle nociceptors induced by higher 
interstitial glutamate level could occur in muscles where trigger points of referred pain are located. 
 
 Other significant findings in the patient group that differed from the healthy controls observed 
in this study were elevated autonomic parameters, such as diastolic blood pressure and heart rate. 
Glutamate signaling via NMDA receptors is a well-known factor in a very complex regulation of 
autonomic system such as blood pressure and heart rate20,21. Past studies of myofascial TMD pain have 
shown elevated interstitial glutamate concentration in patients. In this study, a tendency of elevation 
interstitial glutamate level was observed. It could be speculated that this irregular glutamate level could 
be one of the effectors that disturb the autonomic system. It has been known that the paraventricular 
nucleus (PVN) of the hypothalamus plays a critical role in the regulation of cardiovascular system22. 
Past animal studies showed blockage of NMDA receptors caused decreased in mean arterial blood 
pressure and heart rate23. Elevation of the autonomic parameters in the patients group might be 
considered as a possible characteristic of chronic pain condition. 
Effect of acute MSG administration to myofascial TMD patients 
 
Oral administration of MSG increased the intensity of present pain of the patients in this study. 
Although, so far, both single and repeated (daily for 5 consecutive days) oral administration of 140 
mg/kg MSG did not cause pain in healthy individuals15,16, the intensity of present pain in the 
myofascial TMD patients increased significantly in this study. The interstitial glutamate level in the 
masseter muscle increased 619 % in the patient group and 636 % in the healthy controls, respectively, 
suggesting that identical increase of interstitial glutamate level affect differently according to whether 
spontaneous myofascial pain exists. On the other hand, the MSG ingestion did not influence on muscle 
pain sensitivity in this study, even though interstitical glutamate level in the masseter increased 
significantly in the patient group. In animal experiments, even modest increase in tissue glutamate level 
could excite and sensitize muscle nociception11. And many studies proved hypersensitization of the 
masseter muscle induced by glutamate injection9,24, proposing that elevated tissue glutamate 
concentration would cause the excitation and the sensitization of NMDA receptors. The results of this 
study, showing no significant change in muscle sensitivity in both the patient group and the healthy 
controls, suggest that, different from injection of glutamate that can give high concentration of 
glutamate acutely in local area of muscles, local concentration of the muscle could not reach the high 
enough level to sensitize NMDA receptors in order to induce mechanical muscle sensitization. 
Furthermore, more frequent report of headache as an adverse effect of high dose of MSG was 
observed by the MSG ingestion in this study. Headache as an adverse effect after the ingestion of 150 
mg/kg body weight has been constantly shown in previous studies15,25. The average daily consumption 
of glutamate in the Western diet is estimated to be around 12 g that nearly equals to the average dose of 
MSG for the participants in this study26. A single fast food meal of a hamburger and milk shake 
contains approximately this amount of glutamate and has been shown to elevate serum glutamate 
concentrations for about 6 hours, with peak concentrations reaching ~250% of baseline concentrations 
by 2 hours after consumption26. Actually, by the ingestion of MSG in this study, the plasma glutamate 
level increased 262 % in the patient group and 414 % in the healthy controls, respectively. This much 
increase could easily be associate with adverse symptoms including headache12,27,28. Elevated 
glutamate level in migraine and tension-type headache (TTH) patients compared to healthy controls has 
also been reported29-33, suggesting that excessive ingestion of MSG, which leads elevated systemic 
glutamate level, is likely to be a risk factor of headache. What should be emphasize here is that 
duration the participants in this study had headache was acute, following the curve of interstitial 
glutamate concentration. Headache the patients had disappeared no later than 60 min after the 
intervention, whereas the healthy participants who had headache after the ingestion of MSG stopped to 
report 45 min after the intervention. Moreover, all the participants in this study were asked to drink the 
soda diluted 150 mg/kg body weight within at most 3 min in the MSG session. Even though the 
amount of MSG was almost equal to that in an ordinary fast food meal, it is obvious that the 
intervention of this study was considerably quicker than real situation of meal time. Therefore, we 
would summarize that acute single intake of 150 mg/kg body weight MSG caused brief headache. 
  Methodological consideration 
 
This randomized, double-blinded, placebo-controlled study was performed to investigate how 
single administration of excessive MSG effects on myofascial TMD patients, compared to healthy 
controls. In addition to its invasiveness that was subjectively relatively higher, especially for patients, 
since the study design made based on our previous study made demands for participants on 4.5 hours 
of experimental procedure in total, the sample size of this study resulted in 12 in each group. 
Nevertheless, under this limitation, baseline pain intensity could differentiate the patients and the 
healthy controls significantly.  
For further studies to confirm that interstitial glutamate level is significantly higher in 
myofascial TMD patients compared to healthy individuals, larger number of the patients needs to be 
recuited. 
 
 
Conclusion 
The present study suggested that interstitial glutamate concentration in myofascial TMD 
patients could be elevated by excessive intake of MSG more than healthy individuals. However, the 
elevation of interstitial glutamate level did not induce mechanical muscle sensitization but increased 
the intensity of present pain of myofascial TMD patients. Since both patients and healthy controls 
reported headachd as an adverse effect after the ingestion of MSG, acute intake of MSG could be a 
significant risk factor of headache, even though the duration of headache is brief.
