Abstract 
Objective: To estimate the effect of denosumab compared with bisphosphonates on reducing the risk of knee 
or hip osteoarthritis (OA) among adults with osteoporosis in a real-world cohort.  
Method: This new-user, active-comparator retrospective real-world cohort utilized electronic health records 
from the TriNetX Network. Participants diagnosed with osteoporosis between January 1, 2014 and December 
31, 2023 were selected. We used propensity-score matching (PSM) to balance potential confounders between 
denosumab users and bisphosphonate users. Cox proportional hazards models were used to estimate the hazard 
ratios (HRs) and 95% confidence intervals (CIs) of incident knee and hip OA by comparing PSM groups. 
Results: 59,157 new users of denosumab were matched on propensity score to 59,157 participants of 
bisphosphonate users (mean age 70 years; 87% female). The 10-year incidence probability of knee and hip 
OA in denosumab users was 19.2% (4,839 events and 14.5% (3,396 events), while in bisphosphonate users it 
was 21.4% (5,389 events) and 14.4% (3,388 events). Compared with bisphosphonate, denosumab users were 
significantly associated with a reduced risk of any OA (HR 0.96, 95% CI 0.94-0.98), knee OA (HR, 0.87, 95% 
CI, 0.84-0.91), but not with hip OA (HR, 0.98, 95% CI, 0.94-1.03), or thumb carpometacarpal OA (HR 0.94, 
95% CI 0.87-1.01). These associations were more pronounced among older participants (≥ 65 years, HR 0.88, 
95% CI 0.84-0.91 for knee OA), females (HR 0.87, 95% CI 0.83-0.91 for knee OA), and individuals of Asian 
ethnicity (HR 0.73, 95% CI 0.63-0.84 for knee OA).

Conclusions: Denosumab use was associated with a reduced risk of incident knee OA compared with 
bisphosphonates in adults with osteoporosis, with the association being more pronounced among older adults, 
females, and individuals of Asian ethnicity. This study suggests that denosumab could attenuate the 
development of knee OA, warranting further clinical trials.  
 
Keywords: Osteoarthritis; osteoporosis; denosumab; bisphosphonate 
 
Introduction 
Osteoarthritis (OA) is a highly prevalent chronic joint disease affecting over 595 million individuals 
worldwide, with an estimated global prevalence of 22% in individuals aged 40 and above.1 It is a leading 
cause of chronic pain and disability, accounting for more than 7% of all years lived with disability globally.2 
Despite its widespread prevalence and significant disease burden on healthcare systems, there are currently 
no disease-modifying drugs approved for OA treatment, leaving a critical gap in therapeutic options.3 Current 
interventions primarily focus on symptomatic relief and function improvement, such as pain management and 
joint replacement surgery, underscoring the urgent need for novel therapeutic or preventive strategies that 
address the underlying pathophysiological mechanisms of OA.4 
Denosumab, a humanized monoclonal antibody targeting the receptor activator of nuclear factor κ B ligand 
(RANKL), is a potent antiresorptive agent widely used in managing osteoporosis.5 By inhibiting RANKL 
signaling, denosumab suppresses osteoclastogenesis, reduces bone resorption, and promotes subchondral bone 
remodeling.6 Emerging preclinical evidence suggests that RANKL downregulation may also mitigate 
oxidative stress-induced apoptosis and cartilage degradation, which are key processes implicated in OA 
pathogenesis.7 Notably, a randomized controlled trial demonstrated that denosumab exerted structure-
modifying effects in erosive hand OA, improving subchondral bone integrity and preventing joint erosion.8 
However, evidence on the impact of denosumab on large joint OA, particularly in the knee and hip, remains 
sparse. 
The paucity of real-world evidence examining denosumab’s potential to mitigate the risk of OA in weight-
bearing joints represents a critical knowledge gap. Therefore, this study aimed to evaluate the association

between denosumab use and the risk of incident knee and hip OA in a real-world cohort of adults with 
osteoporosis. By leveraging electronic health records and propensity-score matching (PSM), this investigation 
provides valuable insights into denosumab’s potential as a preventive agent for OA and informs the design of 
future clinical trials targeting this debilitating condition. 
Methods 
Data source and study design 
This retrospective cohort study utilized data from the TriNetX US Collaborative Network to compare the risk 
of OA among adults with osteoporosis treated with either denosumab or bisphosphonates. The dataset was 
extracted on September 10, 2024. TriNetX is a global federated health research network providing access to 
de-identified electronic health records (EHRs), including diagnoses, procedures, medications, laboratory 
values, and genomic information data, contributed by 66 large healthcare organizations across the United 
States.  
The majority of participating institutions are academic medical centers with both inpatient and outpatient 
services spanning all 50 U.S. states. TriNetX adheres to the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability 
Act (HIPAA), U.S. and other applicable data privacy regulations. TriNetX is ISO 27001:2013 certified and 
maintains an Information Security Management System (ISMS) to ensure the protection of healthcare data 
and compliance with the HIPAA Security Rule.  
Data within TriNetX were mapped to standardized clinical terminologies (e.g., SNOMED-CT to ICD-10, 
NDC to RxNorm) and transformed into a proprietary schema following rigorous data quality assessments 9. 
All study variables were binary, categorical (which is expanded to a set of binary columns), or continuous. 
Age was guaranteed to exist. Missing sex values were represented using ‘unknown sex’. The missing data for 
ethnic grouping and ethnicity were presented as ‘unknown race’ or ‘unknown ethnicity’. For other variables, 
including medical conditions, procedures, laboratory tests, and socioeconomic determinant health, the value 
was either present or absent, so ‘missing’ is not pertinent.

All data available on the TriNetX platform was de-identified in accordance with Section §164.514(a) of the 
HIPAA Privacy Rule, and the de-identification process was validated by a qualified expert as defined in 
Section §164.514(b)(1). As the study was based solely on de-identified data, it was exempt from Institutional 
Review Board (IRB) review. Nonetheless, the use of TriNetX for this research was approved by the IRB of 
Chung Shan Medical University Hospital (approval number: CS2-21176). 
Study population 
We identified adults aged ≥45 years who had at least two diagnoses of osteoporosis (ICD-10: M80 and M81) 
between January 1, 2014, and December 31, 2023, within the TriNetX US Network. To ensure a new user 
design, we excluded individuals who had received any prescriptions for denosumab or bisphosphonates before 
December 31, 2013. Eligible participants had newly initiated either denosumab or bisphosphonate treatment 
during the study period, with an osteoporosis diagnosis recorded within one week before or after the 
prescription date. The date of the first prescription for denosumab or bisphosphonates was defined as the index 
date. A total of 360,283 participants met the inclusion criteria. 
Denosumab group 
We identified 104,340 individuals with a diagnosis of osteoporosis who subsequently initiated denosumab 
treatment. To minimize confounding, we excluded participants with advanced chronic kidney disease (defined 
as an estimated glomerular filtration rate [eGFR] <30 mL/min/1.73 m², dialysis, or diagnosis of end-stage 
renal disease (ESRD) with ICD-10 codes N18.4–N18.6), Paget's disease of bone, or a prior diagnosis of OA 
before denosumab initiation. After applying these criteria, a total of 59,157 participants were included in the 
final analysis for the denosumab group (Figure 1). 
Bisphosphonate group 
We identified 255,943 individuals with a diagnosis of osteoporosis who subsequently initiated bisphosphonate 
treatment, either as an intravenous injection or oral administration. To maintain bisphosphonate treatment 
group consistency, individuals who switched to denosumab before December 31, 2023, were excluded. We 
also applied the same exclusion criteria, including advanced chronic kidney disease, Paget’s disease of bone, 
and prior diagnosis of OA before bisphosphonate treatment initiation. After exclusions, 161,044 participants

remained in the bisphosphonate group for analysis (Figure 1). To balance exposure specificity and 
representativeness, we excluded bisphosphonate users who subsequently received denosumab to prevent 
contamination of the comparator group, while retaining denosumab initiators regardless of subsequent therapy 
under an intention-to-treat (ITT) approach. This design ensures that the observed differences primarily reflect 
outcomes associated with the initial treatment choice rather than subsequent medication changes. 
Outcome and follow-up 
The primary outcome was the new onset of OA (Supplementary Table 1), specifically affecting the knee, hip, 
or thumb carpometacarpal. Incident OA was identified using ICD-10-CM codes M15 (polyarthrosis), M16 
(hip OA), M17 (knee OA), and M18 (first carpometacarpal joint OA) 10. The study assessed the 10-year risk 
of newly diagnosed OA (ICD-10-CM: M15–M19) in the denosumab and bisphosphonate groups. For each 
participant, follow-up began at the index date, defined as the date of the first prescription of denosumab or 
bisphosphonate, and continued until the earliest occurrence of the following: a new diagnosis of OA, or the 
last recorded encounter within the 10-year observation period. The median follow-up time before PSM was 
1,297 days in the denosumab group and 1,370 days in the bisphosphonate group. After PSM, the median 
follow-up time was 1,298 days for the denosumab group and 1,252 days for the bisphosphonate group. 
Co-variates 
To control for potential confounding factors, we included a comprehensive set of covariates assessed either 
on the index date or within the six months preceding it. These covariates encompassed demographic 
characteristics (age, sex, ethnicity, race, and socioeconomic status), lifestyle-related factors such as body mass 
index (BMI), nicotine dependence (ICD-10: F17) as a proxy for smoking, and alcohol-related disorders (ICD-
10: F10) as a proxy for alcohol consumption. Healthcare encounters during the baseline period, including 
preventive care visits, hospital admissions, and emergency department presentations within the six months 
preceding the index date, were also identified. We also accounted for a broad range of comorbid conditions, 
including hypertensive diseases (ICD-10: I10-I1A), ischemic heart diseases (ICD-10: I20-I25), heart failure 
(ICD-10: I50), diabetes mellitus (ICD-10: E08-E13), dyslipidemia (E78), acute kidney failure (ICD-10: N17), 
chronic kidney disease (CKD, ICD-10: N18), diseases of liver (ICD-10: K70-K77), spondylopathies (M45-
M49), inflammatory polyarthropathies (ICD-10: M05-M14), depressive episode (F32), chronic lower

(ATC code: H02), non-steroidal anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs; ATC code: M01A), disease-modifying

Statistical analysis 
To address baseline differences between treatment groups, we performed PSM based on key covariates, 
including demographics, lifestyle factors, healthcare encounters, co-morbidities, and co-medication. A 
propensity score for each participant was estimated using logistic regression implemented through the 
validated machine learning library scikit-learn in Python. These scores, ranging from 0 to 1, represented the 
predicted probability of receiving denosumab treatment given the individual's covariate profile. 
TriNetX aggregates data from a global federated health network. Each participating healthcare organization 
contributed a covariate matrix for its patients, which was subsequently pooled for analysis. To minimize 
selection bias, we employed greedy nearest neighbour matching with a caliper of 0.1 pooled standard deviation, 
and the order of records was randomized to prevent order-dependent bias in the matching process. After PSM, 
there were 59,157 pairs of denosumab users and bisphosphonate users selected for analysis (Figure 1). The 
TriNetX Live Analytics environment does not currently support inverse probability of treatment weighting 
(IPTW) for risk estimation. Therefore, PSM was selected as the primary method for causal adjustment 
(Supplementary Figure 1). 
Time-to-event analysis was performed using Kaplan–Meier methods to estimate the cumulative incidence of 
OA over daily intervals. Censoring was applied at the last recorded clinical encounter within the defined time 
window to account for loss to follow-up. Cox proportional hazards models were used to compare the matched 
cohorts, with the proportional hazards assumption assessed via the generalized Schoenfeld residuals approach. 
Hazard ratios (HRs) and corresponding 95% confidence intervals (CIs) were estimated using the survival 
package (v3.2-3) in R, embedded within the TriNetX Analytics Platform.

We performed stratified analyses to examine the heterogeneity of OA risk in denosumab users compared with 
bisphosphonate users across different subgroups categorized by sex, race (White, Black or African American, 
and Asian), age (45-65 years versus 65 years or older), and BMI (<25, 25-30, and ≥30). To assess the 
robustness of the primary analysis, we performed several sensitivity analyses. First, to address concerns about 
differential administration reliability, we conducted separate comparisons of denosumab with injectable and 
oral bisphosphonates. Second, we included separating injectable and oral forms of denosumab and applying 
varying lag periods (3, 6, and 12 months) for the initiation of follow-up. All statistical analyses were two-
sided, with significance defined as P < 0.05. 
Results 
Characteristics of study cohorts 
After PSM, 59,157 denosumab users were successfully matched 1:1 with 59,157 bisphosphonate users (Figure 
1). Table 1 describes the baseline characteristics of participants before and after PSM. Before PSM, those who 
used denosumab were older (69.6 vs 67.5 years), less likely to be Hispanic or Latino ethnic group (3.2% vs 
5.7%), and less likely to be Black or African American (3.2% vs 6.2%), compared with bisphosphonate users. 
Denosumab users also had lower eGFR, lower rates of using preventive medicine services, lower prevalence 
of baseline hypertensive diseases and dyslipidemia, but had a higher prevalence of neoplasms. In contrast, the 
two groups were similar in sex, BMI, socioeconomic status, lifestyle, co-medication like NSAIDs use, opioids 
use, and corticosteroids use, and most comorbidities like diabetes, ischemic heart disease, and gout. In the 
matched populations, baseline characteristics of the two groups were all comparable, with the standardized 
mean difference of less than 0.1 (Table 1).   
Main analysis 
In the matched cohorts, the 10-year incidence of any OA was 50.8% (15,331 events) in denosumab users and 
51.4% (15,569 events) in bisphosphonate users. The 10-year incidence probability of knee, hip and thumb 
carpometacarpal OA in denosumab users were 19.2% (95% CI, 18.3%-20.2%; 4,839 events), 14.5% (95% CI, 
13.7%-15.3%; 3,396 events), and 5.6% (95% CI, 5.1%-6.1%; 1,241 events) while in bisphosphonate users 
were 21.4% (95% CI, 20.5%-22.4%; 5,389 events), 14.4% (95% CI, 13.6%-15.3%; 3,388 events), and 6.0% 
(95% CI, 5.5%-6.6%; 1,297 events). Compared with bisphosphonate, denosumab use was associated with a

reduced risk of any OA (HR 0.96, 95% CI 0.94 to 0.98) and knee OA (HR 0.87, 95% CI 0.84 to 0.91). Use 
of denosumab was associated with a lower cumulative incidence probability of any OA and knee OA (Figure 
2). No significant associations were observed between denosumab use and the incidence of hip or thumb 
carpometacarpal OA subtypes (Table 2, Supplementary Table 2-5). 
Subgroup analysis 
To examine whether individuals with certain characteristics might benefit more from denosumab than from 
bisphosphonate, we performed subgroup analysis stratified by sex, race (White, Black or African American, 
and Asian), age (45-65 years versus 65 years or older), and BMI (<25, 25-30, and ≥30). In participants with 
an Asian ethnic background, denosumab use was significantly associated with reduced incidences of any OA 
(HR 0.77, 95% CI 0.70 to 0.84, P for interaction < 0.0001), and knee OA (HR 0.73, 95% CI 0.63 to 0.84, P 
for interaction = 0.005), while in participants without an Asian ethic background, a significant association was 
observed only between denosumab use and knee OA in Caucasian (HR 0.93, 95% CI 0.89 to 0.97). 
Denosumab use was significantly associated with reduced incidences of knee OA in females (HR 0.87, 95% 
CI, 0.83 to 0.91) but not in males (HR 1.04, 95% CI, 0.88 to 1.23). Similarly, significant associations between 
denosumab use and incident OA were only observed in participants over 65 years old (HR 0.95, 95% CI 0.92 
to 0.97 for any OA; HR 0.88, 95% CI 0.84 to 0.91 for knee OA) but not in those 45-65 years old. No significant 
interactions were observed between BMI and denosumab use in the risk of OA (Figure 3).   
Sensitivity analysis 
In the sensitivity analyses, the lower risks of any OA and knee OA associated with denosumab use remained 
generally consistent when splitting injectable and oral bisphosphonate products (Supplementary Table 6). A 
significantly lower risk of knee OA associated with denosumab use remained consistent across varying lag 
times for the start of follow-up. However, no statistically significant associations were found between 
denosumab use and any OA diagnosis when considering various lag times for the start of follow-up 
(Supplementary Table 7). Sensitivity analysis by excluding patients who initiated denosumab and 
subsequently received bisphosphonates also exerted similar findings, supporting the robustness of primary 
results (Supplementary Table 8).

Discussion 
In this propensity score-matched real-world study with a 10-year follow-up period, among participants 
diagnosed with osteoporosis, denosumab use was associated with a 13%-27% reduced risk of incident any 
OA and knee OA, compared with those using bisphosphonates. Older (≥65 years) female Asian participants 
may benefit more from denosumab use than their counterparts in OA risk compared with those using 
bisphosphonate.  
Denosumab has been widely used to treat osteoporosis, but its potential for managing OA remains unclear 11. 
Previous experimental studies found that denosumab could attenuate OA by protecting cartilage and 
modulating subchondral bone remodeling 6. Recently, a phase 2 randomized placebo-controlled clinical trial 
found that denosumab had structure-modifying effects in erosive hand OA at the primary endpoint of week 
24, with even stronger effects observed at week 48, and no more adverse events compared with placebo 8. 
Another randomized placebo-controlled clinical trial revealed that denosumab could prevent early 
perioprosthetic bone loss after uncemented total hip arthroplasty 12. Our current study is the first to investigate 
the association between denosumab use and incident hip and knee OA. The active comparator bisphosphonate 
was prescribed for the same indication and patient profile, improving clinical comparability versus non-users. 
Although differences in administration reliability between denosumab and oral bisphosphonates could 
introduce adherence bias, our sensitivity analysis using only injectable bisphosphonates yielded consistent 
results, suggesting minimal impact on the findings. Moreover, differences in baseline risk are smaller and 
more measurable 13. A recent population-based cohort study found that denosumab use was associated with a 
lower risk of incident type 2 diabetes compared with bisphosphonate use in adults with osteoporosis 14. Using 
a large real-world database and a sophisticated study design, we found that denosumab use, compared with 
bisphosphonate, was significantly associated with a reduced risk of knee OA but not hip OA. The disparities 
between knee and hip OA may be due to different etiologies 15, 16. Further clinical trials are needed to verify 
the current findings. In addition, while denosumab was associated with a statistically significant 4% absolute 
risk reduction for any OA, the clinical significance of this modest effect in a large-scale study should be 
interpreted with caution.

Our study also found that people with an Asian ethnic background might benefit more from denosumab use 
than from bisphosphonate, compared with White or African American. However, this finding should be 
interpreted with caution due to the relatively small sample size of the Asian subgroup (n=4,734), which 
represented approximately 8% of the total study population. Although individuals of Asian descent generally 
had lower average bone mineral density (BMD) than those of Caucasian or African descent 17, there were no 
documented differences in the effectiveness of increasing BMD for denosumab across different racial and 
ethnic groups 18. The effect of denosumab on bone turnover also appears to be consistent across different 
ethnicities 19. On one hand, differences in diet, lifestyle, socioeconomic factors, access to healthcare, and 
prevalence of other health conditions across ethnic groups can influence the observed different effects of 
denosumab 20, 21, making it difficult to isolate ethnicity as a single factor of effectiveness. On the other hand, 
genetic variations affecting bone metabolism and cartilage health might differ across ethic groups 22, 23, 
potentially influencing an individual’s response to denosumab.  
Both OA and osteoporosis are major global health problems with a high prevalence, imposing significant 
burdens of morbidity and disability 24, 25. Moreover, up to 36% of OA patients also have osteoporosis 26. Our 
previous observational studies have shed light on a commonality: heightened bone loss occurs not only in 
osteoporosis but also in the early stages of OA 27. Meanwhile, inflammation has emerged as a shared risk 
factor for both OA and osteoporosis 28, 29. Healthcare providers must consider the overlap, especially in older 
adults and females. Thus, combined screening and management strategies can be implemented. Preclinical 
studies in animal models of OA have demonstrated that bisphosphate therapy attenuates subchondral bone 
turnover and bone loss while preserving cartilage volume 6. The current study provides population-level and 
real-world evidence that denosumab use for osteoporosis in adults may simultaneously reduce the risk of knee 
OA, particularly in females, older adults, and individuals of Asian ethnicity. These findings have important 
implications for tailoring individualized drug management of knee OA.    
 
This study also has limitations. First, the predominance of non-Hispanic White participants with relatively 
higher socioeconomic status and healthcare access may limit generalizability, and the intensive medical 
surveillance inherent to an osteoporosis cohort may have increased OA detection compared with the general 
Journal Pre-proof
 
12 
population, in whom OA is often underdiagnosed. Second, although we used a new user, active comparator 
cohort, and a PSM strategy to minimize confounding by indication, residual confounding bias (e.g, causes of 
osteoporosis and indication bias) remains possible in this observational study. Third, although continuous 
users of bisphosphonates represent the most widely used drug pattern, other drug sequences (romosozumab, 
teriparatide, selective estrogen receptor modulator) can also be used in specific clinical scenarios, and thus 
could be examined as active comparators in future studies. Fourth, because longitudinal weight data were 
inconsistently captured, we could not assess weight change over time, leaving a potential residual confounder. 
Last, due to the nature of observational study design, the causality of the results should be interpreted with 
caution. As no randomized clinical trials have been performed to examine the preventive effect of denosumab 
on incident knee or hip OA, this study can be viewed as hypothesis-generating and an incentive for future 
randomized controlled trials to be performed. 
Conclusion 
In this real-world study, denosumab use was associated with a reduced risk of incident knee OA compared 
with bisphosphonates in adults with osteoporosis, with the association being more pronounced among older 
adults, females, and individuals of Asian ethnicity. This study suggests that denosumab could attenuate the 
development of knee OA, warranting further clinical trials.
