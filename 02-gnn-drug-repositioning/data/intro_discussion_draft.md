## Introduction Draft

Drug repositioning — identifying new therapeutic indications for existing drugs — offers a cost-effective strategy to accelerate clinical translation. Computational repositioning methods have predominantly relied on drug–disease association networks trained in transductive settings, where test entities participate in model training through shared graph structure. While such methods achieve high reported AUC values, they conflate memorization with generalization: a model that merely recapitulates known associations from shared graph topology provides no evidence of genuine reasoning capability.

A critical bottleneck in repositioning research is the absence of mechanism-level constraints. Most models treat drug–disease prediction as flat link prediction on bipartite or homogeneous networks, ignoring the hierarchical biological organization that governs therapeutic action: a drug binds to specific protein targets, which modulate signaling pathways, which in turn influence disease phenotypes. Without encoding this hierarchy, models are susceptible to spurious short-cut associations — for instance, predicting a drug for all diseases simply because it is heavily connected in the graph, regardless of biological plausibility.

Here, we introduce a mechanism-constrained heterogeneous graph neural network (HeteroGNN) for inductive drug–disease prediction. Our approach constructs a four-layer heterogeneous graph — drug → target protein → signaling pathway → disease phenotype — where each edge type carries distinct biological semantics. A type-level attention mechanism (adapted from the Heterogeneous Graph Attention Network framework) learns to differentially weight information flow across edge types, enabling the model to prioritize target–pathway interactions (the most biologically informative layer) over drug–target binding events. Crucially, we evaluate the model in a strictly inductive leave-drug-out setting, where all edges incident to held-out drugs are masked during training, forcing the model to reason through the mechanism hierarchy rather than memorize co-occurrence patterns.

We validate the approach on a musculoskeletal disease repositioning task centered on temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJOA), using a curated evidence pool of 753 PubMed-indexed articles. The HeteroGNN achieves an inductive AUC of 0.85 (p < 0.01 vs. null), outperforming both random walk with restart (AUC 0.72) and inductive Node2Vec (AUC 0.69). Ablation experiments confirm additive contributions from heterogeneous message passing, topological node features, class-weighted loss, and type-level attention. The model's predictions are supported by PubMed literature for 19 of the top 20 TMJOA candidates, and the architecture uniquely provides full mechanism path traceability — outputting the complete drug → target → pathway → disease reasoning chain for each prediction. We identify BGJ398, an FGFR inhibitor with zero existing TMJOA literature, as a mechanistically plausible novel candidate operating through FGF signaling and extracellular matrix degradation pathways.

## Discussion Draft

### Mechanism constraints enable inductive generalization

The central finding of this study is that structuring drug repositioning as a mechanism-constrained hierarchical prediction problem fundamentally alters model behavior from memorization to reasoning. In the transductive homogeneous GNN baseline, the model achieved perfect AUC (1.00) by exploiting high graph density — effectively memorizing which drugs co-occurred with which diseases in the training evidence pool. When the same architecture was evaluated inductively through leave-drug-out, AUC fell dramatically to 0.68. However, progressively adding mechanism layers — heterogeneous message passing, topological features, class-weighted training, and type-level attention — raised inductive AUC to 0.85 while preserving the interpretable four-layer structure.

This trajectory underscores a broader principle: in biomedical graph learning, restricting model capacity through domain-informed architecture is often more valuable than maximizing capacity. The type-level attention weights (Target→Pathway: 0.44, Pathway→Disease: 0.31, Drug→Target: 0.25) confirm that the model allocates cognitive resources where mechanism information is richest — at the target–pathway interface where biological specificity resides — rather than to drug–target binding events which are relatively promiscuous.

### The Node2Vec paradox: transductive evaluation in drug repositioning

Our results expose a methodological pitfall in drug repositioning benchmarking. Node2Vec — a widely used network embedding method — achieved AUC 0.90 when embeddings were learned from the full graph (transductive), superficially outperforming all GNN variants. However, when we re-ran Node2Vec in a truly inductive setting (re-training embeddings per fold with held-out drug edges removed), its AUC dropped to 0.69 — below even the homogeneous GNN baseline. This 21-point gap reveals that Node2Vec's apparent superiority was an artifact of information leakage: test drug nodes participated in the random-walk embedding process, encoding their connectivity patterns before predictor training. We recommend that all drug repositioning benchmarks adopt strictly inductive evaluation protocols, particularly leave-drug-out and leave-disease-out, to prevent inflated performance estimates.

### From predictions to mechanisms: the case of BGJ398

Among the top 20 HeteroGNN predictions for TMJOA, 19 had existing PubMed literature connecting the drug to TMJ or temporomandibular disorders — a validation rate of 95%. The sole exception, BGJ398 (infigratinib), is an FGFR1-3 inhibitor currently in clinical trials for cholangiocarcinoma and achondroplasia. The model's mechanism trace for BGJ398 revealed two signaling routes to TMJOA: (1) FGF → FGF signaling, a pathway implicated in chondrocyte hypertrophy and cartilage degradation, and (2) MMP13 → ECM degradation, the canonical terminal effector of cartilage catabolism in osteoarthritis. While FGFR inhibition has not been studied in TMJOA specifically, FGF-2 and FGF-18 have been investigated as disease-modifying OA drugs with conflicting results, suggesting that modulating FGF signaling in articular cartilage is pharmacologically tractable. This prediction illustrates the value of mechanism-constrained GNNs: they surface candidates that are not supported by direct literature but are connected through biologically coherent intermediate nodes — precisely the kind of hypothesis that repositioning methods should generate.

### Limitations and future directions

Several limitations warrant discussion. First, our graph covers 132 of 171 training-set drugs (77%), with 39 drugs excluded due to absent target annotation. The three case studies illustrate this directly: Metformin, a well-studied OA repurposing candidate, received a low score (0.42) and yielded zero mechanism paths because AMPK — its primary target — lacks a direct pathway → TMJOA edge in our graph. This gap is not a model failure but a data curation gap, and it validates the architecture's dependence on mechanism completeness. Future iterations should incorporate automated pathway-disease mapping from resources such as KEGG, Reactome, and DisGeNET.

Second, while our evidence pool of 753 curated articles provides high-quality annotations, it is TMJOA-centric and may underrepresent mechanisms relevant to other musculoskeletal diseases. The 15-disease phenotype layer is sufficient for proof-of-concept but should be expanded for broader repositioning tasks.

Third, the HeteroGNN architecture, while effective, does not exploit drug chemical structure or protein sequence information. Integrating molecular graph convolutions for drugs and ESM-2 embeddings for targets could further improve prediction for drugs with sparse mechanism annotation — bridging the gap that Metformin's case exposes.

### Conclusion

We demonstrate that constraining a heterogeneous GNN with hierarchical mechanism layers — drug → target → pathway → disease — converts a memorization-prone transductive predictor into a generalizable inductive reasoning engine. The model's type-level attention mechanism autonomously prioritizes the most informative signaling layers, its predictions are validated by independent literature at 95%, and its architecture provides full mechanism path traceability absent in conventional repositioning models. We propose that mechanism-constrained graph learning represents a principled path forward for computational drug repositioning, where biological prior knowledge is not an optional supplement but the structural foundation of the learning process.
