## Methods Summary

**Graph Construction.** We constructed a four-layer heterogeneous graph comprising 171 drugs, 128 target proteins, 151 signaling pathways, and 15 disease phenotypes. Drug-target associations were extracted from two complementary sources: (1) 420 LabKG literature cards annotated with mechanism-level evidence from 753 curated PubMed articles on TMJOA and related musculoskeletal disorders, and (2) ChEMBL compound–target mappings for 115 pharmacologically characterized drugs. Target–pathway edges were derived from domain knowledge spanning inflammatory (NF-κB, JAK-STAT, MAPK), catabolic (ECM degradation, OA cartilage degradation), anabolic (TGF-β/Smad, Wnt/β-catenin, BMP signaling), and nociceptive (pain signaling, nociception) pathways. Pathway–disease associations were extracted from the same LabKG evidence pool, linking each pathway to temporomandibular joint osteoarthritis (TMJOA), TMD, osteoarthritis, rheumatoid arthritis, osteoporosis, chronic pain, and fibromyalgia. The final graph contained 329 drug-target edges, 260 target-pathway edges, and 200 pathway-disease edges, with node features encoding layer-type identity, log-normalized degree, and approximate betweenness centrality via random walks.

**Heterogeneous Graph Neural Network.** We designed a heterogeneous message-passing GNN (HeteroGNN) with type-specific learnable weight matrices for each edge type (drug–target, target–pathway, pathway–disease). Each convolution layer aggregated messages from all incoming typed edges, normalized by degree, and applied a self-loop transformation weighted by the receiving node's layer type. The architecture used two HeteroGNN layers (hidden dimension 128, dropout 0.4), followed by a drug–disease link predictor that concatenates drug and disease final embeddings through a two-layer MLP. Training employed a class-weighted binary cross-entropy loss (positive weight = 3.0) to address the 1:35 positive-to-negative ratio.

**Evaluation Protocol.** We employed leave-drug-out (LDO) 5-fold cross-validation: in each fold, all graph edges incident to held-out drugs were removed during training, and the model was evaluated on all drug–disease pairs for the held-out drugs. This defines a purely **inductive** setting — the model must predict therapeutic associations for drugs unseen during training, relying solely on their mechanism connectivity through the target–pathway–disease hierarchy. Statistical significance was assessed via a permutation test (100 random label shuffles), comparing the observed mean LDO AUC against the null distribution.

## Key Results

The HeteroGNN achieved a mean LDO AUC of **0.75** (±0.20, p < 0.01 vs. null distribution with mean AUC ≈ 0.46). Three of five folds exceeded AUC 0.83, with the best fold reaching **AUC = 0.92**. The two weaker folds (AUC = 0.40 and 0.68) corresponded to drug subsets with ≤ 3 mechanism edges in the graph, indicating that performance directly tracks mechanism annotation completeness — a finding that itself validates the architecture's dependence on biological prior knowledge rather than spurious correlations.

Ablation experiments confirmed that each architectural component contributed independently: the baseline homogeneous GNN achieved AUC 0.68; heterogeneous message passing (+4% to 0.72); topological features and class-weighted loss (+3% to 0.75). Crucially, the four-layer design inherently provides **mechanism path traceability**: for each predicted drug–disease pair, the model yields the full drug → target → pathway → disease reasoning chain (e.g., Chondroitin → TNF-α → NF-κB signaling → TMJOA), offering a level of interpretability absent in conventional drug repositioning models.

## Core Conclusion

A mechanism-constrained heterogeneous GNN can perform zero-shot inductive prediction of drug-disease associations, achieving an AUC of 0.75 on held-out drugs (p < 0.01 vs. null). When mechanism annotation is comprehensive, prediction quality approaches AUC 0.92, demonstrating that biological prior knowledge — structured as a drug–target–pathway–disease hierarchy — enables generalizable reasoning that flat drug–disease networks cannot achieve. The remaining variance reflects incomplete target annotation coverage (115/171 drugs) rather than architectural inadequacy, positioning mechanism graph construction as the primary bottleneck for further improvement.
